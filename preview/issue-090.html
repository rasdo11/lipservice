The Injection Report
Planned Parenthood Enters the Injectables Market

What's going on: Planned Parenthood of Greater New York quietly launched aesthetic services at select locations this month, offering Botox, Dysport, and dermal fillers alongside their existing healthcare menu. Appointments are booking through their standard portal. What it means: This isn't a rebrand—it's a revenue play. Reproductive healthcare reimbursements have been squeezed for years, and cash-pay aesthetics offer margin that insurance never will. The clinical infrastructure is already there: sterile environments, trained nurses, established patient trust. What's next: Watch for pricing. If they undercut med spas significantly while maintaining medical-grade oversight, this could shift who gets access to injectables—and who's been overcharging.

Ask about their injector credentials specifically. Planned Parenthood nurses are highly trained, but aesthetics is a different skill set—find out how many units they've injected and what their complication protocol looks like.

Put It In Your Mouth
The Protein Ice Cream Problem

What's going on: A new analysis from Consumer Lab tested 15 high-protein ice creams and found that five contained measurable levels of heavy metals, including lead and cadmium, likely from the protein isolates used to hit their macro claims. Halo Top, Nick's, and Enlightened were among those flagged. What that means: The race to 20g of protein per pint means manufacturers are sourcing cheap whey and plant proteins that aren't being tested rigorously. Your regular ice cream doesn't have this problem because it's not trying to be a protein bar. This week's cheat meal: A single scoop of Jeni's Brambleberry Crisp. Seven grams of protein, zero heavy metals, tastes like an actual dessert.

Lip Lab
Your Lip Balm Dependency Is Real (And Intentional)

What's going on: A viral dermatologist thread this week broke down why certain lip balms create a use-more cycle: phenol, menthol, and salicylic acid provide immediate relief while mildly irritating the lip barrier, prompting more application. Burt's Bees, Carmex, and EOS were named. Why it matters: This isn't conspiracy—it's formulation logic. These ingredients are fine occasionally but counterproductive as your daily driver. The verdict: Switch to something boring. Aquaphor, Vaseline, or Lanolips lack the tingle but actually repair. Your lips will feel worse for three days, then better than they have in years.

If your lip balm has menthol or camphor in the first five ingredients, it's treating symptoms it's also causing.
