The Injection Report
A Filler That Dissolves on Command — No Hyaluronidase Needed

What's going on: Juvéderm's parent company Allergan presented Phase II data this week at the IMCAS World Congress showing a next-generation HA filler crosslinked with a photocleavable bond that breaks down when exposed to a specific near-infrared light wavelength. Translation: your injector could dissolve it precisely, in-office, with a device — no enzyme, no guessing, no collateral damage to your own hyaluronic acid.

What it means: Current dissolution with hyaluronidase is blunt. It dissolves everything — filler and your native HA — which is why overcorrection and hollowing happen. A light-triggered system would let injectors erase only what they put in, which fundamentally changes the risk calculus of getting filler in the first place. The trial showed 94% volume reduction in treated areas within one session.

What's next: This is still pre-approval — likely 2027 at the earliest. But if your injector isn't talking about reversibility options at your next consult, bring it up yourself.

Ask your injector: "If I don't like this, what's the dissolution plan and how much of my native tissue is affected?" That question alone tells you if they're keeping up.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Olive Oil Just Embarrassed Your Supplement Shelf

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in *Nutrients* found that 40 mL of extra virgin olive oil daily for 12 weeks significantly improved skin elasticity, hydration, and transepidermal water loss scores in postmenopausal women — outperforming the collagen peptide supplement arm on two of three measures.

What that means: The oleocanthal and hydroxytyrosol in quality EVOO are anti-inflammatory heavyweights, and this study suggests the dose matters more than most people are pouring. Forty mL is roughly three tablespoons — more than a drizzle, less than a dare.

This week's cheat meal: A thick slice of sourdough absolutely drowning in high-polyphenol EVOO with flaky salt and a side of zero regret. That's the study dose in one sitting.

What to cut: If you're spending $60/month on a collagen powder and cooking with canola, the math isn't mathing.

This Week’s Cheat Meal
Thick-cut sourdough with three tablespoons of high-polyphenol EVOO, flaky Maldon salt, and absolutely no apology.
Look for EVOO with a harvest date (not just a best-by date) and a polyphenol count on the label. If neither is there, it's probably not the quality that moved the needle in this trial.

Lip Lab
That Plumping Gloss Ingredient Is Literally a Plasticizer

What's going on: The EU's Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety published updated guidance this week flagging diisostearyl malate — the slip agent in at least a dozen "clean" lip glosses — as requiring further safety assessment due to its structural similarity to known plasticizers and limited oral exposure data.

Why it matters: Diisostearyl malate is what gives high-shine glosses that non-sticky, cushiony feel. It's in products from Rare Beauty, Tower 28, and Kosas. The EU isn't banning it — yet — but the fact that oral exposure data is "insufficient" for an ingredient you literally lick off your lips all day is worth knowing.

The verdict: Don't panic-toss anything. Do start noticing whether your favorite gloss lists it in the first five ingredients versus the last five — concentration matters, and so does how often you reapply.

Check your top three glosses for diisostearyl malate. First five ingredients = high concentration. The EU wants more data before it decides — you should want that too.
