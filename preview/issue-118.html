The Injection Report
The Ozempic Face Fix Isn't Hyaluronic Acid Anymore

What's going on: A multicenter study published this week in *Dermatologic Surgery* found that calcium hydroxylapatite (CaHA) — the biostimulatory filler in Radiesse — restored mid-face volume in GLP-1 agonist patients significantly better than HA fillers at 12 months, with higher patient satisfaction scores and fewer repeat visits. The study tracked 126 patients who'd lost substantial facial fat on semaglutide or tirzepatide.

What it means: HA fillers replace volume. CaHA stimulates your own collagen production, which matters when you've lost the fat pad scaffolding that HA sits on top of. In Ozempic face, the architecture is gone — HA without a foundation migrates. CaHA rebuilds the foundation. It's not new, but the data supporting it for this specific use case finally is.

What's next: Ask your injector if they're trained in dilute CaHA techniques. Many aren't — Radiesse requires different injection planes than HA. If they hesitate, that's your answer.

The key question for your injector: "Do you use dilute Radiesse for biostimulation, or only full-concentration for volumizing?" The dilute technique is what this study validated.

Put It In Your Mouth
The B12 Megadose Shelf Is Getting Smaller

What's going on: The EU this week finalized new upper limits on vitamin B12 in supplements, capping standalone products at 25 mcg — a fraction of the 1,000–5,000 mcg doses sold in most US wellness aisles. Several UK-based brands have already pulled high-dose SKUs. The ruling cites emerging evidence linking chronic high-dose B12 supplementation to increased acne severity and potential interference with certain lab diagnostics.

What that means: Unless you have a documented deficiency, those 5,000 mcg sublingual tabs are pharmaceutical theater. Your body excretes what it doesn't need, but "water-soluble so it's fine" ignores the skin and diagnostic disruption data that triggered this cap.

Lip Lab
Patrick Ta's New Lip Oil Has a Retinoid in It

What's going on: Patrick Ta dropped the Major Headlines Lip Elixir Oil this week ($30, Sephora), and buried in the ingredient list behind the expected roster of jojoba and rosehip is hydroxypinacolone retinoate (HPR) — a next-gen retinoid ester.

Why it matters: HPR doesn't need to convert to retinoic acid the way retinol does, so it works faster with less irritation. In a lip product, it theoretically accelerates cell turnover on the vermillion border — the area where lip lines start. Nobody's putting this in lip oils yet.

The verdict: Sheer wash of color, not sticky, wears about three hours. The HPR is low-concentration enough that it won't peel your lips, but consistent use could genuinely smooth lip texture over weeks. Best for the person who wants lip care doing actual work.

HPR in a lip oil is a first from a major brand. If you're already using a retinoid on your face and skipping your lips, this closes the gap without a prescription.
