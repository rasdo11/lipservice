The Injection Report
MRI Data Is Exposing Filler Migration — Even From 'Good' Injectors

What's going on: A study published this week in *Aesthetic Surgery Journal* used high-resolution MRI to track hyaluronic acid filler in 98 patients who'd had injections 1–7 years prior. Researchers found measurable migration in 68% of nasolabial fold injections and 41% of lip filler cases — even when performed by board-certified practitioners using standard techniques. The displacement was subclinical in most cases, meaning patients couldn't see it but the imaging could.

What it means: This isn't a scare piece about back-alley filler. These were textbook injections. The finding suggests that HA filler behaves more dynamically in tissue over time than previously modeled — gravity, muscle movement, and hydrophilic swelling all contribute. It also validates what dissolution-first injectors have been saying: you may be layering new product on top of old product you didn't know was still there.

What's next: If you're due for a touch-up, ask your injector whether they'd consider a baseline ultrasound or MRI to check for residual product before adding more. The phrase you want: "Can we image before we inject?"

Put It In Your Mouth
Lycopene Just Got a Dermatology Upgrade

What's going on: A meta-analysis out of Seoul National University, published Monday in *Nutrients*, pooled 14 RCTs and found that lycopene supplementation at ≥12 mg/day significantly reduced UV-induced erythema and improved skin elasticity markers after 12 weeks. The effect size was modest but consistent — and notably stronger than beta-carotene in head-to-head comparisons.

What that means: Cooked tomatoes, watermelon, pink grapefruit — you already eat this stuff. The study suggests the threshold for skin-relevant benefit is about 12 mg daily, which is roughly one cup of watermelon plus a half-cup of marinara. Supplementation worked too, but food sources showed better bioavailability when consumed with fat.

This week's cheat meal: A thick slice of burrata over heirloom tomatoes with good olive oil and flaky salt. It's 90°F somewhere. This is medicine now.

Lip Lab
Pat McGrath Quietly Reformulated MatteTrance — and the Ingredient List Changed a Lot

What's going on: Pat McGrath Labs rolled out reformulated MatteTrance lipsticks this week with updated packaging and a significantly revised ingredient deck. Gone: dimethicone crosspolymer and two synthetic waxes. Added: squalane, meadowfoam seed oil, and a ceramide complex.

Why it matters: The original MatteTrance was iconic but divisive — stunning pigment, genuinely drying after hour three. The new formula signals a shift toward the comfort-matte category that Rare Beauty and Merit have been dominating. The ceramide addition is interesting: it suggests PMG is targeting barrier function on the lip, not just color payoff.

The verdict: Swatched Elson 2.0 — the pigment density is unchanged, but the slip is noticeably different. Less drag on application, more cushion. If you quit MatteTrance because it turned your lips into parchment, this is your re-entry point.

The reformulated MatteTrance swapped dimethicone crosspolymer for squalane and ceramides — same pigment, dramatically less drying.
