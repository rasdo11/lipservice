The Injection Report
The FDA's New Filler Database Is Live — And Already Revealing

What's going on: The FDA updated its MAUDE (Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience) database this week with a new batch of dermal filler adverse event reports from Q1 2025. The filings include a spike in hyaluronidase-related complications — specifically, cases where dissolving filler caused more tissue damage than the original filler did. Several reports cite delayed vascular occlusion after attempted correction.

What it means: Hyaluronidase has always been positioned as the safety net — the undo button. These reports suggest it's being used reactively by undertrained injectors who panic-dissolve and create a second injury. The enzyme isn't the problem; the protocol gap is. Board-certified derms and plastic surgeons have been flagging this for years, and now the data is catching up.

What's next: Ask your injector directly: what's your vascular occlusion protocol, and how many times have you used hyaluronidase in the last year? If the answer is vague, that's your answer.

The undo button has side effects. If your injector treats hyaluronidase like a casual eraser rather than an emergency tool, find a new injector.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Flavanol Cocoa Got a Real Trial and the Skin Results Are Annoying

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in *Nutrients* tested 500mg daily cocoa flavanols against placebo over 12 weeks and found statistically significant improvements in skin elasticity and hydration — but only in participants over 40. The effect in younger skin was negligible. Researchers attributed the result to flavanol-driven improvements in dermal blood flow.

What that means: This isn't a permission slip to eat chocolate for skincare. The doses used required a standardized supplement — you'd need roughly 10 bars of commercial dark chocolate to hit 500mg of actual flavanols. But for the 40+ crowd already supplementing, the elasticity data is real and worth paying attention to.

This week's cheat meal: A wedge of St. James truffle brie with membrillo paste on sourdough. Unapologetic. Unrelated to flavanols. Perfect.

What to cut: Those collagen creamers with 2g of collagen per serving — the dose is clinically irrelevant and the sugar content is doing more harm than the peptides are doing good.

Lip Lab
Glossier Reformulated Ultralip and Took Out the Good Part

What's going on: Glossier quietly updated the Ultralip formula this month — eagle-eyed reviewers on r/glossier noticed the texture change before the brand confirmed it. The reformulation swaps meadowfoam seed oil for a lighter silicone blend and reduces the castor oil concentration.

Why it matters: The original Ultralip worked because the oil ratio gave it that balm-meets-lipstick slip without the silicone slide. Meadowfoam seed oil is occlusive and rich in long-chain fatty acids — it's what made the product feel like skincare. The new version applies thinner and wears off faster.

The verdict: If you loved the original, you'll notice. The color payoff is the same but the lip-feel is cheaper. Stock up on old-formula tubes if you can find them. The new version is fine for someone who never tried the first one.

Glossier removed meadowfoam seed oil from Ultralip. The color is the same but the feel is noticeably thinner and less conditioning.
