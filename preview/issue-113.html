The Injection Report
A Fifth Neurotoxin Enters the Chat

What's going on: Evolus announced this week that the FDA accepted its Biologics License Application for EV-M01, a liquid-ready neurotoxin that doesn't require reconstitution — making it the potential fifth option alongside Botox, Dysport, Xeomin, and Daxxify. The filing is based on Phase 3 data showing noninferiority to onabotulinumtoxinA with a median onset of 2 days.

What it means: The reconstitution piece is the real story. Every current toxin except Daxxify requires your injector to mix saline into a powder, which introduces variability — how much saline, how long it's been sitting, how vigorously they swirled. A pre-mixed formula standardizes dosing and removes one layer of human error.

What's next: PDUFA date is expected Q1 2026. If you're happy with your current toxin, nothing changes yet. If you've been chasing faster onset or more consistent results across appointments, this is the one to watch.

Ask your injector: 'Do you reconstitute fresh for each patient or batch-mix?' — it matters more than which brand they use.

Watermelon Just Got a Résumé Update

What's going on: A study published this week in *Nutrients* found that daily watermelon consumption (about 2 cups) for 4 weeks significantly increased plasma lycopene and L-citrulline levels while reducing arterial stiffness in overweight adults. The citrulline-to-arginine conversion pathway improved blood flow markers that show up in skin perfusion studies too.

What that means: Better blood flow means better delivery of every topical and oral supplement you're already taking. Lycopene is also a UV-damage mitigator — not a replacement for SPF, but a meaningful internal layer.

Lip Lab
Dior Lip Oil Got a Quiet Reformulation and Not Everyone's Happy

What's going on: Eagle-eyed beauty buyers noticed this month that Dior Addict Lip Glow Oil updated its ingredient list — swapping squalane higher in the formula and removing cherry oil, which was part of the original marketing story. Dior hasn't issued a public statement.

Why it matters: Cherry oil was the lipid that gave the original its slightly tacky, high-shine finish without feeling plasticky. Squalane is a fine emollient but it's thinner, drier on the lip, and functionally different. This is the reformulation-without-announcement pattern that keeps catching consumers off guard.

The verdict: If your current tube still has cherry oil on the label, savor it. The new version is fine — but it's a different product wearing the same name.

Always photograph the ingredient list of a product you love. Reformulations don't come with announcements.
