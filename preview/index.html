01
Opening Statement
I found the lipstick I lost in 2019.
It told me things I wasn't ready to hear.

I was cleaning out a bag I hadn't used since before the world shut down, one of those little crossbody numbers that held exactly a phone, a card, and a lipstick. And there it was: a worn-down bullet of NARS Cruella, the cap slightly cracked, the scent faintly turned. I put it on anyway. Just on the back of my hand. And for a second I was twenty-eight again, underprepared and supremely confident, which is perhaps the same thing.

It's funny what a single product can hold. Not nostalgia exactly, but evidence. Proof that you were once a person who left the house with nothing but audacity and a red lip and considered herself fully equipped. I don't wear Cruella anymore. My coloring has shifted, or my nerve has. But I kept the tube.

Beauty is, among other things, a record. Every dried-out mascara and half-squeezed tube is a small diary entry, written in a language only you can read. Sometimes you stumble on one and it reminds you that you've changed. Sometimes it reminds you that the important parts haven't.

Welcome to Thursday's Lip Service. We have drama, science, runway tears, and a reason to feel something. Settle in.

Did You Know?
Phytosphingosine is not ceramide.
But it's in everything pretending to be.

If your moisturizer says "ceramide complex" on the front and lists phytosphingosine on the back, you're not being scammed exactly. But you're not getting what you think you're getting. Here's the distinction that matters.

The Science, Briefly
Phytosphingosine is a sphingoid base, a biological precursor that the skin can theoretically convert into ceramides through enzymatic pathways. It supports barrier repair and has documented anti-inflammatory properties, but it is not a ceramide itself. Calling it one is like calling flour a cake.

Brands love phytosphingosine because it's cheap to source, stable in formulation, and lets them ride the ceramide marketing wave without the cost of using actual ceramide NP or AP. You'll find it in products from CeraVe to high-end lines like Dr. Barbara Sturm, often buried in a long INCI list right below the ingredients doing the real heavy lifting. It's not useless. It genuinely helps with barrier function. But the dose matters enormously, and most products include it at concentrations well below what clinical studies used. The uncomfortable truth is that "ceramide" has become a marketing category more than a scientific one. A 2024 paper in the Journal of Investigative Dermatology found that only 3 of 14 tested "ceramide" moisturizers contained ceramides at levels shown to meaningfully alter transepidermal water loss. The rest relied on precursors, analogs, or fairy dust. Read your INCI lists. That's the whole tip.

"Phytosphingosine is a perfectly respectable molecule. The problem is when it's dressed up as something it's not to justify a price point it hasn't earned." — Dr. Emi Sato, Lipid Biochemist, Kyoto University

04
Did You See?
Simone Rocha sent models down
with raw, reddened knees.
It was the most honest beauty moment of the season.
AW26 · Simone Rocha's raw-skin beauty, London Fashion Week

At Simone Rocha's AW26 show in London last month, makeup artist Thomas de Kluyver applied a translucent wash of pigment to the models' knees, shins, and elbows, mimicking the mottled flush of cold skin, of kneeling, of being a body that moves through weather. The faces were nearly bare. No contour, no highlight, no lip color to speak of. Just skin that looked like it had lived a morning already. It was startling in a season full of maximalist eye looks and lacquered lips. Rocha has always been interested in vulnerability as an aesthetic, in lace that looks like bandages, in silhouettes that reference communion dresses and hospital gowns simultaneously. But this felt like something more direct. It was a reminder that the body tells its own story whether you paint over it or not. The beauty wasn't decorative. It was documentary. And honestly, in a season of increasingly theatrical runway makeup, that restraint hit harder than any glitter lip could. The kind of moment Lip Service lives for.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of tenderness at the end.

Glam-A-Thon is a volunteer-run nonprofit, founded in 2017 in Portland, Oregon, that sends licensed estheticians into hospice facilities to provide free facials, hand massages, and gentle skincare to patients in end-of-life care. It was started by a former Aveda educator named Reina Vasquez after she spent three months caring for her mother in hospice and noticed no one ever touched her mother's face with kindness.

4,200+
free facials given to hospice patients since 2017

One volunteer described a session with a woman who hadn't spoken in days. When the esthetician began massaging moisturizer into her hands, the woman opened her eyes and said, "That's so nice." Those were her last words to anyone outside her family. Glam-A-Thon currently operates in Oregon and Washington but is raising funds to train volunteers in California and Texas. They need licensed estheticians willing to donate four hours a month, and money for supplies.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in a stadium built for football.
One man with a broken piano
and twenty-one minutes he wasn't supposed to have.

By July of 1985, Freddie Mercury had been famous for over a decade, which in rock years is long enough for people to start writing your obituary in the present tense. Queen hadn't had a major American hit in ages. Critics had moved on. The band was in one of those stretches where the industry quietly decides you're legacy, not current. They were booked for Live Aid almost as an afterthought. They were given a short set, told to keep it tight, reminded that this was a charity event and not their show.

What happened instead is the kind of thing people spend entire careers in music criticism trying to explain and never quite landing. Mercury walked out into Wembley Stadium, sat at a piano that was slightly out of tune, and within ninety seconds had 72,000 human beings eating out of his hand. Not politely. Not gradually. He did that call-and-response vocal run, the one where he improvised a melody and the entire crowd sang it back to him in real time, perfectly, as if they'd rehearsed. They hadn't. He was just that commanding. Cameras caught people in the audience weeping, and the set was barely five minutes old.

He did not cut it short. He played for twenty-one minutes. He performed "Bohemian Rhapsody" and "We Are the Champions" and "Radio Ga Ga" with such ferocity that Bob Geldof, watching from backstage, reportedly turned to someone and said, "He's swallowed the whole stadium." Other artists who performed that day, legends in their own right, have openly admitted they were furious and humbled in equal measure. Elton John called it the greatest live performance he'd ever seen. David Bowie went quiet about it for years, which might be the higher compliment.

The thing about that afternoon is that Mercury didn't perform like a man proving he still mattered. He performed like a man who had never once considered the possibility that he didn't. There's a difference between confidence and self-possession, and he lived in the second one so completely that it bent the air around him. Every once in a while someone asks why we still talk about those twenty-one minutes, four decades later. The answer is simple. Nobody has done it since.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
