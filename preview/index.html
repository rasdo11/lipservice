Opening Statement
I counted my serums.
There were eleven.

Last night I stood in my bathroom and lined them all up on the counter like suspects in a police procedural. Eleven serums. Two of them do the same thing. One of them expired in January and I have been putting expired fermented rice water on my face for two months like a woman with nothing left to lose. The hyaluronic acid had separated into two distinct layers, which I believe technically makes it a vinaigrette.

I did what any rational person would do: I threw away exactly zero of them and purchased a twelfth. In my defense, it was on sale, and also it promised to rebuild my lipid barrier, which is a phrase that now lives in my brain the way song lyrics used to. I cannot remember my dentist's name but I can explain ceramide ratios to a stranger at a dinner party.

This is, I think, the tension at the heart of everything we talk about here. We are simultaneously too smart for the marketing and completely, willingly seduced by it. We read the clinical studies AND add to cart at 1 a.m. We know the game. We simply love playing it.

Welcome to today's Lip Service. Pull up a chair, bring your expired serum, let's get into it.

Did You Know?
Your moisturizer contains
synthetic spider silk now.

If you thought snail mucin was the ceiling for "ingredients that sound like a dare," allow me to introduce recombinant spider silk protein, which has quietly landed in at least four prestige skincare launches this quarter and is being positioned as the next frontier in barrier repair.

The Science, Briefly
The key compound is recombinant spidroin, a bioengineered protein that mimics the structural silk proteins spiders produce naturally. When applied topically, spidroin forms a breathable, flexible micro-film over the skin that reduces transepidermal water loss by up to 34% in clinical settings. Unlike occlusive barriers such as petrolatum, this film allows gas exchange while locking moisture in, essentially giving your skin a second, better-performing stratum corneum.

Bolden Beauty's new Silk Shield Concentrate ($78) uses it as its hero ingredient, and both Tatcha and Drunk Elephant are rumored to have spidroin-based formulations in their fall pipelines. The ingredient is produced via precision fermentation, the same technology behind lab-grown dairy, which means no spiders are harmed, or even involved, really. The proteins are grown in yeast cultures. It is, for all practical purposes, beer that makes your face soft. Here is the uncomfortable part: the technology is genuinely impressive, the data is solid, and it still costs a fortune. Most spidroin-containing products are priced above $60 because fermentation-grade production is expensive to scale. So for now, synthetic spider silk is performing the same role high-end skincare always does. It works, it is fascinating, and it is available primarily to people who already have nice skin and good insurance.

"Recombinant spidroin represents a genuinely novel mechanism for barrier support. We are not just occluding the skin, we are reinforcing its architecture. The challenge now is cost and scale." — Dr. Hannah Cho, Director of Biomaterials Research, MIT Media Lab

Did You See?
Zendaya showed up in Paris
with no foundation.
Just cheekbones and audacity.
AW26 · Bare Skin at Valentino

At Wednesday's Valentino AW26 show, Zendaya sat front row wearing a deconstructed ivory suit and what her makeup artist Kay Montano confirmed on Instagram was genuinely bare skin. No base, no tint, no blur. Just moisturizer, a single coat of brown-black mascara, and a clear lip balm that Montano identified as Weleda Skin Food (the $13 one, for anyone keeping score). In a season where most front-row beauty has been maximalist, full of lacquered lids and sculpted contour, the choice felt almost confrontational in its simplicity. What makes it interesting is context. AW26 runways have been trending heavily toward visible makeup. Prada sent models out in theatrical white liner. Givenchy leaned into high-gloss burgundy everything. Zendaya sitting in that room with nothing on her face was not minimalism. It was a flex. And the online response was immediate: searches for "no-foundation makeup" spiked 370% on Google in the 12 hours following the show. Sometimes the most radical beauty statement is just your actual face. That is, as they say, very Lip Service.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of reentry.

Beauty Behind Bars, a nonprofit founded in 2017 by a former corrections officer named Alicia Soto, provides professional skincare treatments, haircuts, and cosmetics workshops to women transitioning out of incarceration. The organization started in a single halfway house in the Bronx and now operates in 14 cities across seven states.

8,200+
women served with free beauty and skincare services since 2017

One of their clients, a woman named Denise who served nine years in a state facility, told the organization's blog that the first time she sat in their pop-up salon chair, it was the first time anyone had touched her face gently in almost a decade. She cried through the entire facial. That story is not unusual for them. They hear versions of it constantly. Beauty Behind Bars is currently fundraising to expand into three new cities this year and needs both donations and licensed cosmetologists willing to volunteer.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in a stadium.
One man deciding
they would all sing.

By the summer of 1985, Freddie Mercury had already done everything a rock frontman was supposed to do. He had written anthems that entire countries knew by heart. He had worn catsuits and capes and played to arenas on four continents. He had also, in the way the music industry punishes anyone who refuses to be easily categorized, been underestimated for years. Critics called Queen overblown. Radio programmers in America had moved on. The band had not released a hit in the U.S. in some time, and the general consensus among industry people was that their best years were behind them.

Then came July 13th and a stage at Wembley Stadium. Live Aid, the charity mega-concert organized by Bob Geldof, was not designed to be a career-defining moment for anyone. It was designed to raise money for famine relief in Ethiopia. The lineup was enormous. Every major act in rock and pop was there. Queen was given a 20-minute slot, the same as everyone else, and they were told, firmly, to keep it tight.

What happened instead has been analyzed by musicians, vocal coaches, and crowd psychologists ever since, and none of them have fully explained it. Mercury walked out in a white tank top, sat at the piano, and opened with the first notes of "Bohemian Rhapsody." Within seconds he had 72,000 people in complete submission. Not just listening. Responding. He conducted them through call-and-response vocal exercises as though the entire stadium were his private rehearsal room. He stretched the set. He did not rush. He played the crowd like they were a single instrument, holding notes, dropping silences, knowing exactly when to pull back and when to let the volume swallow everything. The BBC cameramen, hardened professionals who had filmed concerts for decades, later said they had never seen anything like it. One reportedly forgot to change angles because he was watching.

The set lasted 21 minutes. When it ended, the consensus was immediate and has never been revised: it was the single greatest live performance in the history of rock music. Mercury never addressed it with much gravity afterward. In an interview later that year, he said something simple that carried the weight of a man who understood exactly what he was capable of and felt no need to explain it. "I gave them a good time," he said. "That is what I do."

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
