01
Opening Statement
I bought a foundation at the airport.
It was a cry for help.

There is something uniquely deranged about the beauty purchases you make in transit. I was at Heathrow Terminal 5 on Tuesday, forty minutes early for a flight I did not want to take, and I walked out of World Duty Free with a foundation two shades too dark and a "hydrating mist" that smells like a department store elevator. I regret nothing and also everything.

The woman at the counter told me my skin looked "tired but hopeful," which is the most British thing anyone has ever said to me. She was right. My skin was doing its best, and I was doing mine, and sometimes the best you can do is let a stranger with excellent contouring spray something cold on your face and tell you it's going to be fine.

I think we buy beauty products in airports for the same reason we cry at commercials on long-haul flights. The usual defenses are down. You're between places, between selves, and some part of your brain whispers: what if a new primer could fix all of this? It cannot. But the trying is its own kind of tenderness.

Welcome to Thursday's Lip Service. We have reported drama, real science, a runway moment that made me text three people, and an organization doing quiet, life-changing work. Settle in.

Brand Drama
Byredo Wants to Be a Skincare Brand Now, and the Fragrance Purists Are Furious
Ben Gorham's cult fragrance house filed trademarks last month for a full skincare line under the Byredo name, and the first three SKUs, a cleansing balm, a serum, and a moisturizer, were spotted in a Swedish regulatory filing dated March 4. Longtime fans are calling it a dilution play. Industry insiders say it's an inevitable move post-Puig acquisition, but the pricing (rumored north of $120 for the cleanser) suggests Byredo is betting its community will follow it anywhere.
Industry Moves
Shiseido Quietly Closes New Zealand Distribution, Affecting 200+ Retail Partners
Shiseido confirmed this week it will cease direct distribution to New Zealand by Q3 2026, consolidating through an Australian third-party distributor instead. More than 200 independent beauty retailers received notice letters last Friday. The move signals a broader contraction in APAC direct-to-retail that other Japanese conglomerates are reportedly watching closely.
Trend Alert
The "Pore Flooding" Technique Has Officially Jumped from TikTok to Sephora Signage
Sephora US rolled out new in-store signage this week organizing an entire endcap around "pore flooding," the multi-layer hydration method that originated with Korean beauty creators in late 2025. The fact that a retail giant is now merchandising around a social trend rather than a brand partnership tells you everything about where the power sits right now. Expect dupes to multiply.
03
Did You Know?
Beta-glucan is having
a very quiet takeover.

You have probably been using beta-glucan without knowing it. Derived from mushrooms, oats, or yeast cell walls, this polysaccharide has been lurking in ingredient lists for years, but a flurry of new clinical data is pushing it from supporting player to lead.

The Science, Briefly
Beta-glucan works by activating Dectin-1 receptors on the skin's Langerhans cells, which triggers a controlled immune response that strengthens the skin barrier and reduces transepidermal water loss without the irritation profile of more aggressive actives.

The mushroom-derived version, specifically from Tremella fuciformis, is showing up in new launches from Allies of Skin, Kinship, and the Korean brand Roundlab, which reformulated its bestselling toner to bump beta-glucan to the second slot on the INCI list. It's being positioned as the gentler alternative to hyaluronic acid, which, let's be honest, has been coasting on name recognition for a while. Here is the uncomfortable truth: beta-glucan's molecular weight matters enormously, and most brands do not disclose which fraction they are using. A high-molecular-weight oat beta-glucan sits on the surface and forms a film. A lower-molecular-weight mushroom extract can penetrate and modulate inflammation deeper in the epidermis. These are not the same product doing the same thing, even if the label reads identically. Ask your brand. If they cannot answer, that tells you something.

"Beta-glucan is one of the most well-tolerated bioactive polysaccharides we have, but sourcing and molecular weight are everything. Without that transparency, you're essentially buying a story, not a mechanism." — Dr. Yolanda Lenzy, Board-Certified Dermatologist, University of Connecticut Health

Did You See?
Simone Rocha sent models out
with blush on their temples.
It made me rethink my entire face.
AW26 · Temple blush at Simone Rocha, London

At the Simone Rocha AW26 show in London last month, makeup artist Thomas de Kluyver applied a wash of muted rose blush not on the cheeks but across the temples, the browbone, and the bridge of the nose, skipping the apples entirely. The effect was somewhere between a fever and a Vermeer painting. Models looked like they had been standing in cold wind for exactly the right amount of time, flushed in the places skin actually flushes when you are moved or startled or running late. What struck me was the restraint. In a season full of maximalist eye looks and heavy contour, Rocha's team went in the opposite direction: one product, placed where nobody expected it, making the whole face feel different. De Kluyver reportedly used a single cream product blended with his fingers, no brush, no sponge. It is the kind of moment that does not photograph as dramatically as a glitter lip, but in person it apparently stopped editors mid-sentence. That is the Lip Service sweet spot: beauty that changes the way you look at a face, not just at a product.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of rebuilding.

Free to Flourish is a Chicago-based nonprofit founded in 2019 that provides full cosmetology training and licensing support to survivors of human trafficking and domestic violence. The organization was started by former salon owner Maria Torres after she hired a trafficking survivor and realized how few pathways existed for women to re-enter the workforce with a portable, well-paying skill.

340+
women who have earned their cosmetology license through the program since 2019

One graduate, a woman named Rae who asked that her last name not be used, told the organization's newsletter last fall that cutting hair was the first time she had done something with her hands that felt like it belonged to her. She now works at a salon in Oak Park and is training to become an instructor herself. Free to Flourish covers tuition, supplies, exam fees, and six months of mentorship, but they are perpetually short on donated professional-grade tools, especially shears and clippers.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in the palm of one hand.
Twenty-one minutes that broke time.

By the summer of 1985, Freddie Mercury had been famous for over a decade, which in rock years is long enough for the world to think it knows you. Queen was considered past their peak. Critics had been unkind about their recent albums. The band had played Sun City in apartheid-era South Africa, a decision that earned them a boycott and a place on the United Nations blacklist. They arrived at Wembley Stadium on July 13 for Live Aid carrying more baggage than any band on the bill.

The set was supposed to be short. Bob Geldof's entire concept depended on strict time limits: every act got a window, no exceptions. Mercury walked out in front of 72,000 people and a global television audience estimated at 1.9 billion, and he simply refused to be brief. He opened with "Bohemian Rhapsody," truncated but seismic, and from the first held note you could hear the stadium lock in. Not just cheering. Responding. He conducted the crowd through call-and-response passages that had never been rehearsed, and they followed him as though they had practiced for weeks.

What happened over those 21 minutes is difficult to explain without sounding hyperbolic, so here is the plain version: every other performer that day, and the lineup included Bowie, Jagger, McCartney, U2, and The Who, was measured against Mercury's set and found smaller. Not worse. Smaller. The footage still circulates not as nostalgia but as evidence. Evidence that a single human voice, plus conviction, plus an absolute refusal to be diminished, can turn a stadium into a single living thing.

Mercury never spoke much about it afterward. He was private in the way that only deeply public people learn to be. But Brian May, his guitarist and closest collaborator, said something years later that still sits with me: "He knew exactly what he was doing. He knew he had them. And he knew it might never happen again like that. So he just gave everything, as if the giving itself was the point."

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
