Opening Statement
I reorganized my skincare fridge at 1 a.m.
It did not fix what needed fixing.

There is a particular kind of despair that only strikes when you realize you own four nearly identical vitamin C serums, all opened, all slowly oxidizing, all purchased during what I can only describe as a "feelings moment." I lined them up on the counter like suspects. Each one promised radiance. None of them could explain why I kept buying them.

I think we do this more than we admit. The ritual of acquisition as a stand-in for the ritual of care. You don't actually need a new moisturizer. You need to sit still long enough to let the one you have absorb. But sitting still is hard and Sephora is open until nine.

The truth about beauty, the one nobody puts on a product page, is that it requires a kind of patience that feels almost countercultural. Letting a retinol work for twelve weeks instead of switching after two. Trusting a sunscreen even though it doesn't make your skin "glow" on camera. Boring, undramatic consistency. The least Instagrammable virtue.

Anyway. Welcome to today's Lip Service, where we talk about all of it: the science, the spectacle, and the quiet moments in between. I'm glad you're here.

Did You Know?
Copper peptides are
having their third act.

You have probably seen GHK-Cu on at least three product labels this month without fully understanding why it keeps showing up. Copper peptides are not new, not exactly trendy, and not going away. Here is what is actually happening under your skin when you use them.

The Science, Briefly
GHK-Cu works by activating your skin's wound-healing cascade even when there is no wound. The copper tripeptide complex binds to copper ions and signals fibroblasts to produce collagen, glycosaminoglycans, and decorin, essentially tricking the skin into remodeling itself as though it were repairing damage. This is why dermatologists sometimes call it "controlled regeneration."

Brands like Niod, Dr. Pickart's Skin Biology line, and the newer Aussie label Rationale have built entire ranges around copper peptide technology, but the ingredient is now infiltrating mid-range formulas too. The Ordinary added a copper peptide serum years ago and it remains one of their most quietly repurchased products. You will also find it in post-procedure recovery kits, which tells you something about who the early believers were: plastic surgeons and wound care specialists, not beauty editors. The uncomfortable truth is that copper peptides work best on skin that is already slightly compromised, which means the people seeing the most dramatic results tend to be those recovering from aggressive treatments like microneedling or laser resurfacing. On healthy, unbothered skin, the effects are subtler and slower. This does not make them useless. It makes them honest, which in this industry is practically radical.

"Copper peptides are one of the few ingredients where the in-vivo data actually matches the marketing claims, which is rarer than consumers realize." — Dr. Shereene Idriss, Board-Certified Dermatologist, Idriss Dermatology NYC

Did You See?
Dries Van Noten took his last bow.
The models wore bare faces
and his mother's jewelry.
SS26 · Bare Skin and Borrowed Jewelry at Dries Van Noten's Final Show

At the Dries Van Noten SS26 show in Paris last weekend, the designer's final collection before full retirement featured almost no visible makeup on any model. Skin was prepped with heavy moisturizer and nothing else. No concealer, no brow gel, no lip tint. The only adornment above the neck came from pieces of vintage costume jewelry sourced from the personal collection of Van Noten's late mother, clipped to ears and woven into hair that had been deliberately left unstyled. It was the opposite of a "beauty moment" and it was the most beautiful thing on any runway this season. There is something gutting about a designer choosing visibility over perfection for his final act. Every freckle, every undereye shadow, every pore was simply there, unedited and undefended. It felt less like a fashion statement and more like a permission slip. The kind of thing we try to articulate here at Lip Service every day: that beauty is not the absence of flaws. It is the presence of intention.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of rebuilding.

Dress for Success is known for its suiting programs, but its beauty and grooming initiative, which launched in 2010, has become one of the most quietly powerful parts of the organization. Volunteer makeup artists and stylists help women preparing for job interviews learn routines that make them feel prepared, not performed.

1.3M+
women served across 150 cities in 30 countries since founding

A program coordinator in Detroit described a client who had not owned a tube of lipstick in four years. She had been living in transitional housing after leaving an abusive partner. On the morning of her interview for an administrative role at a hospital, a volunteer helped her pick out a shade, a muted berry, and taught her how to line her lips so the color would last through a long day. She got the job. She still wears the shade. They always need monetary donations, professional-grade cosmetics, and licensed cosmetologists willing to volunteer even a few hours a month.

Healthy Tears
Viola Davis walked to the podium
at the 2015 Emmys
and said what no one had said before.
The room forgot how to breathe.

Before that night, Viola Davis had already done the work that should have made history inevitable. She had carried stage productions on her shoulders, excavated grief on screen with a precision that left audiences unsettled in the best possible way, and built a career that critics called "masterful" while the industry kept handing its highest honors to someone else. She was, by every measure, overdue. But being overdue and being recognized are two different countries, and she had spent a long time living in only one of them.

The role that brought her to that podium was Annalise Keating on "How to Get Away with Murder," a character so complicated, so deliberately unlikable in her brilliance, that playing her was itself an act of defiance. Annalise was not written to be palatable. She was written to be whole. And Davis made her feel like the most real person on television, right down to the scene where she peeled off her wig and wiped away her makeup and stared at herself in the mirror with something that looked a lot like reckoning.

When her name was called, she stood, and you could feel the weight of the moment settle over the room like weather. She became the first Black woman to win the Emmy for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series. And then she opened her mouth and quoted Harriet Tubman: "In my dreams, I saw a line, and on the other side of that line were green fields, and lovely flowers, and beautiful white women with their arms stretched out to me, over that line. But I couldn't seem to get there no-how. I couldn't seem to get over that line." She paused. Then she named names. She named the Black women whose talent had been the seed of everything, who could not win what was never offered to them. She said, "You cannot win an Emmy for roles that are simply not there."

It took less than two minutes. Nobody clapped at first. There was just silence, the kind that happens when a room full of people realizes they are hearing the truth and do not quite know where to put it.

The speech that left the entire room still.
