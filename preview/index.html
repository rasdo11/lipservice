Opening Statement
I believed everything the skincare industry told me.
For twenty-three years.

There's a specific kind of grief that comes with realizing you've been doing something wrong for a very long time — not dangerous wrong, just quietly, expensively pointless. I've been layering a 0.025% retinol serum over my moisturizer every night for four years. I bought it because a beauty editor I trust called it 'the gold standard.' I renewed the subscription without thinking. It cost me, over those four years, something I don't want to calculate.

Last month a dermatologist I'd never met looked at my skin and then at my shelf and said, very gently, that the retinol I'd been using was 'not doing much of anything.' The concentration, she explained, was below the threshold at which retinol produces meaningful cellular change. It wasn't harmful. It wasn't doing what I thought it was doing. I'd been moisturizing, essentially, at a significant markup.

The thing is: I'd read the studies. Or I thought I had. I'd read the stories about the studies. Which is not the same thing. This is the gap that Lip Service exists in — between what the beauty industry says and what the research actually shows, between what we buy and what we might actually need.

LVMH Moët Hennessy Louis Vuitton has taken a minority stake in Barcelona-based dermocosmetic brand Sesderma, known for its liposome technology and strong prescription-channel presence. Trend Alert
The 'Petal Blush' — Applied to the Nose Bridge — Is Everywhere Right Now
The soap brow era isn't over exactly, but something softer is moving in alongside it. The 'petal blush' — a concentrated flush of pigment swept across the nose and inner cheeks, mimicking a genuine embarrassed flush — has taken over editorial beauty this month. It reads as effortless but requires significant precision. The Milk Makeup Cooling Water Jelly Tint in Rose is the product being credited most often.
Brand Drama
Tatcha Founder's New Brand Files Quietly, Beauty Community Notices
Vicky Tsai, who founded Tatcha and sold it to Unilever in 2019 for a reported $500M, has quietly incorporated a new beauty entity in Delaware. The filing — spotted by a brand intelligence tracker — lists Tsai as the registered agent and names no products or categories. The timing, eight months after her non-compete clause is believed to have expired, is being read as significant.

Did You Know?
Retinol has a dose
below which it does nothing.

Retinol is the most evidence-backed anti-aging ingredient in skincare. The clinical literature is unambiguous and voluminous. What the literature is equally clear about — and what the beauty industry has largely chosen not to advertise — is that efficacy is highly concentration-dependent, with the threshold for meaningful results sitting well above what most over-the-counter products actually contain.

The Science, Briefly
Peer-reviewed research identifies 0.1% retinol as the minimum effective concentration for measurable changes in collagen production and cell turnover. Products below this threshold show results indistinguishable from placebo in controlled trials.

The skincare market is flooded with products containing 0.025%, 0.05%, or vaguely labeled 'retinol complex' — formulations that market the ingredient without delivering the dose required to produce the effects consumers are paying for. This isn't illegal. It isn't even unusual. It is, arguably, the entire business model of the accessible-luxury skincare tier. The uncomfortable implication: if you want retinol to work, you need a product at or above 0.1%, properly formulated with a stabilizing system, and ideally applied to dry skin at night. Prescription tretinoin — which bypasses the conversion step retinol requires — starts at 0.025% and is more potent at that dose than any OTC retinol at 10x the concentration. The dermatologist who prescribes it costs more upfront. The math usually favors her.

"The dose makes the drug. This is true in pharmacology and it's true in cosmetics. Below the threshold, you have an elegant moisturizer. Above it, you have a treatment." — Dr. Rachel Westbrook, MD, Dermatology, UCSF Medical Center

Did You See?
Prada made the makeup
look like nothing.
It was the most makeup they'd ever worn.
SS26 · Prada Milan — Negative Space

At Prada's SS26 show in Milan, makeup artist Linda Cantello built what she called 'a face that looks like it forgot it was wearing makeup' — a technically elaborate construction of skin-matched foundation, strategically placed shadow, and lips stained rather than painted. Shot against the collection's architectural silhouettes, the effect was of skin so perfected it could only be artificial. This is the paradox at the center of contemporary beauty: the most labor-intensive looks are the ones that most convincingly pretend they aren't there. The Prada woman doesn't look like she got up early to do her makeup. She looks like she was born this way and also happens to have extraordinary pores. The technique — 'negative space contouring' is what some makeup artists are calling it — involves removing rather than adding, working with concealer to create absence rather than presence. Lip Service will be watching this one.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of making someone whole.

Facing the World is a UK-based charity that performs life-changing reconstructive surgery on children in developing countries with severe facial disfigurements — cleft palates, tumors, burns, and congenital conditions including microtia, the absence of a fully formed outer ear. The charity was founded in 2003 by a group of surgeons and anesthesiologists who began traveling to perform operations, and has since completed over 2,500 procedures.

2,500+
life-changing surgeries, all provided free of charge

A boy named Arjun was born in rural Nepal with bilateral microtia — no outer ears and significantly limited hearing. At nine years old, after being identified through a community health worker, he received reconstructive ear surgery through a Facing the World mission team. His surgeon, who volunteers annually, described the moment Arjun heard his mother's voice clearly for the first time as 'the reason I still do this work.' The charity needs funding for operating supplies, post-surgical care, and the logistics of running remote surgical missions.

Healthy Tears
She danced at the Apollo
at the Cotton Club
for seventy years
without a single photograph.

Alice Barker was born in 1913 and spent the 1930s and '40s as a chorus dancer at the most storied stages in Harlem — the Apollo Theater, the Cotton Club, the Zanzibar. She danced alongside Frank Sinatra, Gene Kelly, Ella Fitzgerald, Count Basie. She appeared on some of the first television broadcasts ever made, as one of the first Black women to perform on TV alongside a white male performer. She was, by every account of people who saw her, extraordinary.

Then the records scattered. The films went into archives. Alice grew old and eventually moved into a nursing home in Brooklyn, carrying seventy years of performance in her body and nothing on paper to prove any of it happened. She would tell the staff she had been a dancer. They believed her the way you believe anyone who is ninety-two and insistent about their past.

In 2015, a film preservationist named Frank Cullen spent three years tracking down archival footage from those Harlem stages. He found Alice in the nursing home and brought an iPad to her room. He played the footage. Alice watched herself at twenty-three, in full costume, doing the thing she had spent her life doing. It was the first time she had ever seen herself dance. She was 102 years old.

She reached out and touched the screen. "That's me," she said. "That's really me." She watched for a long time without speaking. Then she said she was glad someone had found her.

40 million people have watched this. You'll know why in 30 seconds.
