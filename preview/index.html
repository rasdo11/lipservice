01
Opening Statement
I reorganized my entire skincare fridge.
Nothing inside deserved to be there.

Last night I spent forty-five minutes pulling every serum, mist, and dubious ampoule out of the little white mini-fridge next to my bathroom sink and lining them up on the floor like suspects. Some had oxidized into a shade I can only describe as 'abandoned kombucha.' One vitamin C had turned so dark it looked like it was staging a protest. I threw out nine products. Nine. The fridge now contains one toner, one eye cream, and a single sheet mask I am saving for an emergency I have not yet defined.

There is something clarifying about admitting that most of what we accumulate in the name of skincare is not skincare at all. It is anxiety in a dropper bottle. It is the fantasy of control dressed up in frosted glass and a French sans-serif font. I do not say this from a position of moral superiority. I bought every single one of those bottles with real enthusiasm and real money.

But the edit felt good. Like cleaning a closet and realizing you own seventeen white t-shirts and love exactly two of them. Beauty should be a pleasure, not inventory management. That is the energy I am bringing to this Sunday edition.

Welcome to Lip Service. Today we have a brand shakeup that Unilever does not want you to examine too closely, a fermented ingredient your microbiome has been begging for, a Valentino runway that understood the beauty of looking slightly wrecked, and a charity doing extraordinary work for burn survivors most of the industry has forgotten. Settle in.

Brand Drama
Hourglass is gutting its concealer range and Unilever's fingerprints are everywhere
Hourglass confirmed this week it is discontinuing its Vanish Airbrush Concealer in twelve shades, framing the decision as a 'portfolio refinement to serve our core consumer.' Two former brand insiders tell a different story: Unilever's beauty division, which acquired Hourglass in 2017, has been quietly tightening SKU counts across its prestige portfolio since Q1, and Hourglass's concealer line, with its higher-cost packaging and lower sell-through in Asia, was an easy cut. Expect the shade range to shrink to eight by summer.
Industry Moves
Byredo just poached Chanel's head of color development
Byredo Beauty confirmed late Friday that it has hired Marielle Loubet, who spent seven years leading color innovation at Chanel, as its new VP of product development. The hire signals that Ben Gorham's brand is serious about expanding beyond its minimalist lip lineup into complexion and eyes. Sources say the first full color collection is targeting a Q1 2027 launch.
Trend Alert
Seoul's 'raw barrier' trend is replacing glass skin, and it looks like doing less
Korean beauty editors are calling it 'saeng-pi-bu,' which translates roughly to 'raw skin.' The idea is a visible, slightly textured barrier finish rather than the dewy, poreless look that has dominated K-beauty for a decade. Products are following: Roundlab and Illiyoon both dropped ceramide-heavy finishing balms this week designed to leave skin looking healthy but explicitly not glassy. The glass-skin girlies are in their mourning era.

Did You Know?
Lactobacillus ferment filtrate
is not yogurt for your face. Mostly.

You have probably seen 'ferment filtrate' on an ingredient list and moved on with your life. Fair. But lactobacillus ferment filtrate, the strained metabolic byproduct of bacteria you would recognize from a probiotic capsule, is one of the few microbiome-friendly actives with clinical data that actually holds up to scrutiny. And most people are using it wrong.

The Science, Briefly
When lactobacillus bacteria metabolize sugars during controlled fermentation, they produce a cocktail of peptides, organic acids, and bacteriocins. The filtrate left behind acts as a postbiotic signaling matrix that communicates with the skin's resident microflora, reinforcing barrier lipids and suppressing the inflammatory cascades that drive redness and sensitivity. It does not colonize the skin. It trains what is already there.

The issue is rinse-off formulas. A shocking number of cleansers and wash-off masks now boast lactobacillus ferment filtrate high on their ingredient lists, but microbiome researchers are clear that a sixty-second rinse gives the postbiotic fraction almost no time to interact with resident bacteria. Leave-on formulations, serums, essences, and creams are where the data actually lives. Laneige's Cream Skin Cerapeptide Toner and Biossance's new Squalane + Probiotic Gel Moisturizer both use leave-on delivery, and both launched reformulated versions within the last week that bump the filtrate concentration up. The uncomfortable truth is that the fermented-skincare boom has created a labeling incentive where brands add trace amounts of filtrate to products that wash off in under a minute, then market the result as 'microbiome-friendly.' It is the probiotic equivalent of misting yourself with green juice and calling it nutrition. If you want the real benefit, you need contact time, and you need enough of the active to matter. Read the back of the bottle, not the front.

"Postbiotics are not magic. They require sustained contact with the stratum corneum to modulate microbial communities in any meaningful way. A rinse-off product with lactobacillus filtrate is mostly a marketing exercise." — Dr. Sunita Patel, Microbiome Research Lead, Centre for Skin Sciences, University of Bradford

AW26 · Valentino's Bruise-Tender Flush

At Valentino's AW26 show in Milan, makeup artist Pat McGrath pressed diffused washes of plum and oxblood pigment along cheekbones, temples, and the inner corners of eyes, creating a look that read less like blush and more like the aftermath of a long, complicated night. The skin beneath was barely touched, just moisturizer and the faintest veil of tint, so the color sat on top of real texture, real pores, real movement. It was intimate in a way that full-coverage beauty almost never is. What made it Lip Service material is the restraint. In a season overloaded with maximalist beauty statements, Pierpaolo Piccioli chose to let the imperfection be the statement. The bruise-soft pigment bled at the edges. It was not blended to oblivion. It was placed there and left alone, the way a thumbprint stays on a fogged mirror. That kind of confidence, in an imperfect finish, in a face that looks like it has actually lived, is the most radical thing happening on a runway right now.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of reconstruction.

The Strength of a Woman Foundation, founded in 2018 by a Guatemalan-American nurse named Gabriela Morán, delivers free burn-scar skincare, silicone sheeting, and SPF to women and girls recovering from cooking-fire injuries in Guatemala's Western Highlands. More than 80% of the women they serve had never seen a dermatologist before their first visit.

4,200+
women and girls treated with free scar-care since 2018

One of their patients, a seventeen-year-old named Luisa, sustained third-degree burns across her neck and chest from an open-flame stove explosion when she was twelve. When the Foundation's mobile clinic reached her village in Huehuetenango last month, it was the first time anyone had applied medical-grade silicone sheeting to her scars. Luisa told a volunteer it was the first time the skin on her neck did not hurt in the sun. The Foundation needs donations to fund its next mobile clinic rotation in June, which will serve an estimated 600 women across five departments.
