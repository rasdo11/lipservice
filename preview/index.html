The Injection Report
The Filler Migration Study That Changes the Conversation

What's going on: A team at Utrecht University Medical Centre published MRI findings on March 28 showing hyaluronic acid filler migration in 67% of patients scanned more than 12 months post-injection, with the highest displacement found in nasolabial and lip augmentation sites. The paper, in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology, used gadolinium-enhanced imaging to distinguish filler from native tissue for the first time at scale.

What it means: This is the first study with imaging rigorous enough to quiet the "migration is a myth" crowd. The data suggests that lower-viscosity HA fillers migrate more readily, and that injection depth matters as much as product choice. Your injector's technique is now a quantifiable variable, not a vibe.

What's next: Ask your provider whether they use a cannula or needle for lip work, and which viscosity they prefer. The Utrecht team is publishing a follow-up on enzyme-based reversal efficacy this summer.

The study found that filler placed superficially in the lip vermilion migrated at nearly twice the rate of filler placed in the wet-dry border. Depth of placement is the variable to discuss at your next appointment.

Put It In Your Mouth
The Olive Oil Dose That Gastroenterologists Are Fighting About

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial out of the University of Jaén, published March 29 in Nutrients, found that 50mL of high-polyphenol extra virgin olive oil daily for eight weeks significantly reduced markers of intestinal permeability in adults with metabolic syndrome. That is roughly 3.5 tablespoons, which is more than most people put on a salad in a week.

What that means: The dose is the story. Most "Mediterranean diet" studies use 20–25mL. At 50mL you are getting a pharmacological load of oleocanthal and hydroxytyrosol, both of which are anti-inflammatory at the gut lining level. Your skin barrier starts in your intestinal wall, and this study makes that connection measurable.

This week's cheat meal: A thick slice of sourdough, good butter, flaky salt, and a shallow pour of the greenest, most peppery EVOO you can find. Functional and indecent.

What to cut: Seed-oil salad dressings masquerading as "light" options. If the first ingredient is soybean oil, it is not doing what olive oil does at any dose.

This Week's Cheat Meal
Sourdough toast with salted butter and a generous pour of high-polyphenol EVOO. Three food groups, zero regret.
Look for olive oil with a polyphenol count above 250 mg/kg listed on the label. If the bottle does not list polyphenol content, the producer probably is not testing for it.

Pat McGrath Reformulated Her Lip Fetish Balm and Nobody Announced It

What's going on: Eagle-eyed members of the r/MakeupAddiction subreddit flagged on March 28 that Pat McGrath Labs quietly updated the ingredient list on Lip Fetish Divinyl Lip Shine, swapping out the old petroleum-and-lanolin base for a blend of squalane, meadowfoam seed oil, and a ceramide complex. Why it matters: The old formula was a glossy masterpiece that tanked in the EU market because of lanolin sensitization concerns and updated cosmetic regulations. The new version reads like a treatment product wearing a party dress. Ceramides in a gloss is a real formulation flex.

The verdict: Slightly less sticky, more emollient, same mirror-like finish. If you ditched the original because it dried you out by hour three, the reformulation fixes that.
