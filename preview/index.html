Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my eyebrow.
It was the most clarifying thing that happened all week.

I was doing my brows on Saturday morning, one of those quiet ten-minute rituals I've convinced myself is about grooming but is really about stalling before I open my inbox, when I spotted it. A single silver strand, wiry and defiant, growing at a slightly unhinged angle from the tail of my left brow. My first instinct was to tweeze it immediately. My second instinct, which arrived about three seconds later, was stranger: I felt relieved.

There is a particular kind of peace that comes from encountering your own aging face in good light and not spiraling. I will not pretend I have fully arrived at that peace. But the gray eyebrow hair gave me a tiny window into it. It was so specific, so undeniably mine, that it felt less like deterioration and more like a signature.

This is the tension beauty culture lives inside, isn't it? The simultaneous desire to be surprised by yourself and to control every variable. We want the dewy glow but also the permission to look a little rough. We want the perfect product but also the confidence to skip it. The gray hair stays, for now. I'm watching it with something approaching respect.

Welcome to Monday's Lip Service. We have reported drama, nerdy science, runway beauty that made me hold my breath, and a charity doing the most unglamorous, essential work imaginable. Pull up your chair.

Brand Drama
Tower 28 Is Quietly Disappearing From Ulta Shelves, and Nobody Will Say Why
Multiple Ulta locations have begun pulling Tower 28 displays as of this weekend, with employees reporting internal memos referencing a "brand partnership restructuring." Tower 28 has not commented publicly, but their DTC site just added a splashy free-shipping banner that feels like a contingency plan. The timing is curious: Ulta's spring reset is weeks away, and losing a clean-beauty darling right before it sends a signal.
Industry Moves
Shiseido Just Poached Estée Lauder's Head of Global Innovation
Dr. Mina Park, who spent seven years leading Lauder's advanced research division, officially started at Shiseido's Osaka headquarters this week. Her mandate reportedly involves accelerating the company's biofermentation pipeline, a category Shiseido has been investing in heavily since 2024. Lauder's bench just got thinner at the worst possible time.
Trend Alert
The "Undereye Flush" Trend Is Making Concealer Brands Nervous
A wave of TikTok creators are deliberately applying blush beneath the lower lash line to mimic the look of gentle sleep deprivation, and the videos are racking up tens of millions of views. It is, essentially, the opposite of everything concealer marketing has told you for two decades. Patrick Ta's team reportedly pushed up the launch of a cream blush stick by three weeks to ride the wave.

Did You Know?
Bakuchiol got a
smarter younger sibling.

You probably already know bakuchiol as the gentle, plant-derived retinol alternative that blew up around 2021. What you may not know is that a new stabilized derivative, bakuchiol oleate, is showing up in clinical formulations this spring with significantly better skin penetration and shelf stability than the original. It is not the same ingredient, and the distinction matters.

The Science, Briefly
Bakuchiol oleate is an esterified form of bakuchiol that binds more efficiently to retinoid receptors in the skin without triggering the inflammatory cascade associated with retinoic acid. Early in-vitro studies suggest it achieves comparable collagen-stimulating activity at lower concentrations, with a longer active window on the skin surface due to its improved oxidative stability.

A handful of Korean and Japanese brands have started incorporating bakuchiol oleate into night serums launching this month, including a notable formulation from Roundlab that pairs it with centella extract. The ingredient is particularly appealing for anyone who found even original bakuchiol slightly irritating, which, despite the marketing, was more people than the clean beauty world liked to admit. Here is the uncomfortable truth: most bakuchiol products on the U.S. market still use the unmodified extract, and many at concentrations too low to do much of anything. The derivative changes the math, but only if brands formulate at effective levels rather than dusting it in for label appeal. As always, the ingredient list tells you less than the percentage, and almost nobody discloses the percentage.

"Esterification solves bakuchiol's two biggest problems: penetration depth and formulation instability. This derivative is what we wished the original was from the start." — Dr. Yuna Choi, Cosmetic Chemist, Seoul National University Skin Science Lab

Did You See?
Bottega Veneta AW26
made wet hair look like
something you'd confess to a priest.
AW26 · Wet hair, bitten lips, zero apologies

Matthieu Blazy sent models down the Milan runway on Saturday with hair so slicked and heavy with moisture it looked like they had just surfaced from somewhere private. No product shine, no gel crunch. It read as water, pure and recent, as though the show had interrupted something. Makeup was nearly invisible: bare lids, lips blotted to a just-bitten flush, skin that looked neither matte nor dewy but simply alive. The effect was startlingly intimate for a brand that usually trades in architectural cool. What made it a beauty moment rather than just a styling choice was the restraint. Lead hair stylist Guido Palau reportedly used a single lightweight leave-in on towel-dried hair and nothing else. In a season full of elaborate lacquered updos and editorial maximalism, the Bottega face felt like a dare: what if the most seductive version of you is the one nobody was supposed to see? That is the kind of beauty thesis Lip Service exists to pay attention to.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of safe harbor.

Refuge Beauty Project is a grassroots organization founded in 2022 by two estheticians in Portland, Oregon, that provides free skincare kits and guided routines to women living in domestic violence shelters. Their philosophy is simple: when everything has been taken from you, a five-minute ritual that belongs only to you can be the first step back toward autonomy.

4,200+
custom skincare kits delivered to shelter residents since 2022

One resident, a mother of three who arrived at a Portland shelter last fall with nothing but her children and a grocery bag of clothes, described the moment a volunteer handed her a small pouch containing cleanser, moisturizer, and lip balm as the first time in months she remembered she had skin that deserved care. That realization, small and physical, preceded every other step in her recovery. The organization runs entirely on donations and needs both product contributions and funding to expand to shelters in four new cities this year.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in the palm of one man's hand.
Twenty-one minutes
that still haven't ended.

By the summer of 1985, Freddie Mercury was already the kind of performer who made other performers quietly reconsider their careers. He could turn a stadium into a living room. He could make seventy thousand strangers feel like he was singing directly into the soft part of their sternum. But Live Aid, on July 13th at Wembley Stadium, was not supposed to be his moment. Queen had been written off by large portions of the British press. Their set was short. The organizers expected competence, not transcendence.

What happened instead has been studied, rewatched, and dissected for over four decades, and it still resists full explanation. Mercury walked onto that stage and simply decided that every single person in that stadium, and every single person watching on television across the globe, belonged to him for the next twenty-one minutes. He opened with "Bohemian Rhapsody," a song that should not work live and certainly should not work as an opener, and bent it into something urgent and raw. By the time he reached the call-and-response section, the crowd was not just singing along. They were obeying.

There is footage of the audience during "Radio Ga Ga" that looks almost frightening in its synchrony. Thousands upon thousands of hands clapping in perfect time, not because someone told them to, but because Mercury's voice made any other physical response impossible. He was told the set should be shorter. He did not cut a single second. He ran long, ran hot, and turned what was meant to be a charity slot into the single greatest live performance in rock history. Every artist who followed him that day knew, on some level, that the contest was already over.

He said, once, in an interview years later, something that could serve as his entire philosophy: "I won't be a rock star. I will be a legend." He was not wrong. He was not even exaggerating. The twenty-one minutes at Wembley are proof that certain moments do not belong to time. They belong to whoever was brave enough to seize them.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
