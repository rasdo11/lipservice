01
Opening Statement
I found a grey hair in my eyebrow.
It was the most honest thing that happened to me all week.

I was doing my brows Wednesday morning, one of those meditative, slightly unhinged routines where you hold a magnifying mirror six inches from your face and dare God to show you something new. He did. A single silver hair, wiry and defiant, growing at a forty-five-degree angle from everything around it like a tiny rebellion. I stared at it for longer than I should admit.

My first instinct was to pluck it. My second instinct was to photograph it, for reasons that are still unclear. My third instinct, the one I actually followed, was to brush it into place and keep going. It looked kind of electric, honestly. Like a filament.

There is a version of beauty culture that would have me panicking about this. Booking a brow lamination, Googling peptide serums for follicle pigmentation, spiraling into a Reddit thread at 1 a.m. But I think the older I get the more I realize that the most interesting faces are the ones that let something slip. A grey brow hair. A sun spot you stopped covering. The evidence that you have, in fact, been alive.

Anyway. Welcome to Friday's Lip Service. We have a brand drama that involves vanishing shades, a molecule your dermatologist may already be prescribing in pill form, a runway moment that made me feel feelings, and a charity doing work so quietly essential it should be front-page news. Settle in.

Brand Drama
Kosas just axed six Revealer Concealer shades and the explanation isn't adding up.
Six shades across the deeper end of the Revealer Concealer range vanished from the Kosas site this week, replaced by a banner promising 'refined, next-level formulas coming soon.' The brand says it's a pigment technology upgrade. A chemist who previously worked with their contract manufacturer told Lip Service the real holdup is a raw-material cost dispute with their Italian pigment supplier that has been dragging since late 2025. Convenient timing to call it innovation.
Industry Moves
Shiseido is restructuring its entire Americas prestige division. Again.
Internal memos reviewed by several trade outlets this week confirm Shiseido is collapsing its NARS and Laura Mercier marketing teams into one shared services unit across the Americas, effective May 2026. The company frames it as 'operational agility.' Multiple employees have framed it differently on LinkedIn. It is the third restructure in eighteen months for the Tokyo-based group.
Trend Alert
The 'cold-plunge face' has officially hit TikTok and it's exactly what it sounds like.
A wave of creators are filming themselves dunking their faces in ice baths mid-routine, claiming it locks in serums and depuffs in seconds. Dermatologists are weary. Dr. Angelo Landriscina posted a stitch this week noting that sudden vasoconstriction can worsen rosacea and broken capillaries, and that a cold spoon from the fridge does the same thing without the content-farm theatrics.

Did You Know?
Tranexamic acid is not new.
Your skin just wasn't invited until now.

Tranexamic acid has been a staple in operating rooms for decades, prescribed orally to stop heavy bleeding and, more quietly, to treat stubborn melasma. In the last two years it has migrated from prescription pads to serums, and the clinical data is finally catching up to the hype.

The Science, Briefly
Tranexamic acid works by inhibiting the plasminogen-to-plasmin pathway, which in skin means it interrupts the signal between UV-triggered inflammation and melanocyte activation. In plain terms, it does not bleach pigment that already exists. It stops new pigment from being dispatched in the first place.

Topical concentrations in most over-the-counter products hover between 2% and 5%, which aligns with the dosing used in a 2023 split-face trial published in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology that showed statistically significant improvement in melasma severity after twelve weeks. Brands like SkinCeuticals (Discoloration Defense), Naturium, and Good Molecules have all built serums around it, but formulation matters enormously. Tranexamic acid is water-soluble and has a relatively high molecular weight, so penetration depends heavily on pH and delivery vehicle. The uncomfortable truth is that tranexamic acid works best as part of a system, not a solo hero. Oral tranexamic acid at 250mg twice daily remains the gold standard for recalcitrant melasma in dermatology literature, and topical formulations are considered adjunctive. If a serum brand is marketing it as a replacement for professional treatment, they are overselling the science. That said, for general post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and mild sun damage, the topical evidence is genuinely promising, and unlike hydroquinone it does not carry a risk of rebound darkening or ochronosis with extended use.

"Tranexamic acid is one of the few ingredients where oral and topical data both support the mechanism. It is not a miracle, but it is a very good tool, and we are still figuring out the optimal topical delivery." — Dr. Shadi Kourosh, Director of Community Health, Department of Dermatology, Massachusetts General Hospital

AW26 · Flushed devotion at Miu Miu

Miuccia Prada sent models down the AW26 runway in Paris with skin so flushed and undone it looked devotional. Makeup artist Pat McGrath skipped foundation entirely on most faces and instead focused on blotted-red inner corners, bare lashes, and lips that appeared bitten and then abandoned. The effect was somewhere between a Caravaggio weeping saint and the girl in your seminar who just got devastating news but still showed up. It was the most alive skin on any runway this season. What made it remarkable was the restraint. No glossy lids, no sculpted cheekbones, no strategic highlight. Just redness, rawness, and the quiet assertion that an unfinished face can be the most arresting thing in a room. This is the energy Lip Service was built for.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of reaching the unreachable.

The Mama Glow Foundation, founded by doula and maternal health advocate Latham Thomas, has expanded its programming to include free skincare and body-care kits for women in rural maternity deserts, counties with no obstetric provider within 60 miles. The initiative launched in early 2025 and has grown rapidly through partnerships with rural health clinics.

4,200+
postpartum skincare kits delivered to women in counties with zero OB-GYN access

In rural Appalachian Kentucky, a first-time mother named Brianna received a Mama Glow kit through her county health nurse three days after giving birth at home, because there was no hospital with a delivery unit within driving distance. The kit included gentle cleansers, nipple balm, and a stretch mark oil alongside a printed self-care guide in both English and Spanish. It was the first time anyone had asked about her skin, not just her baby's. The foundation needs funding to expand into the Delta South, where the density of maternity deserts is growing.
