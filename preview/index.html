Opening Statement
I threw out my entire skincare shelf.
It only took one bad reaction.

Last Thursday I woke up with the kind of face that makes your partner say "are you… okay?" with a careful pause in the middle. The culprit, I'm almost certain, was a layering experiment gone wrong — a new acid toner under a peptide serum under a barrier cream that promised to fix the damage the first two products would inevitably cause. A perfect closed loop of consumerism and mild chemical burn.

I stood in my bathroom staring at seventeen bottles arranged like a tiny, expensive skyline and thought: I have a graduate degree. I have read the studies. And yet here I am, blotchy and betrayed, because I wanted to believe that more steps meant more virtue. That effort was the point.

It isn't, of course. The point is your skin, attached to your body, carrying you through your life. The point is that beauty should feel like something you're doing for yourself, not to yourself. I kept four products. The rest went into a shoebox I'll probably revisit in six weeks when I have no self-control again.

All of which is to say: welcome to today's Lip Service. We're talking about the things that actually matter — and a few things that just make for excellent gossip.

Brand Drama
Drunk Elephant quietly changed its Protini formula and Reddit noticed first
Eagle-eyed users on r/SkincareAddiction flagged an updated INCI list on the Protini Polypeptide Cream, with squalane now replacing the previous meadowfoam seed oil base. The brand confirmed the change to Allure, calling it "an optimization," which is corporate for "we made it cheaper and are hoping you won't notice." Resale of the old formula has already spiked on eBay.
Industry Moves
L'Oréal poaches Estée Lauder's head of global innovation
Dr. Meena Sharma, who spent nine years steering Lauder's clinical franchise, officially starts at L'Oréal's research division in Aulnay this month. The hire signals L'Oréal's aggressive push into prescription-adjacent skincare — a category analysts estimate will hit $14 billion by 2028. Lauder, for its part, has not yet named a replacement.
Trend Alert
The "cold girl" blush placement has gone fully mainstream — and is already dying
When Hailey Bieber posted the high-bridge-of-the-nose flush in late 2025 it felt like a secret. Now that Maybelline has a tutorial and your aunt is doing it for book club, the trend's lifecycle is approximately complete. Next up, according to three separate makeup artists we spoke to: blush is moving down and out, back toward the hollows, giving "elegant hangover."

Did You Know?
Bio-retinol sounds gentle.
The science is more complicated.

Every brand from Biossance to your local farmer's market serum company is slapping "bio-retinol" on packaging right now, usually next to a picture of a leaf and the word "gentle." But bakuchiol and its botanical cousins aren't just a milder version of tretinoin — they work through an entirely different mechanism, and the distinction matters more than anyone's marketing team would like you to know.

The Science, Briefly
Bakuchiol activates retinoid receptor-independent pathways, stimulating type I and type III collagen production through upregulation of specific genes without binding to retinoic acid receptors. This means it can deliver some overlapping results — firmer texture, improved tone — but it does not replicate retinol's full cascade of cellular turnover effects.

The 2019 British Journal of Dermatology study everyone cites showed bakuchiol and retinol performing comparably on wrinkles and pigmentation over 12 weeks — but the sample was 44 people, which is the size of a large dinner party, not a definitive verdict. More recent work out of Seoul National University in 2025 suggests the real sweet spot may be combination use: low-dose retinal with bakuchiol, which appears to buffer irritation while preserving efficacy. Brands like Herbivore, Allies of Skin, and Osea have reformulated accordingly. Here's the uncomfortable truth: "bio-retinol" is not a regulated term. It can mean bakuchiol, it can mean rosehip-derived compounds, it can mean whatever extract a formulator decided was close enough. If your skin is genuinely reactive and you're avoiding tretinoin on a dermatologist's advice, bakuchiol is a legitimate option with real data behind it. But if you're avoiding retinol because a TikTok told you it thins your skin (it doesn't, at therapeutic doses), you might be solving a problem that was never there.

"Bakuchiol is a meaningful ingredient, not a miracle one. I recommend it for patients who can't tolerate retinoids, but I'm honest that the evidence base is still young." — Dr. Shereene Idriss, Board-Certified Dermatologist, Idriss Dermatology, NYC

Did You See?
Dries Van Noten's last bow.
And a single, devastating red lip.
AW26 · The Last Dries Van Noten Red Lip

The final Dries Van Noten collection under the designer's own name — AW26, shown in Paris on Saturday — was everything you'd expect: dense with textile intelligence, uninterested in trend, deeply felt. But the beauty direction, led by Peter Philips, was the gut punch. Almost every model walked bare-faced, skin untouched, with one exception: a single model near the close, in a draped black coat, wearing a matte crimson lip so precise it looked like it had been applied by a surgeon. No liner visible. No gloss. Just pigment and nerve. It was the kind of moment that reminds you makeup can be an act of punctuation — that restraint is what gives a single gesture its power. Philips reportedly mixed two discontinued Nars shades backstage to get the color right, because of course he did. If Lip Service had a patron saint moment, it might be this: one woman, one lip, and an entire career's worth of taste distilled into sixty seconds on a runway.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of dignity.

Beauty Bus Foundation, based in Los Angeles, brings beauty and grooming services directly to people who are terminally or chronically ill and their caregivers. Founded in 2009 by Katie Willcox after her father's battle with ALS, the organization grew out of a devastatingly simple observation: when you're losing everything, being helped to feel like yourself is not vanity — it's humanity.

75,000+
beauty and grooming services delivered to homebound patients since founding

One of their volunteers described visiting a hospice patient who hadn't looked in a mirror in three months. After a haircut and a shave, he asked his wife to take his photo — the first he'd requested since his diagnosis. He died eleven days later. His wife later said that photograph is the one she keeps on her nightstand. Beauty Bus needs licensed volunteers and monthly donors to keep their mobile units running across Southern California and beyond.

Healthy Tears
Viola Davis stood
at a podium in 2015
and said what no one else
had ever been allowed to say.

Before the speech, before the Emmy, before any of it — there was the work. Viola Davis had spent decades building characters from the inside out, playing women the industry didn't know how to see: too dark, too intense, too much of everything that made white Hollywood shift in its seat. She took roles that were written as margins and made them the center. She did this for years. She did it knowing the awards might never come.

In September 2015, she won Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series for How to Get Away with Murder. She was the first Black woman to win in that category in the sixty-seven-year history of the Emmys. Sixty-seven years. That number should sit in your chest for a moment.

She walked to the stage and opened with Harriet Tubman: "In my mind, I see a line. And over that line, I see green fields and lovely flowers and beautiful white women with their arms stretched out to me over that line. But I can't seem to get there no how. I can't seem to get over that line." Then she pivoted, her voice breaking and holding at the same time, to the real point — that the only thing separating women of color from opportunity is opportunity itself. "You cannot win an Emmy for roles that are simply not there."

The room went silent. Not polite-applause silent. Actually silent — the kind of quiet that happens when a large group of powerful people realizes, together, that they have been indicted with grace. The whole thing lasted under two minutes. It is still, a decade later, the most important thing anyone has ever said at that podium. Not because it was beautiful, though it was. Because it was true, and she said it anyway, standing there in a gown, holding a trophy that should have existed for someone long before her.

The speech that left the entire room still.
