The Injection Report
The Micro-Botox Evidence Gap Is Bigger Than You Think

What's going on: A systematic review published this week in *JAMA Dermatology* evaluated every available study on intradermal micro-dosing of botulinum toxin — the technique marketed as "micro-Botox" or "meso-Botox" for pore size, oil control, and skin texture. Of 23 studies identified, only 4 met inclusion criteria, and none were large-scale RCTs. The authors called the evidence base "insufficient to support routine clinical use."

What it means: Micro-Botox targets the arrector pili muscles and eccrine glands in the dermis rather than the deeper muscles that cause wrinkles. The mechanism is plausible — that's not the issue. The issue is that most clinics are charging $400–$800 per session for an off-label technique backed by pilot studies with 20 participants. Your injector may be skilled, but she's improvising.

What's next: If you're currently getting micro-Botox and like your results, nobody's telling you to stop. But if someone pitches it as a proven treatment at your next consult, ask them to name the study. The silence will be informative.

Ask your injector: "Which study are you basing the dosing protocol on?" If the answer is experience-based, that's fine — but you should know you're paying for art, not evidence.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Omega-3s Just Got a Skin Inflammation Win

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial out of King's College London, published Monday in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*, found that 12 weeks of high-dose EPA/DHA supplementation (3g/day) significantly reduced UV-induced skin inflammation compared to placebo. The effect was dose-dependent — the 1g/day group saw no meaningful change.

What that means: You need the full 3 grams to move the needle on cutaneous inflammation. Most over-the-counter fish oil capsules deliver 300–500mg of combined EPA/DHA per pill, which means your "one-a-day" omega-3 habit is doing approximately nothing for your skin.

This week's cheat meal: The lobster roll at Luke's just came back on the summer menu with drawn butter and a brioche split-top that has no business being that good. Omega-3s, technically.

What to cut: If your fish oil supplement says "1000mg fish oil" on the front but only delivers 300mg EPA/DHA on the back label, it's filler. Swap it.

Lip Lab
Rhode's Peptide Lip Tint Got a Quiet Reformulation

What's going on: Rhode updated the Peptide Lip Tint formula this month — the brand confirmed the change to *Allure* on Tuesday after beauty Reddit flagged texture differences in the Espresso shade. The new version swaps hydrogenated polyisobutene for a lighter emollient base and adds a higher concentration of shea-derived peptide complex.

Why it matters: The original formula was polarizing because it sat heavy on textured lips and migrated into lip lines within an hour. The new emollient system should reduce that creep, and the peptide bump isn't just label dressing — shea peptides have modest evidence for barrier support in compromised skin.

The verdict: Lighter slip, slightly less opaque on first swipe but buildable. If you bounced off version one, this is worth a re-try. If you loved the original's thickness, you'll notice the difference and might not love it.

The shade Espresso in the new formula pulls warmer and sheerer than V1. If you're a one-swipe person, Toast or Mocha will give you more payoff.
