The Injection Report
Galderma's 5-Year Restylane Data Is Here — and It's Complicated

What's going on: Galderma published 5-year post-market safety and efficacy data for Restylane Lyft and Defyne in *Dermatologic Surgery* this week, covering 4,200+ patients. Retention rates were lower than the marketing suggests — roughly 60% of patients sought retreatment by month 14, not the 18-month mark most injectors quote. Late-onset nodules appeared in 2.1% of jawline placements.

What it means: This is the largest real-world longevity dataset we've gotten for HA fillers outside of Allergan's portfolio. The nodule rate for jaw and chin is notably higher than for nasolabial folds (0.4%), which supports what good injectors already practice — lower-G' products and conservative volumes in high-movement areas. If your provider is using Lyft on your jawline like it's spackle, this is your conversation starter.

What's next: Ask your injector which specific SKU they're placing and where. "Restylane" is not one product — it's a family. The right filler in the wrong plane is the whole problem.

The question to ask at your next appointment: "What's the G-prime of what you're using on my jaw, and why that one over Defyne?" If they can't answer fluently, that's your answer.

Put It In Your Mouth
The 30-Grams-of-Protein-Per-Meal Rule Is Not Doing What You Think

What's going on: A meta-analysis published this week in *The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* examined muscle protein synthesis response across 52 studies and found that total daily protein intake matters far more than per-meal distribution. The viral "30g per meal or you're wasting it" rule — currently dominating wellness TikTok and half the meal-prep industry — is an oversimplification of one 2009 study that looked at young male bodybuilders. Not you.

What that means: If you're eating 100g of protein a day for skin, hair, and connective tissue support, your body doesn't care if it comes in three tidy portions or one disgusting post-workout shake and a steak at 9 PM. Stop anxiety-eating cottage cheese at your desk.

This week's cheat meal: The lobster roll at Luke's just came back in its summer configuration — cold, mayo-dressed, on a split-top bun. 28g of protein and absolutely zero moral complexity.

Lip Lab
Pat McGrath Reformulated the Lust Gloss and We Need to Talk

What's going on: Pat McGrath Labs quietly updated the Lust: Lip Gloss formula this month — new packaging, same name, different ingredient deck. Reddit's r/MakeupAddiction caught it Wednesday: the reformulation swaps the original's lanolin base for a polybutene-heavy system and drops the hyaluronic acid micro-spheres entirely.

Why it matters: The original Lust Gloss was one of the only luxury glosses that delivered genuine short-term plumping via HA spheres that hydrated on contact. Polybutene gives you shine and slip but zero bioactive function. This is a cost-engineering move dressed as a refresh.

The verdict: If you loved the original for how it felt after an hour — that slight fullness — this isn't the same product. The new version is a perfectly fine $29 lip gloss. It is no longer a $29 lip treatment.

Check the back of the tube. If "sodium hyaluronate" isn't in the first ten ingredients, you have the reformulation. The original is still floating around at Sephora — grab it while it lasts.
