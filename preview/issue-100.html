The Injection Report
The FDA Just Made Filler Reversals Faster

What's going on: The FDA cleared Hylenex (hyaluronidase) recombinant injection under a new supplemental approval this week that expands its labeled use for reversing hyaluronic acid filler complications, including vascular occlusion events. The updated labeling includes specific dosing protocols for emergency situations — something injectors have been doing off-label for years but now have clearer regulatory backing on.

What it means: This isn't a new molecule. Hyaluronidase has been the filler eraser since forever. But formalized emergency-use labeling means more clinics will stock it, more training programs will standardize around it, and insurance pathways for complication management get less murky. If your injector doesn't keep hyaluronidase on hand, that's a red flag that just got redder.

What's next: Ask your injector point-blank whether they stock hyaluronidase and what their vascular occlusion protocol is. If they blink, leave.

The question to ask at your next appointment: 'What's your vascular occlusion protocol and do you keep hyaluronidase in-office?' If they can't answer in one breath, rebook elsewhere.

Put It In Your Mouth
Collagen Peptides Finally Got a Decent Meta-Analysis

What's going on: A new meta-analysis published this week in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology pooled 26 RCTs and found that oral collagen peptide supplementation significantly improved skin hydration and elasticity versus placebo, with effects showing up as early as four weeks. The effect size on wrinkle depth was smaller and more variable — don't throw out your tretinoin.

What that means: The collagen-in-your-coffee crowd finally has a genuinely robust dataset behind them. Hydrolyzed collagen peptides (not whole collagen, not bone broth) at 2.5–10g/day are doing something measurable at the dermal level. The wrinkle data is noisier, so manage expectations there.

Lip Lab
Dior Lip Oil's New Shade Drop Is Pretty but the Formula Hasn't Changed

What's going on: Dior expanded its Addict Lip Glow Oil line this week with six new shades, including two that shift color based on lip pH. The internet is already unhinged about Rosewood.

Why it matters: The formula is still cherry oil–based with zero actives that justify the $40 price point. The color-shift technology is fun but cosmetic — it's a pH indicator dye (Red 27), same as your middle school mood ring lip gloss. The wear is decent. The hydration is temporary.

The verdict: Beautiful packaging, satisfying applicator, genuinely flattering sheers. But if you're paying $40, you're paying for the case and the experience, not the lip care. At this price, something with ceramides or peptides should be in the tube.

pH-reactive color is Red 27 dye, not personalized science. It's a nice party trick, not a reason to upgrade from your $14 Clinique Almost Lipstick.
