The Injection Report
The Post-Ozempic Filler Correction Is Getting Its Own Protocol

What's going on: Allergan Aesthetics published new guidance this week for providers treating facial volume loss in patients on GLP-1 receptor agonists, acknowledging what injectors have been whispering about for a year — these faces don't respond to filler the way a normal aging face does. The rapid subcutaneous fat loss creates a deflation pattern that's distinct from age-related volume loss, and stacking filler into it aggressively is producing migration, puffiness, and uncanny results.

What it means: The key difference is the tissue bed. Age-related volume loss happens slowly and the remaining tissue adapts; GLP-1 deflation can outpace the skin's ability to contract, leaving a looser scaffold that doesn't hold product the same way. Allergan's guidance recommends staging — smaller amounts over multiple sessions, separated by 6–8 weeks minimum — rather than the one-and-done full-face correction patients are requesting.

What's next: If you're on semaglutide or tirzepatide and considering filler, ask your injector whether they're staging and what their per-session syringe cap is. Anyone willing to do more than three syringes in a single visit on a GLP-1 face right now is moving faster than the evidence.

The question to ask your injector: "Are you staging my correction, and how many sessions are you planning before we assess?" If the answer is one appointment, get a second opinion.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Olive Oil and Your Skin Barrier: The Study Is Finally Here

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial out of the University of Navarra, published this week in *Nutrients*, tested 50 mL of extra-virgin olive oil daily (about 3.5 tablespoons) against a control diet in 72 women over 12 weeks and measured transepidermal water loss, hydration, and elasticity. The EVOO group showed a 14% improvement in skin hydration and measurably reduced TEWL — the first time this specific dose has been validated in a controlled human trial rather than extrapolated from Mediterranean diet cohort data.

What that means: The mechanism is oleocanthal and hydroxytyrosol acting as systemic anti-inflammatories that support ceramide synthesis from the inside. Three and a half tablespoons is a lot — you're looking at 400+ calories — so this is a dietary architecture decision, not a garnish.

Lip Lab
Glossier Just Quietly Reformulated Lip Gloss and Nobody Told You

What's going on: Glossier updated the formula for its original Lip Gloss this month — the ingredient list on the site changed in the last two weeks, swapping out the old polybutene-heavy base for a blend that now leads with castor seed oil and adds jojoba esters. No press release. No announcement. Just a different INCI list.

Why it matters: The original formula's stick was its whole personality — love it or hate it, it stayed put. Polybutene is what gave it that tacky, high-shine cling. Castor oil is thinner, less adhesive, and more emollient. Early reports from the r/glossier subreddit confirm what the ingredient swap predicts: less stick, more slip, slightly less shine.

The verdict: If you loved the original for its grip, this isn't the same product. If you quit it because it felt like lip glue, it might be worth a retry. The jojoba esters are a genuine comfort upgrade.

Check your tube's batch code — if you bought before June 2025, you have the old polybutene formula. New stock has the castor-oil-forward version. They feel meaningfully different.
