The Injection Report
The Feds Just Seized $12M in Counterfeit Filler

What's going on: The DOJ announced indictments this week against a network that imported and distributed an estimated $12 million in counterfeit dermal fillers — primarily fake Juvederm and Restylane — through unlicensed med spas in at least seven states. The products, sourced from overseas manufacturers with no FDA oversight, contained inconsistent HA concentrations and, in some cases, undisclosed industrial-grade polymers.

What it means: This isn't a scare story about back-alley injections. These were marketed to — and used by — legitimate-looking practices buying at a discount. Counterfeit fillers can trigger granulomas, vascular occlusion, and chronic inflammatory responses that don't behave like normal filler complications because the material isn't what your injector thinks it is.

What's next: Ask your injector where they source product. The answer should be "directly from Allergan/Galderma" or an authorized distributor — not a third-party reseller, not a group-buy Telegram chat. If they hesitate, that's your answer.

If your provider can't show you the product box with a matching lot number before uncapping the syringe, leave. Full stop. Authorized product has traceable lot numbers and holographic seals.

Put It In Your Mouth
The Omega-3 Study That Rewrites the Playbook

What's going on: A meta-analysis published this week in *The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* pooled data from 29 RCTs (n = 16,000+) and found that EPA-dominant omega-3 supplementation reduced circulating inflammatory markers — specifically hs-CRP and IL-6 — significantly more than DHA-dominant or mixed formulations. The effect size was dose-dependent and showed up as early as eight weeks.

What that means: If you're taking a generic fish oil capsule for "inflammation" or skin health, the ratio matters more than the total mg on the label. EPA is the anti-inflammatory driver. Most bargain fish oils are DHA-heavy or 50/50 — which is fine for brain health but not what this data supports for systemic inflammation and, by extension, skin.

This week's cheat meal: The lobster roll at Luke's just came back in summer-weight form — butter, split-top bun, zero greenery. Omega-3s in the wild, if you need to justify it.

What to cut: Those gummy omega-3s with 80mg of EPA per serving. That's a rounding error, not a dose.

Dior's New Lip Oil Changes Color Based on pH — And Actually Works

What's going on: Dior dropped its reformulated Addict Lip Glow Oil this week with updated pH-reactive pigment technology that the brand claims adapts to individual lip chemistry within 60 seconds of application. It's not new science — pH-responsive dyes have been around since the '90s — but the execution here is genuinely different.

Why it matters: The old generation of color-changing lip products relied on Red 27, which just turned everyone the same shade of pink. Dior's using a proprietary blend of pH indicators across a wider spectrum, and early swatches across multiple skin tones actually show meaningful variation. The cherry oil base also delivers real emollience, not just slip.

The verdict: Sheer, buildable, tastes like nothing offensive. The "universal" shades (001 and 004) genuinely look different on different people, which is the whole point. Skip if you want opacity. Buy if you want the lip equivalent of "I woke up like this."

Shade 004 (Coral) is the sleeper — it pulls more peach on acidic lip chemistry and more rose on alkaline. It's the one that actually demonstrates the tech.
