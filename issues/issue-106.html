The Injection Report
Micro Fat Grafting Is the Emerging Answer to GLP-1 Face

What's going on: A paper published this week in *Aesthetic Surgery Journal* documented a refined micro fat grafting protocol specifically designed for patients experiencing mid-face volume loss from GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide. The technique uses nanofat processed through a closed filtration system, targeting the malar and buccal regions in a single session. Early cohort results show 70–80% graft survival at 12 months.

What it means: Filler can address Ozempic face, but it migrates, dissolves, and requires repeated sessions. Autologous fat — your own, harvested from wherever you're still losing it — integrates with existing tissue and brings its own stem-cell-rich stromal vascular fraction along for the ride. The tradeoff: it's a surgical procedure with real downtime (7–10 days of swelling), and results are operator-dependent.

What's next: Ask your injector or PS whether they're trained in nanofat processing specifically — not all fat grafting is created equal. If you're on a GLP-1 and noticing hollowing, this is worth a consult before you stack more HA filler.

The key question to ask: 'Do you process nanofat with closed filtration, and what's your graft survival rate at one year?' If they can't answer both, keep looking.

Put It In Your Mouth
A Cardiologist Said Eat Chocolate and He Meant It

What's going on: Dr. Steven Nissen at Cleveland Clinic referenced a new meta-analysis this week — pooling 21 prospective studies and over 200,000 participants — showing that high-flavanol cocoa intake (≥200 mg/day) is associated with a 10% reduction in cardiovascular events. He specifically endorsed high-cocoa dark chocolate (≥70%) as a reasonable dietary addition, not a supplement gimmick.

What that means: Cocoa flavanols improve endothelial function, reduce arterial stiffness, and lower blood pressure modestly but consistently. This isn't "chocolate is a superfood" noise — it's a cardiologist reading the aggregate data and saying the effect size is real. The catch: milk chocolate doesn't count, and most commercial dark chocolate is alkalized, which strips the flavanols.

This week's cheat meal: Two squares of Lindt 85% with a shot of espresso at 3 PM. Tastes like a personality. Zero apology required.

Lip Lab
Your Lip Gloss Might Be a PFAS Delivery System

What's going on: A study published this week in *Environmental Science & Technology Letters* tested 48 lip products sold in the U.S. and found detectable levels of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) in 56% of glosses tested — significantly higher than matte lipsticks. The highest concentrations were in long-wear and transfer-proof formulas.

Why it matters: PFAS are endocrine disruptors with a half-life measured in years, not days. Lip products are uniquely problematic because you ingest them — estimated 24 mg per day for regular gloss users. The FDA does not regulate PFAS in cosmetics; the 2024 modernization act punted on enforcement timelines.

The verdict: Until brands voluntarily disclose or third-party testing becomes standard, the move is switching to brands that have publicly committed to PFAS-free formulations — Ilia, Kosas, and RMS have all made verifiable claims. If it says "long-wear" and doesn't disclose its fluorine status, be skeptical.

You eat approximately 24 mg of lip product per day. If your gloss contains PFAS, so does your bloodstream. Check the brand's ingredient transparency page before restocking.
