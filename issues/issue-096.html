The Injection Report
Allē Breach Exposes Injectable Purchase Histories

What's going on: Allergan Aesthetics confirmed this week that its Allē loyalty platform experienced a data breach affecting an undisclosed number of members. Exposed data reportedly includes names, emails, and — critically — treatment purchase histories, meaning someone's Botox frequency and filler spend is now in the wild. The company is offering 12 months of credit monitoring, which does not, notably, un-leak your lip filler cadence.

What it means: Medical aesthetic data sits in a weird privacy gap — it's commercial loyalty data attached to medical procedures. HIPAA doesn't cover Allē because it's a rewards program, not a provider. Your injector's EMR is protected; the app you use to redeem points for $50 off Juvéderm is not.

What's next: Ask your provider whether they're recommending patients pause Allē enrollment until the forensic audit completes. If you're uncomfortable, you can request account deletion directly through the app under Settings > Privacy.

Allē is a rewards platform, not a medical record — HIPAA doesn't apply. Your treatment purchase history has fewer legal protections than your pharmacy receipts.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Omega-3s Just Outperformed Collagen Peptides

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in the *Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology* compared 12 weeks of high-dose EPA/DHA (3g/day) against 10g/day of hydrolyzed collagen peptides for skin elasticity and hydration in women 35–55. The omega-3 group showed statistically superior improvements in transepidermal water loss — the measure that actually tracks your moisture barrier — while the collagen group showed no significant difference from placebo on that metric.

What that means: This isn't anti-collagen; it's pro-fat. The omega-3 dose used was therapeutic-grade, roughly six standard fish oil capsules. Your skin barrier is a lipid structure. Feeding it lipids works.

Lip Lab
Glossier Ultralip Got a Quiet Reformulation

What's going on: Glossier's Ultralip — the balm-meets-lipstick hybrid that was the top-selling prestige lip SKU in Q1 per Circana — shipped a reformulated version this month with no announcement. Ingredient-watchers flagged swapped emollients: meadowfoam seed oil out, synthetic squalane in.

Why it matters: Meadowfoam was the cult ingredient — a long-chain fatty acid ester that gave the original its slip without the sticky. Squalane is lighter and cheaper to stabilize at scale. The reformulation likely extends shelf life and simplifies global manufacturing.

The verdict: Wear-tested both. New formula is slightly thinner, sets faster, loses the melt-in quality about two hours in. If you hoarded originals, you were right.

Check the batch code on your tube. If the first two digits are 25 and the third character is C or later, you have the new formula.
