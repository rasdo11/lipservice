Opening Statement
I have seventeen mascaras.
None of them work.

It started, as most beauty spirals do, at 11pm. I was standing over my bathroom sink, removing what had once been a very promising volumizing mascara and was now a situation on my cheekbones. The cotton pad came away gray-black. My face came away roughly the same color. I had been promised smudge-proof. I had been promised all-day wear. I had been, to put it plainly, lied to.

This is my twentieth year of buying mascara. I have tried the drugstore ones, the luxury ones, the cult ones you have to import from Japan, the ones that require a special oil-based remover that also removes your will to live. There is a theory that the perfect mascara exists and that women keep buying new ones not because they're bad but because we can't stop hoping. I subscribe to this theory. It is the only explanation for the seventeen wands currently living in my makeup drawer.

A lab in Seoul is claiming they've solved it — tubing technology so advanced that it slides off with warm water and leaves zero panda eye. I've heard this before. I'm going to believe it anyway. That's what this newsletter is: the belief that something better is coming, combined with a healthy suspicion that we've been here before.

Welcome back to Issue No. 2 of Lip Service. Let's get into it.

Did You Know?
Your nervous system has
a favorite fragrance ingredient.

Linalool is in nearly every perfume you've ever owned, almost certainly without your knowledge. It's the compound responsible for the soft, slightly floral, slightly clean quality in lavender, bergamot, rosewood, and coriander. It's also, according to a growing stack of research, something your brain responds to in ways that go well beyond 'smells nice.'

The Science, Briefly
Studies have found that linalool activates GABA-A receptors — the same receptors targeted by anti-anxiety medications — triggering a measurable reduction in stress markers without requiring inhalation deep enough to enter the bloodstream. The effect happens through olfactory signaling alone.

This is why the beauty industry's current obsession with 'calming' fragrances isn't just marketing language — there's real neurochemistry behind why certain scents feel like exhaling. Brands like Aesop, Sisley, and the entire aromatherapy wing of Aveda have built category success on linalool-forward formulations, even if they've never called it that. The uncomfortable-but-true part: synthetic linalool (used in most mainstream fragrances) and naturally-derived linalool behave almost identically in these studies. If you've been paying a premium for 'natural' fragrance specifically for the calming benefit, the science doesn't fully support the markup. What matters is the molecule, not where it was grown.

"Linalool has one of the most consistent safety and efficacy profiles of any fragrance constituent we study. The evidence for its anxiolytic properties is genuinely compelling." — Dr. Anneke Suuronen, Sensory Neuroscience, Karolinska Institute

Did You See?
Valentino put a lip
on the runway
and the internet lost it.
AW26 · Valentino Milan — The Monochrome Lip

At Milan Fashion Week's AW26 presentations, Valentino's beauty direction chose one thing and committed completely: an almost brutally saturated lip — lacquered, architectural, somewhere between oxblood and arterial red — with the rest of the face left deliberately bare. Skin dewy and unfiltered. Brows natural. Eyes entirely untouched. The effect was less 'beauty look' and more 'manifesto.' This is the tension Lip Service lives in: the idea that makeup can be both armor and surrender, that a single deliberate choice can say more than a full face. Pierpaolo Piccioli has been making this argument for years, and this season's lip is the clearest statement yet. The beauty edit will hit retail in September. The internet has already been recreating it for 48 hours.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of radical generosity.

Locks of Love is one of the most recognized hair donation organizations in the US, but Wigs for Kids has been doing quieter, arguably more specialized work since 1981 — creating custom hairpieces specifically sized for children under 18 who've lost hair due to chemotherapy, alopecia, or burns. Each wig takes 12 to 18 months to complete and is provided free of charge.

4,500+
children served annually, entirely through donations

A girl named Sofia, who was seven when she lost her hair to ALL (acute lymphoblastic leukemia), described her Wigs for Kids hairpiece as 'the thing that made me feel like myself again' during treatment. Her mother spent two years fundraising for the organization afterward. It's that kind of story — specific and quiet and enormous — that Wigs for Kids runs on. What they need most right now is human hair donations of 12 inches or longer, and financial support to cover the cost of master wigmakers.

Healthy Tears
He had 21 minutes.
The crowd had 72,000 people.
He ran every single one of them.

It was July 13, 1985, and Freddie Mercury had been told to cut it short. The Live Aid set was scheduled, staged, managed down to the minute — a global broadcast feeding 1.9 billion people across 150 countries, the largest television event in history to that point. Every artist was on the clock. Bob Geldof had begged them all to be brief.

Freddie Mercury was not brief. He walked out in a white tank top and jeans and proceeded to run Wembley Stadium — all 72,000 people in it — for 21 minutes in a way that music historians still struggle to fully account for. He did the call-and-response. He did the arms. He stalked the full length of the stage like he had built it himself. At one point he simply leaned into the microphone, made a sound, and 72,000 people made it back.

Queen's sound engineer, James Barton, said later that the monitor mix that day was almost unusable — they could barely hear themselves — and that Freddie performed the entire set essentially blind and deaf to everything except the crowd. He felt the 72,000 people like a current and he rode it.

David Bowie was there. Paul McCartney was there. U2 was there and later admitted, publicly, that their own performance that day was a disaster. Freddie Mercury finished his set, waved once, walked off, and everyone in that field understood they had just watched something that wouldn't happen again. They were right.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
