The Injection Report
The Counterfeit Filler Pipeline Just Got Its Biggest Spotlight

What's going on: U.S. Customs and Border Protection disclosed this week that it seized over 90,000 units of counterfeit dermal fillers and neuromodulators in May alone — the largest single-month haul on record. The bulk originated from unlicensed manufacturing facilities in China and South Korea, with packaging sophisticated enough to fool even experienced buyers. Several seized lots tested positive for non-medical-grade silicone and industrial polymers.

What it means: This isn't a med-spa-in-a-strip-mall problem anymore. Counterfeit product is entering legitimate-looking supply chains through gray-market distributors who undercut Allergan and Galderma pricing by 40–60%. Your injector may not even know. The tell is price: if your syringe of Juvederm Voluma costs dramatically less than the ~$800 average, someone in the chain made a substitution.

What's next: Ask your provider two questions at your next appointment: *Where do you source your product?* and *Can I see the lot number on the box before you open it?* Any injector worth seeing will answer both without flinching.

Legitimate filler always comes with a traceable lot number and holographic seal. If your provider opens the syringe before you're in the room, that's your cue to ask why.

Put It In Your Mouth
Collagen Peptides Just Failed Another Skin Trial

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in *Nutrients* followed 120 women taking 10g of hydrolyzed collagen peptides daily for 12 weeks. The result: no statistically significant improvement in skin elasticity or hydration versus placebo. Gut health markers did improve modestly, but the beauty claims — the ones on every tub at Erewhon — didn't hold.

What that means: Your body breaks collagen peptides into amino acids before absorption. Where those aminos end up is not something a supplement can direct. The glycine and proline are useful, but you get the same aminos from bone broth, chicken skin, or a pork shoulder.

This week's cheat meal: The crispy pork belly bao at any dim sum spot that takes itself seriously. Collagen-rich skin, rendered fat, pickled cucumber, zero pretense about it being a wellness food.

This Week’s Cheat Meal

Lip Lab
Dior Lip Oil Got a Quiet Reformulation and Reddit Noticed First

What's going on: Dior Addict Lip Glow Oil — the $40 lip oil that launched a thousand dupes — has been quietly reformulated. Batch codes from May 2025 show a revised INCI list that swaps cherry oil for a synthetic emollient blend and drops the pH-reactive color technology down the ingredient list. Users on r/MakeupAddiction flagged the change before any beauty editor did.

Why it matters: The original's cult status was built on that color-shift mechanic and the fruit-oil base. The new formula feels thinner on application and the color payoff skews more uniform — which some will prefer, but it's functionally a different product wearing the same name.

The verdict: If you loved the original, the reformulation is a lateral move at best. The finish is glossier but less balm-like, and the shade range now leans cooler. Stockpile old batches if you spot them, or pivot to the Rare Beauty Soft Pinch Tinted Lip Oil, which is doing what the old Dior used to.

Check the batch code on the bottom of your Dior Lip Glow Oil — codes starting with 5A are the new formula. Anything starting with 4 is the original.
