The Injection Report
The Largest Filler Complication Study Ever Just Dropped

What's going on: A JAMA Dermatology retrospective published March 30 analyzed over 14,000 dermal filler procedures across 23 clinics and found a 2.1% vascular occlusion rate, nearly triple the 0.7% figure the industry has been citing from smaller datasets. Calcium hydroxylapatite fillers showed the highest incidence. What it means: The old safety stats were drawn from manufacturer-sponsored trials with cherry-picked patient pools. This is real-world, messy-patient, high-volume data, and it suggests the conversation around informed consent has been inadequate for years. Board-certified derm or plastic surgeon offices had roughly half the complication rate of med spas. What's next: Ask your injector if they stock hyaluronidase on-site (and if it's CaHA they're using, ask what their occlusion protocol is, because hyaluronidase won't dissolve it).

The single best screening question before any filler appointment: 'What is your vascular occlusion protocol and do you keep emergency supplies in-office?' If they hesitate, leave.

Put It In Your Mouth
The Collagen Peptide Trial That Has Derms Rewriting Their Scripts

What's going on: A double-blind RCT published March 31 in Nutrients followed 120 women ages 35 to 55 taking 10g of specific collagen dipeptides (prolyl-hydroxyproline and hydroxyprolyl-glycine) daily for 12 weeks. The treatment group showed a 22% increase in skin elasticity and a measurable reduction in perioral fine lines versus placebo. What that means: This isn't the vague "collagen powder" haze. The study isolated two dipeptides and used validated cutometry, which means your smoothie collagen might do nothing if it doesn't contain these specific fragments. Look for supplements listing prolyl-hydroxyproline on the label. This week's cheat meal: A charred miso-glazed salmon rice bowl from your nearest Japanese spot. The glycine and proline in the skin are basically a collagen precursor delivery system you can eat with chopsticks.

Westman Atelier's New Lip Suede Is Doing Something Different With Squalane

What's going on: Westman Atelier quietly launched Lip Suede on March 30, a matte lip color built on a squalane-and-jojoba base rather than the typical wax matrix. Why it matters: Most matte lipsticks dehydrate because they rely on volatile silicones to set. This formula skips them entirely, using plant-derived squalane as the carrier, which means the matte finish develops as the oils absorb rather than as solvents evaporate. It's a genuinely different engineering approach. The verdict: Soft, buildable, surprisingly hydrating for a true matte. Best suited for anyone who quit mattes because of the flaking.
