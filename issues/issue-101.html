The Injection Report
Galderma Officially Comes for Ozempic Face

What's going on: Galderma presented new Phase III data this week at a European aesthetics congress showing Restylane Volyme significantly restored midface volume in patients who experienced facial fat loss from GLP-1 receptor agonist use. The study tracked 200+ patients over 18 months — the first large-scale trial designed specifically around weight-loss-drug-induced volume depletion. What it means: This isn't off-label improvisation anymore. The data showed sustained improvement at 12 months with a single treatment session, and the hyaluronic acid behaved differently in fat-depleted tissue than in age-related volume loss — slower migration, better structural hold. Your injector's approach to GLP-1 patients should be different from their standard midface protocol. What's next: FDA submission is expected Q4 2025 with a specific indication for iatrogenic facial lipoatrophy. Ask your injector whether they're adjusting depth and volume per session for patients on semaglutide — if they look confused, that's your answer.

The key finding: filler placed in fat-depleted tissue held structure 23% longer than in age-related volume loss. If you're on a GLP-1 and considering filler, this changes the math on touch-up frequency.

Put It In Your Mouth
Your Matcha Habit Has a New Clinical Alibi

What's going on: A study published this week in *Nutrients* found that daily matcha consumption (2g, roughly one proper bowl) for 8 weeks significantly increased skin elasticity and reduced transepidermal water loss in women 35–55. The mechanism isn't just antioxidants — it's the L-theanine modulating cortisol, which in turn reduces collagen-degrading MMP enzymes. What that means: Your ceremonial-grade matcha is doing double duty: stress modulation and barrier repair from the inside. The effect was dose-dependent and disappeared below 1.5g, so the dusting-of-matcha-in-your-smoothie crowd is out of luck. Lip Lab
Glossier Quietly Reformulated Ultralip and We Have Notes

What's going on: Glossier's Ultralip — the one you've already repurchased three times — got a quiet formula update this month. New batches swap hydrogenated polyisobutene for a meadowfoam seed oil base and add 2% squalane. Why it matters: The original's slip came from synthetic polymers that sat on top of lips; the new base actually penetrates. The trade-off: slightly less initial gloss, noticeably better feel at hour three. The squalane is doing real barrier work, not just marketing. The verdict: Better formula, marginally less drama on application. If you liked Ultralip for the mirror selfie moment, you'll notice the difference. If you liked it for the 2 PM reapplication skip, this is an upgrade.

Check the batch code on your current tube — if it starts with 25A or later, you have the new formula. Anything older is the original polymer-heavy version.
