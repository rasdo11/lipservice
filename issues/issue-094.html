The Injection Report
Allergan Files for a Filler That Dissolves on Purpose

What's going on: Allergan Aesthetics submitted a new FDA application this week for a biostimulatory filler using a dissolvable poly-L-lactic acid scaffold — essentially a next-gen Sculptra variant engineered for mid-face volume restoration. The filing specifically references GLP-1-related facial volume loss in its clinical rationale, making it the first injectable to formally target so-called "Ozempic face."

What it means: Current options for GLP-1 volume loss — Sculptra, Radiesse, fat transfer — all work but weren't designed for this specific pattern of buccal and periorbital fat loss. A purpose-built scaffold filler could mean fewer sessions and more predictable collagen stimulation in the zones that hollow first. The Phase II data showed measurable volume improvement at 12 weeks with a single treatment.

What's next: FDA review will take 10–12 months minimum. In the meantime, ask your injector whether they're using dilute Radiesse or Sculptra for GLP-1 volume loss — the technique matters more than the product right now.

If you're on a GLP-1 and noticing mid-face flattening, request a volumetric assessment before filler — not after. A good injector will map where you've lost structural fat versus where you've just lost water weight.

Put It In Your Mouth
Watermelon Just Got a Clinical Co-Sign

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in the *Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry* found that 300ml of watermelon juice daily for six weeks significantly increased plasma L-citrulline and improved markers of vascular elasticity in women aged 30–55. The mechanism: L-citrulline converts to L-arginine, which drives nitric oxide production — the same pathway that keeps your skin perfused and your blood pressure honest.

What that means: This isn't a "superfood" hype cycle. Citrulline supplementation has solid cardiovascular data, and getting it from whole fruit means you're also pulling lycopene and vitamin C. The skin-perfusion angle is real but secondary — the vascular benefit is the headline.

Lip Lab
That Viral Lip Oil? It's Causing Contact Dermatitis.

What's going on: The FDA's latest CFSAN adverse event report, updated Monday, flagged a spike in contact dermatitis complaints tied to flavored lip oils containing cinnamal and limonene — two fragrance allergens that the EU restricted in leave-on cosmetics last year but the US still permits at higher concentrations. At least one major brand (not yet named in the report, but the internet has theories) saw a 340% increase in complaints since reformulating in Q1.

Why it matters: Lip skin is thinner and more permeable than anywhere else on your face. Fragrance allergens that are tolerable on your arm can cause perioral dermatitis on your lips within days. The reformulation likely increased essential oil concentration for a "cleaner" scent profile — which, ironically, made it more sensitizing.

The verdict: If your lip oil smells like a dessert, flip it over. Cinnamal, cinnamaldehyde, limonene, linalool — any of these in the top half of the INCI list is a flag. Unfragranced is not boring; it's dermatologically literate.

Perioral dermatitis from lip products often gets misdiagnosed as dryness, leading people to apply more of the product that's causing it. If your lips have been "chronically dry" for more than two weeks, suspect the product first.
