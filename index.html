The Injection Report
The Filler Migration Study That's Reshaping How Injectors Think About HA

What's going on: A study published this week in *Aesthetic Surgery Journal* used MRI imaging on 50 patients who'd received hyaluronic acid filler over the past 5–20 years and found residual filler deposits in unintended tissue planes in 73% of cases — even in patients who believed their filler had fully dissolved. The most common migration sites were the infraorbital region and the nasolabial fold, where product drifted inferiorly over time under gravity and muscle movement.

What it means: HA filler doesn't just "dissolve in 6–12 months" the way we've been told. The body encapsulates small deposits in fibrotic tissue, making them invisible to the naked eye but very visible on imaging. This doesn't mean your face is in danger — most deposits were asymptomatic — but it reframes the conversation about cumulative volume. Your injector should be mapping what's already there, not just what you're adding.

What's next: Ask about ultrasound-guided assessment before your next syringe. It's not standard yet, but clinics offering it are growing fast, and this study just made the case that flying blind with filler history is no longer best practice.

Before your next appointment, ask: "Do you use ultrasound to assess existing filler before injecting?" If the answer is no, ask why not — and whether they'd refer you for imaging first.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Niacinamide Supplements Just Got a Liver Flag

What's going on: A case series published this week in *Hepatology Communications* documented elevated liver enzymes in 12 patients taking niacinamide (vitamin B3) supplements at doses above 1,500 mg/day — a dose that's become popular on TikTok for acne and "skin longevity." All cases resolved after discontinuation, but three patients had enzyme levels high enough to trigger hepatology referrals.

What that means: Topical niacinamide at 5–10% is still golden — this is about the mega-dose oral trend. The therapeutic window for supplemental B3 is narrower than the wellness internet wants you to believe, and your liver is the one footing the bill.

Lip Lab
Glossier Quietly Reformulated Ultralip and Nobody's Talking About It

What's going on: Glossier updated the Ultralip formula this month with no announcement — eagle-eyed Redditors on r/glossier caught the ingredient list change last weekend. Out: castor oil moved down the list. In: a meadowfoam-squalane blend that's now the primary emollient base.

Why it matters: The original Ultralip was beloved but polarizing — the castor oil base made it slip off within an hour for a lot of people. Meadowfoam seed oil has significantly better occlusive staying power and a drier slip, which should extend wear time without killing the balmy texture that made Ultralip a cult product.

The verdict: The new formula feels noticeably less greasy, wears about 90 minutes longer in my testing, and the shade Trench is still the perfect my-lips-but-functioning shade. If you bounced off the original, revisit.

Check your tube: if the first three ingredients list meadowfoam seed oil instead of castor oil as the lead emollient, you have the new formula.
