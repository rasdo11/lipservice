The Injection Report
Sculptra Gets the Mid-Face Nod — Just in Time for GLP-1 Season

What's going on: The FDA cleared Sculptra (poly-L-lactic acid) for mid-face volume restoration this week, expanding its indication beyond the nasolabial folds for the first time since 2004. Galderma timed the filing perfectly — derms report a 40%+ increase in volume-loss consultations tied to rapid GLP-1 weight loss over the past year.

What it means: Sculptra isn't a filler — it's a collagen stimulator that builds volume over 2–3 sessions across several months. That slow-build mechanism is actually ideal for GLP-1 face, where the deflation is diffuse rather than localized. HA fillers can overcorrect in specific spots; PLLA rebuilds the scaffolding.

What's next: If you're on a GLP-1 and noticing mid-face hollowing, ask your injector specifically about biostimulators vs. traditional fillers. The treatment timeline matters — starting Sculptra earlier means fewer vials total.

Ask your injector: 'Am I a better candidate for a biostimulator or an HA filler given my rate of volume loss?' The answer should reference your weight loss timeline, not just your anatomy.

Put It In Your Mouth
Eight Strawberries a Day Kept the Cognitive Decline Away (No, Seriously)

What's going on: A Rush University trial published this week in *Alzheimer's & Dementia* found that older adults who consumed the equivalent of one cup of strawberries daily for three years showed significantly less cognitive decline than the placebo group. The active compound — pelargonidin, an anthocyanin specific to strawberries — crossed the blood-brain barrier and reduced tau tangles in post-mortem analysis.

What that means: This isn't a smoothie-brand-funded observational study. It's a randomized, placebo-controlled, three-year trial with biomarker confirmation. Pelargonidin is also an antioxidant that shows up in skin-elasticity research, so your face and your brain are both interested.

This week's cheat meal: The strawberry-pistachio croissant at Maison Kayser. Flaky, obscene, and apparently neuroprotective if you squint hard enough.

Lip Lab
Dior Lip Oil Gets a Ceramide Backbone — and It's Not Just Marketing

What's going on: Dior quietly reformulated its Addict Lip Glow Oil this month, adding a ceramide-3 complex to the existing cherry oil base. The reformulation hit Sephora shelves this week with no fanfare — same packaging, new INCI list.

Why it matters: Ceramide-3 is the specific ceramide that maintains the lip's moisture barrier, which is thinner and more permeable than facial skin. Most lip oils are occlusive only — they sit on top. Adding ceramides means this one is actually repairing the barrier underneath the shine.

The verdict: The texture is slightly thicker than the original. Finish is glossy without the slip. If you liked the old formula, you'll like this more. If you hated the price ($40), this won't change your mind.

Check the box: if your Dior Lip Glow Oil lists 'ceramide NP' in the ingredients, you have the new formula. If not, you're using old stock.
