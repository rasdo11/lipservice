Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my eyebrow.
It was the most honest thing that happened to me all week.

I was tweezing under the kind of bathroom light that should be classified as psychological warfare when I spotted it: one single, wiry, completely silver eyebrow hair growing at an angle that suggested it had given up on conformity entirely. I did not pluck it. I stared at it. I considered naming it.

There is a specific kind of vanity spiral that hits when the change is small enough to ignore but too real to unsee. It is not the same as finding a wrinkle, which at least comes with cultural scripts about wisdom and expensive retinoids. A gray eyebrow hair is just your face quietly renegotiating terms without consulting you.

I ended up tinting my brows later that afternoon, not because I was ashamed but because the ritual itself felt like a small conversation with the person I am becoming. Beauty is sometimes about looking better. But more often it is about noticing yourself closely enough to stay in relationship with your own face.

Welcome to Monday's Lip Service. We have drama, we have science, we have a runway that looked like an ancient burial rite in the best possible way, and we have a charity doing unglamorous, essential work. Pull up a chair.

Brand Drama
Merit Beauty is killing shades and calling it minimalism
Merit is discontinuing four of its ten Flush Balm shades, including the deeper tones that took two years to develop. The brand is calling it a 'simplification of the edit,' but two Sephora buyers told me the real issue is velocity thresholds: the shades were not turning fast enough to justify their shelf footprint under new merchandising rules rolling out this spring. Convenient that 'simplification' always seems to simplify in the same direction.
Industry Moves
Puig is reportedly in late-stage talks to acquire Byredo's skincare IP separately from fragrance
According to two sources familiar with the negotiations, Puig wants to carve out Byredo's nascent skincare line and license it under a different operational umbrella from the fragrance house, which Puig already majority-owns. The move would let them scale skincare manufacturing without the margin constraints of Byredo's artisanal fragrance supply chain. If it closes, expect a flood of Byredo serums at a price point your wallet will feel.
Trend Alert
The 'raw hem' nail is coming for your clean manicure
Nail artists in Seoul and East London are intentionally leaving the free edge of gel extensions slightly uneven and matte-finished, creating what they are calling a 'raw hem' effect. Think of it as the nail equivalent of an unhemmed Margiela edge. Two of the artists behind the trend say the point is to reject the tyranny of the perfectly filed tip, which, fine, but my cuticles are already chaotic enough.

Did You Know?
Ectoin is the molecule
that survived a volcano.

Before it showed up in your barrier-repair cream, ectoin was busy keeping extremophilic bacteria alive in salt lakes, hot springs, and volcanic soil. It is a cyclic amino acid derivative that functions as a compatible solute, meaning it protects cellular structures from environmental stress without interfering with normal metabolism. Dermatologists have been paying attention for about five years. The rest of the industry just caught up.

The Science, Briefly
Ectoin forms a structured water shell around proteins and cell membranes, stabilizing them against UV radiation, thermal stress, and pollution-driven oxidative damage. Unlike traditional humectants that simply attract water, ectoin physically shields lipid bilayers from disruption. Recent in-vivo studies show it reduces UVA-induced mitochondrial DNA mutations by up to 80 percent at concentrations as low as 0.5 percent.

The German biotech company Bitop has held the primary commercial patent on ectoin production for decades, which is why you have mostly seen it in European pharmacy brands like Dado Sens and Dr. Spiller rather than on the shelves at Sephora. That is changing fast. At least three major K-beauty brands have filed formulations with ectoin as a headline active in the past six months, and Merck's personal care division is now supplying a fermentation-derived version at scale. Here is the uncomfortable part: ectoin works best in products you leave on, at concentrations most brands are not disclosing. Many formulations list it near the bottom of the INCI, which means you are getting a dusting of extremophile poetry rather than a functional dose. If the brand will not tell you the percentage, assume it is there for the story, not the science.

"Ectoin is one of the few molecules where the mechanism of action is genuinely elegant. It does not force the skin to do anything. It simply makes the environment around the cell more survivable." — Dr. Heinrich Büning, Biochemist, University of Münster

Did You See?
Rick Owens AW26
looked like a thaw.
A beautiful, unsettling thaw.
AW26 · Rick Owens glacial translucence

Every model at Rick Owens's AW26 show in Paris walked out looking like she had been frozen in a glacier for a thousand years and had just begun to defrost under studio lights. Lead makeup artist Lucia Pieroni used a translucent lavender-gray base over bare skin to create a bloodless, almost cryogenic pallor, then layered a barely-there iridescent film across the cheekbones and temples so that under the overhead lighting each face appeared to be releasing moisture, as if life were slowly returning to stone. There was no lip color. There was no brow definition. Just skin that looked ancient, alien, and somehow newborn all at once. The effect was the opposite of the dewy-skin trend that has dominated runways for three years. This was not glow. This was translucence as vulnerability, skin so pared back it felt almost medical. Owens has always treated the body as architecture, but this season the face was treated as weather: something that shifts and reveals and cannot be controlled. It is the kind of beauty moment that makes you want to throw away your highlighter, and we mean that as the highest compliment Lip Service can offer.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of shelter.

Chicago-based nonprofit Brave Space Alliance runs the only Black-led, trans-led health center on the South Side, and since 2022 its hygiene and wellness program has been providing free wound-care skincare, SPF, and barrier-repair products to unhoused women and trans femmes staying in shelters across the city. It was founded in 2017 by LaSaia Wade.

8,200+
individual skincare and wound-care kits distributed since 2022

Last winter, a woman recovering from frostbite on her hands and face came into the Brave Space Alliance drop-in center and received a barrier-repair kit with ceramide balm, petrolatum gauze, and broad-spectrum SPF. Staff helped her apply it. She returned every week for eight weeks, and the program connected her with a dermatologist who treated her scarring pro bono. It is not glamorous work. It is the kind of care that disappears from public attention precisely because the people receiving it have already been disappeared by every other system. They need donated product, funding for their clinical partnerships, and volunteers who can do intake.
