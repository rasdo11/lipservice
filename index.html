01
Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my eyebrow.
It was the most honest thing that happened to me all week.

I was tweezing under the kind of bathroom light that should be classified as a psychological weapon when I found it. Not on my head, where I've made peace with the silver situation, but in my left eyebrow, sticking straight up like a little antenna trying to pick up a signal from the future. It was coarse and luminous and completely uninterested in lying flat.

I stared at it for a long time. I thought about plucking it. I thought about dyeing it. I thought about the fourteen-step brow lamination tutorial I saved last week from a woman whose eyebrows look like they were designed by a structural engineer. And then I just left it there, because sometimes the most radical beauty choice you can make on a Tuesday morning is to simply leave your face alone.

There is a whole industry built on the idea that every new visible sign of age is a problem to be solved before anyone else notices it. But the gray eyebrow hair doesn't care about your opinion. It is already pointing toward the ceiling, thriving. I respect that energy deeply.

Welcome to today's issue. We have a brand quietly ghosting an entire continent, a mushroom-derived antioxidant that puts vitamin C in an uncomfortable position, a runway that made minimalism feel almost violent, and a charity that meets women at the exact moment they need beauty the most. Brand Drama
Typology Paris Is Pulling Its Tinted Serums From North America and the Explanation Doesn't Add Up
The French direct-to-consumer darling quietly removed its entire range of tinted serums from its US and Canadian storefronts last week, citing "localization priorities." But three former employees say the move follows months of disappointing sell-through after a reformulation last fall that shifted the shade range warmer and alienated the brand's core cool-toned loyalists. Typology's customer service team has been instructed to offer store credit, not refunds.
Industry Moves
Shiseido Just Poached Estée Lauder's Head of Global Skincare Innovation
Dr. Naia Morishita, who spent seven years leading Lauder's advanced serum pipeline, has quietly started at Shiseido's Osaka R&D center as of this month. The move signals Shiseido's aggressive push into biofermentation-based actives for its premium Clé de Peau line. Lauder declined to comment; Shiseido confirmed the hire with one sentence and no exclamation point.
Trend Alert
The "Skin Finishing" Trend Is Just Setting Powder for People Who Read Vogue
TikTok's latest obsession involves layering a translucent balm over powder foundation to create what creators are calling a "finished" complexion. The technique has been standard in editorial makeup for decades and involves nothing proprietary, but that hasn't stopped three brands from rushing to launch dedicated "finishing veil" products this week alone. At some point we will run out of new names for old things, but today is not that day.

Did You Know?
The mushroom molecule
your vitamin C should be worried about.

Ergothioneine is a naturally occurring amino acid found in high concentrations in shiitake and king oyster mushrooms, and it has spent the last decade being quietly studied by longevity researchers while the beauty industry was busy arguing about ascorbic acid stability. That quiet era is ending fast.

The Science, Briefly
Ergothioneine is transported into cells via a dedicated transporter protein called OCTN1, which means your body actively pulls it in rather than passively absorbing it. This selective uptake allows it to accumulate in tissues under high oxidative stress, including skin exposed to UV and pollution, where it neutralizes singlet oxygen and hydroxyl radicals with an efficiency that rivals L-ascorbic acid but without the pH sensitivity or oxidation problems.

A handful of K-beauty and J-beauty brands have been formulating with ergothioneine for over a year, but it hit the Western indie scene this spring when OS1 Skincare launched a 0.5% ergothioneine serum that sold out in 72 hours. The clinical data is still thin compared to vitamin C's mountain of trials, but what exists is unusually promising: a 2024 study in Free Radical Biology and Medicine found that topical ergothioneine reduced UV-induced DNA damage markers by 40% in ex vivo human skin. The mushroom wellness crowd is already claiming it, but the science here is genuinely interesting, not vibes-based. The uncomfortable truth is that ergothioneine is expensive to source at cosmetic grade and difficult to stabilize in water-based formulations, which means the first wave of products will either be genuinely well-made or quietly underdosed. The brands that list it fifth or sixth on the INCI panel are probably using it as a marketing garnish. The ones that lead with it and publish their concentration are the ones worth your money. Ask for the percentage. If a brand won't tell you, that is your answer.

"Ergothioneine is one of the very few antioxidants the body has built a dedicated transport system for. That biological specificity tells you something about how important it is to cellular defense." — Dr. Robert Beelman, Professor Emeritus of Food Science, Penn State University

Did You See?
Jil Sander AW26
made bare skin feel like armor.
AW26 · Zero-Product Skin at Jil Sander

At Jil Sander's AW26 show in Milan, makeup artist Thomas de Kluyver sent every model out with skin that looked almost refrigerated. No foundation, no blush, no visible product at all, just a thin layer of clear balm pressed onto freshly cleansed faces. The effect was deliberate and startling: under the cold white lights of the venue, each face looked like it had been sealed in ice, smooth and luminous in a way that felt almost confrontational. In a season full of maximalist skin textures and visible pigment, Jil Sander chose radical refusal, and it landed harder than any statement lip could have. The hair was similarly restrained, pulled into tight low knots still damp from washing, and the nails were bare and filed blunt. The whole proposal was that beauty could be an act of subtraction so severe it becomes its own spectacle. De Kluyver reportedly told backstage press that the brief was "skin that has never performed for anyone." In a fashion week where every other show seemed to shout, Jil Sander whispered, and the room went completely quiet. That tension between nothing and everything is the exact frequency Lip Service was built to transmit on.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of beginning again.

Look Good Feel Better is a global nonprofit founded in 1989 by the Personal Care Products Council that provides free beauty workshops and skincare kits to women undergoing cancer treatment. What makes their model distinct is the timing: they meet patients at active treatment centers, often on chemotherapy days, when the physical toll on skin, hair, and nails is most acute and most demoralizing.

117K+
women served annually across 27 countries with free workshops and kits

A volunteer facilitator in Houston described a recent session where a woman who had lost her eyebrows to chemo learned to draw them back on and then sat in the parking lot for twenty minutes just looking at herself in the rearview mirror. That is not vanity. That is a woman reintroducing herself to her own face. The organization runs on donated product and volunteer beauty professionals, and they are always in need of both, particularly licensed cosmetologists willing to lead sessions at rural treatment centers where patients have the fewest resources.

Healthy Tears
She was thirteen
and they said her body
was the wrong instrument.
She played it anyway.

Misty Copeland grew up in a world that was not designed to make room for her. She started ballet at thirteen in a Boys & Girls Club in San Pedro, California, learning positions on a basketball court in socks because she didn't own ballet shoes. Within months, her teacher recognized something extraordinary. Within years, the institutions that were supposed to nurture that talent began telling her it was shaped wrong. Too muscular. Too short. Too much curve in a form that classical ballet insisted should be a straight line.

She auditioned for the most elite training programs in the country and was turned away. Not because she lacked ability, but because her body didn't match the silhouette those programs had worshipped for a century. The rejection was delivered in the polite, devastating language of gatekeepers who believe they are curating rather than excluding. Copeland heard all of it. She kept showing up to the barre.

In 2015, American Ballet Theatre promoted her to principal dancer, the first Black woman to hold the title in the company's 75-year history. She was in the middle of a sold-out run of Swan Lake when the announcement came. That evening's curtain call lasted eleven minutes. The audience would not stop. People in the balcony were weeping. Copeland stood there in her white costume, absorbing something that had been withheld from an entire lineage of dancers who looked like her.

She said later, in an interview so quiet you had to lean in: "I had moments of doubting myself, and it felt like the world was saying, 'You are right to doubt.' But my body kept answering." That answer, given over and over in every tendu, every jump, every performance where she refused to become smaller, is the kind of reply that changes the dimensions of a stage. The room got bigger because she would not shrink.

They told her she had the wrong body. She became the standard.
