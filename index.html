Opening Statement
I went to the derm.
She was not impressed.

She looked at my skin for approximately four seconds, said "good barrier," and then looked at my chart the way a doctor looks at a chart when they're deciding how honest to be. Turns out I had been over-exfoliating — not dramatically, just enough to keep my skin in a state of polite inflammation it had clearly stopped complaining about. It had simply adapted. Gone quiet. Accepted its fate.

"Your skin is not a project," she said, already halfway out the door. "It wants to be left alone." I have been thinking about this every day since, partly because she's right, and partly because I have spent approximately $4,000 this year on things designed to intervene.

Anyway. That's where we are. Welcome to Lip Service — the newsletter for people who send the PubMed link and the unhinged meme in the same breath. This is issue one. Let's get into it.

Did You Know?
Salmon sperm is
in your serum now.

PDRN — polydeoxyribonucleotide, derived from salmon DNA — has officially graduated from South Korean dermatology clinics to your local Sephora shelf, and the fact that its origin story involves fish reproduction has done absolutely nothing to slow it down. In fact it might be helping.

The Science, Briefly
PDRN works by stimulating tissue regeneration at the cellular level — activating receptors that trigger collagen production and reduce inflammation. It's not a marketing word. It's a mechanism. Those are different things.

What makes it interesting beyond the headline is the delivery method: PDRN is now showing up in sheet masks, serums, and eye creams — which means the regenerative results that used to require a clinic visit are becoming accessible at home. Charli XCX uses it. Hailey Bieber uses it. Jennifer Aniston uses it. These are not automatically good endorsements but they do suggest a trend has cleared the celebrity adoption threshold, which is its own kind of data point.

The 360 beauty note here: your skin's ability to regenerate is also significantly mediated by sleep quality, cortisol levels, and magnesium — none of which come in a serum. PDRN works harder on a well-rested face. File that wherever you keep the things you know are true but find inconvenient.

"It's gone from a K-Beauty icon to a global breakout ingredient." — Clare Varga, VP Content, WGSN

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of defiance.

Lipstick Angels sends trained makeup artists and estheticians to hospital cancer wards — not for a makeover, but for an hour that belongs entirely to the patient. A facial. A hand massage. An eyebrow tutorial if that's what she wants. Founded in 2012 after the founder watched her grandmother apply lipstick before every chemo session as a badge of courage.

50K+
patients served in hospitals and cancer centers across the US

The woman who had lost her eyebrows to chemo and asked to learn how to draw them back on herself — not to have it done for her, but to learn — is the entire point. "It's not vanity. It's holding on to who you are."

They're expanding. They need volunteers with beauty credentials and donors who understand that this is medical care wearing a different face.

Healthy Tears
She danced at
the Apollo, the Cotton Club,
and with Frank Sinatra.
Then she was forgotten.
Then she wasn't.

Alice Barker was a Harlem Renaissance chorus dancer — the Apollo, the Cotton Club, the Zanzibar — performing alongside Frank Sinatra, Gene Kelly, and Ella Fitzgerald. She made television history as one of the first Black dancers to perform on TV with a white man. Then time passed, and no one remembered, and she ended up in a Brooklyn nursing home with no photos, no footage, no proof that any of it happened.

In 2015, a man who brought his therapy dog to visit her spent three years tracking down her films. Then he brought an iPad to her room and pressed play. She was 102. It was the first time she had ever seen herself dance.

"I am being paid to do something I enjoy doing — and I would do it for free, because it just felt so good."

40 million people have watched this. You'll know why in 30 seconds.
