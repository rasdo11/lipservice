The Injection Report
GLP-1 Volume Loss Now Has a Clinical Playbook

What's going on: Galderma released its first formal injection protocol for patients experiencing facial volume loss from GLP-1 receptor agonists this week, targeting the buccal fat pad, nasolabial folds, and periorbital hollows as primary treatment zones. The guidance recommends Restylane Contour for mid-face and Restylane Eyelight for tear troughs, with specific depth and volume parameters.

What it means: This is the industry acknowledging that semaglutide and tirzepatide patients aren't just losing weight — they're losing it in predictable patterns that require a different restoration strategy than standard age-related volume loss. The mid-face deflates faster; the temples hollow earlier.

What's next: Ask your injector if they've reviewed the new protocol. If they're still treating GLP-1 face like regular aging, you might be getting the wrong filler in the wrong plane.

The protocol specifies treating temples and lateral cheeks *before* nasolabial folds — the opposite of most standard approaches.

Put It In Your Mouth
That B12 Supplement Might Be Giving You Acne

What's going on: A study published this week in the Journal of Investigative Dermatology found that high-dose B12 supplementation alters the gene expression of skin bacteria, specifically *Cutibacterium acnes*, triggering inflammatory acne in previously clear-skinned subjects. The threshold appears to be above 1000mcg daily.

What that means: If you started a B complex for energy or added a methylcobalamin spray and suddenly broke out along your jawline, this is probably why. The bacteria aren't changing — their behavior is.

Lip Lab
High-Shine Gloss Is Back — With Actual Clinical Data

What's going on: Tower 28 launched ShineOn Milky Lip Jelly this week, a high-shine gloss formulated to meet National Eczema Association standards — the first glossy lip product to carry the certification.

Why it matters: Gloss has historically been a sensitivity nightmare: fragrance, synthetic polymers, and occlusive films that trap irritants. This formula uses hypochlorous acid (yes, the wound-healing ingredient) and skips every common sensitizer while still delivering that wet-look finish.

The verdict: Thick but not gluey. Actually comfortable on reactive lips. The 'magic hour' shade is the one.

Hypochlorous acid in a lip gloss isn't gimmick — it's the same antimicrobial used in clinical wound care.
