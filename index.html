The Injection Report
Allergan Drops Its First Clinical Framework for 'GLP-1 Face'

What's going on: Allergan Aesthetics published a clinical consensus paper this week outlining injection protocols specifically for patients who've lost significant facial volume on semaglutide and tirzepatide. The framework recommends a phased, deep-plane layering approach — starting with volumizers like Voluma at the periosteal level before addressing superficial hollowing — rather than the traditional single-visit full correction most patients request.

What it means: The volume loss pattern on GLP-1s is different from aging-related deflation. It's more diffuse, hits the buccal fat and periorbital area simultaneously, and tends to progress even after weight stabilizes. Injecting it like standard age-related loss — a syringe of filler in the cheeks, see you in a year — often produces that overfilled-but-still-hollow look. The phased protocol spaces sessions 4–6 weeks apart and uses 2–3 products per phase.

What's next: Ask your injector whether they've seen the Allergan consensus paper and whether they're adjusting their layering protocol for GLP-1 patients. If they look blank, that's your answer.

The key shift: Allergan now recommends treating GLP-1 volume loss in 2–3 staged sessions rather than one aggressive fill. If your injector is pushing a single-visit full correction, get a second opinion.

Put It In Your Mouth
Strawberries Just Got a Dentistry Co-Sign

What's going on: A study published this week in the *Journal of Oral Rehabilitation* found that malic acid from fresh strawberries reduced extrinsic tooth staining by 18% over four weeks when participants ate roughly eight strawberries daily — without the enamel erosion associated with whitening strips or high-concentration peroxide trays.

What that means: Malic acid acts as a gentle surface astringent that breaks down chromogen deposits (the compounds from coffee and wine that bind to enamel) without dropping oral pH to dangerous levels. It's not replacing professional whitening, but it's a legitimate maintenance strategy between appointments.

Lip Lab
High-Dose Niacinamide Lip Products Are Irritating More People Than They're Helping

What's going on: The Ordinary quietly reformulated its niacinamide serum to 5% from 10% this month after years of user complaints about irritation — and derms on social media are pointing out that lip products riding the niacinamide wave are using concentrations that vermilion border skin was never meant to handle.

Why it matters: Niacinamide above 5% can trigger histamine release in sensitive skin. Lip skin is thinner, has no sebaceous glands, and absorbs actives faster. Three lip serums launched in the last quarter advertise 10% niacinamide as a feature. For most people, that's a contact dermatitis origin story.

The verdict: If your lip serum has niacinamide above 5%, you're likely getting irritation without proportional benefit. Look for 2–4% in lip-specific formulas, or just let your face serum migrate south on its own.

The Ordinary dropped its niacinamide to 5% for a reason. If your lip product is advertising 10%, your lips are absorbing a concentration that even facial skin finds irritating.
