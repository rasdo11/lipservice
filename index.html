The Injection Report
Sculptra Gets Its Ozempic-Face Moment

What's going on: Galderma published new 24-month data this week showing poly-L-lactic acid (Sculptra) restores mid-face volume loss with a 92% patient satisfaction rate at two years — and the company is explicitly marketing the results toward GLP-1-related facial fat loss. Dermatologists at the AACS meeting in Orlando reported a 40% increase in Sculptra consultations tied directly to semaglutide patients.

What it means: Unlike HA fillers that add volume day-of, Sculptra stimulates your own collagen over 3–6 months, which makes it a better match for the diffuse, gradual hollowing GLP-1s cause. It's not replacing what's lost — it's rebuilding the scaffolding. Two to three vials per session, two to three sessions, and you're looking at results that outlast most filler by a year.

What's next: If you're on a GLP-1 and noticing temple or cheek deflation, ask your injector about biostimulators before reaching for Voluma. The timing matters — starting Sculptra early in your weight-loss arc gets better results than trying to backfill after the fact.

Ask your injector: 'Am I a better candidate for a biostimulator or a traditional HA filler given my rate of volume loss?' That one question separates a good plan from an expensive bandaid.

Put It In Your Mouth
Cocoa Just Got a Cardiology Co-Sign

What's going on: Results from the COSMOS follow-up published this week in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition confirmed that 500mg/day of cocoa flavanols reduced cardiovascular events by 15% over a 5-year period — and the subgroup analysis showed the strongest benefit in women over 40 with low baseline fruit intake. This is the largest flavanol RCT ever completed.

What that means: Flavanols improve endothelial function, which is a fancy way of saying they help your blood vessels relax. That's circulation to your skin, your brain, and everything else you care about. The dose is specific: 500mg flavanols, not "dark chocolate." A standard bar gets you maybe 40mg. You need a supplement or pure cocoa powder.

This week's cheat meal: A double-thick frozen hot chocolate from Jacques Torres in NYC — or your own version with two tablespoons of Dutch-process cocoa, oat milk, and a stupid amount of whipped cream. It's 90°F outside and I don't care.

What to cut: Those "beauty chocolate" bars marketing 80% cacao as a flavanol source — the processing destroys most of it. Check for actual flavanol content on the label or skip it.

Lip Lab
That Lip Oil Might Be an Endocrine Disruptor

What's going on: A new Environmental Health Perspectives study out this week tested 48 popular lip oils and glosses and found that 31 of them contained lavender or tea tree oil derivatives at concentrations linked to estrogenic and anti-androgenic activity in cell assays. Several products from Dior, Clarins, and a cult K-beauty brand were flagged.

Why it matters: You reapply lip products 5–14 times a day and ingest a significant portion. Lavender oil has been linked to prepubertal gynecomastia in prior case studies — the dose-on-lips-all-day exposure model hasn't been studied yet, but the endocrine disruption signal is real at these concentrations.

The verdict: Check your lip oil ingredient list for lavandula angustifolia or melaleuca alternifolia. If it's in the top ten ingredients, consider swapping. The Tower 28 ShineOn and Merit Shade Slick lines are both free of both — and the formulas actually perform.

Quick label check: if lavandula angustifolia (lavender) or melaleuca alternifolia (tea tree) appears in the first ten ingredients of your lip oil, that's a meaningful exposure given reapplication frequency.
