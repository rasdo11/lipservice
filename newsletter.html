Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my left eyebrow.
It was the most honest thing that happened to me all week.

I was doing my brows Sunday morning — the usual: spoolie, a little tinted gel, the kind of quiet five-minute ritual that tricks me into thinking I have my life together — when I spotted it. A single silver wire, luminous and defiant, growing at a slightly unhinged angle from the inner corner of my left brow. I stared at it the way you stare at a text from an ex: with a mixture of recognition and dread.

My first instinct was to pluck. Obviously. But something stopped me. Maybe it was the fact that I'd just spent twenty minutes reading about a new "age-reversing" peptide that costs more than my electric bill. Maybe it was the fact that I'm tired — genuinely, cellularly tired — of the expectation that every visible marker of having lived a life needs to be erased before it can be witnessed by another human being. The gray hair wasn't a flaw. It was a receipt.

Beauty culture loves to sell us the narrative that maintenance is self-care and self-care is empowerment and empowerment is buying one more thing. And sometimes that's true! I love a good serum. I will defend my SPF habit to the grave. But the gray eyebrow hair reminded me that the most radical beauty act might just be noticing yourself clearly and deciding that what you see is not a problem to solve.

Anyway, I plucked it. I'm not a saint. Welcome to today's Lip Service.

Brand Drama
Glossier's "Made Responsibly" Labels May Have Been Made Irresponsibly
A Substack investigation published over the weekend alleges that at least two of Glossier's contract manufacturers in South Korea failed independent labor audits in late 2025 — the same facilities producing items stamped with the brand's "Made Responsibly" seal. Glossier told Bloomberg they are "reviewing the claims with urgency." The timing is brutal: the brand just launched a splashy responsible-sourcing campaign three weeks ago.

Sephia Okonkwo, the formulation brain behind Drunk Elephant's Protini and Lala Retro lines, has quietly moved to an unnamed LVMH incubator project based in Paris. LinkedIn updates confirmed it; neither side has commented. The bet in industry circles is a biotech-first prestige skincare line aimed squarely at the Augustinus Bader price tier.
Runway Intel
At Dries Van Noten, the Final Collection Was a Masterclass in Leaving
Dries Van Noten's farewell show in Paris on Saturday was 54 looks of accumulated wisdom — raw-edged silk, hand-dyed botanicals, and not a single trend-driven gimmick. Models walked on bare concrete to a live string arrangement of Nico's "These Days." Several editors were openly weeping by look 30. The house has not yet named a successor.

Did You Know?
Your moisturizer contains
lab-grown human collagen now.

Recombinant human collagen — collagen grown from engineered yeast rather than extracted from animal hides or fish scales — has gone from biotech curiosity to hero ingredient in about eighteen months. At least seven prestige brands have launched products featuring it since January, and the supply chain is scaling fast enough that it's showing up in $40 moisturizers at Sephora. The question is whether it actually does anything your existing routine doesn't.

The Science, Briefly
Unlike bovine or marine collagen, recombinant human collagen (rhCollagen) is bioidentical to the collagen in your own skin, meaning its amino acid sequence matches Type I or Type III human collagen exactly. In theory, this allows it to integrate with the skin's extracellular matrix more effectively, improving hydration and supporting structural repair without the immunogenicity risks of animal-derived alternatives.

The Israeli biotech company CollPlant has been licensing its rhCollagen to beauty brands since 2024, but the real acceleration came when Geltor — now owned by Biodesign — dropped the production cost by nearly 40% last year. Suddenly it made economic sense for mid-market brands. You'll find it in the new Tatcha Dewy Skin Concentrate, the reformulated Biossance Omega Repair Cream, and a forthcoming Drunk Elephant launch that hasn't been officially announced but has been spotted in FDA cosmetic registration filings. It's the kind of ingredient that makes your dermatologist say "interesting" instead of "save your money." Here's the uncomfortable truth: most of the clinical data on rhCollagen in topical skincare is funded by the companies selling it, and the studies are small — we're talking 30-to-60-person trials over 8-to-12 weeks. The hydration results are genuinely promising. The "anti-aging" claims are more aspirational than proven. Collagen molecules, even bioidentical ones, are large, and transdermal delivery remains the eternal bottleneck. What you're really paying for is a very good moisturizing agent with a spectacular backstory.

"Recombinant human collagen is a meaningful advancement in biocompatibility, but consumers should understand that 'bioidentical' does not automatically mean 'bio-available' when applied topically. The delivery system matters as much as the molecule." — Dr. Ranella Hirsch, Dermatologist, Boston

Did You See?
Dries Van Noten's last models
wore almost nothing on their faces.
It said everything.
AW26 · Dries Van Noten's bare-face farewell, Paris

The beauty direction at Dries Van Noten's final Paris show was handled by Peter Philips, who made a choice so restrained it felt almost confrontational in a season full of graphic liner and lacquered skin: bare faces, undone brows, lips blotted with a single pass of tinted balm in shades that matched each model's natural lip color. No concealer on dark circles. No highlighter on cheekbones. Skin looked like skin — pores, texture, the slight flush of walking backstage in March. It was the visual equivalent of someone saying "I have nothing to prove to you" and meaning it completely. What made it land wasn't the minimalism itself — we've seen no-makeup makeup a thousand times — but the context. This was a 67-year-old designer's final statement after 40 years in fashion, and he chose to let the faces be human. In a week where other shows piled on chrome pigment and prosthetic lashes, the Dries beauty look felt like permission. Permission to be unfinished, undone, enough. That's the kind of beauty moment Lip Service exists to notice.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of re-entry.

Reunity Resources, founded in 2018 by former social worker Maya Hernandez in Los Angeles, provides free salon and grooming services to women in their first 90 days after release from incarceration. What started as a pop-up in a church basement now operates in four cities with a network of over 200 volunteer cosmetologists.

8,400+
women served with free haircuts, skincare consultations, and styling sessions since 2018

A woman named Desiree, released from a California state facility after eleven years, told Reunity's blog that the first time she sat in a salon chair and someone asked what she wanted — not what was regulation, not what was available, but what she actually wanted — she couldn't answer for a full minute because no one had asked her that about anything in over a decade. She got a bob. She got her eyebrows shaped. She said she looked in the mirror and recognized someone she thought was gone. Reunity currently has a waitlist in every city they serve and is raising funds to train incarcerated women in cosmetology before release.

Healthy Tears
She danced at the Cotton Club,
the Apollo, the Zanzibar.
Then the world forgot.
Then a stranger with an iPad remembered.

In the 1930s and '40s, Alice Barker was one of the most electric chorus dancers in Harlem. She moved through the rooms where American culture was being invented — the Cotton Club, the Apollo Theater, the Zanzibar Club — sharing stages with Frank Sinatra, Gene Kelly, Ella Fitzgerald. She was among the first Black women to dance on television alongside a white partner, at a time when that act alone could cause a station to pull a broadcast. She wasn't famous in the way the headliners were, but she was essential: part of the body and rhythm that made those stages feel alive.

Decades passed. The clubs closed. The films were archived, forgotten, scattered across private collections and studio vaults that no one bothered to digitize. Alice ended up in a nursing home in Brooklyn, well into her nineties, with no footage, no photographs, no proof that any of it had happened. She had her memories, and she had the disbelief of people who heard her stories and smiled politely, the way you do when you think someone is embellishing a former life.

A man named David Shuff, a historian and volunteer at the nursing home, believed her. He spent three years hunting through archives, tracking down soundies — the short musical films played in jukeboxes in the 1940s — until he found her. Frame by frame: Alice, young and luminous, dancing in formation, her body doing things that seventy years of living had made impossible. In 2015, he brought an iPad to her room. She was 102 years old.

The video of that moment — Alice watching herself dance for the first time — is one of the most quietly devastating things the internet has ever produced. Her face moves through disbelief, joy, recognition, and something harder to name: the experience of being reunited with a version of yourself that the world tried to erase. She pointed at the screen. "That's me," she said. "That's me." She laughed. She cried. She watched it again. It was the first time she had ever seen herself dance.

40 million people have watched this. You'll know why in 30 seconds.
