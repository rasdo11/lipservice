Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my eyebrow.
It felt like a prophecy.

I was doing my brows on Sunday — the usual: spoolie, pencil, a prayer — when I noticed it. One single, wiry, almost translucent hair growing at a diagonal that suggested it had no interest in cooperating with the rest of my face. A gray eyebrow hair. Not on my head, where I've made my peace with the silver. In my eyebrow. Where I was not emotionally prepared.

I plucked it, obviously. Then I held it up to the bathroom light like a forensic analyst on a prestige cable drama. It shimmered. It was kind of beautiful, actually. And I immediately felt guilty for removing it, which is the precise emotional cycle that beauty culture has perfected: notice, panic, fix, regret fixing, Google "is it bad to pluck gray eyebrow hairs," read three contradictory articles, close phone, move on with day.

The thing nobody tells you about getting older in the beauty space is that it's not one dramatic moment. It's a series of tiny, absurd negotiations with your own reflection. You're not aging — you're just constantly being introduced to new versions of yourself, each one slightly more unfiltered than the last. And honestly? That gray hair had more personality than anything else on my face that morning.

Anyway. Welcome to today's Lip Service, where we talk about the things that actually happen to your face, your cart, and your psyche — with full specificity and zero judgment. Let's get into it.

Did You Know?
Ergothioneine is
the new niacinamide.

You're going to start seeing this word everywhere, so let's get ahead of it: ergothioneine is an amino acid found in mushrooms, and it's being called the body's "longevity vitamin." The skincare industry has noticed.

The Science, Briefly
Ergothioneine works as a cytoprotective antioxidant — meaning it doesn't just neutralize free radicals on the skin's surface but actually penetrates cells and accumulates in mitochondria, protecting them from oxidative damage at the source. Unlike vitamins C and E, it has its own dedicated cellular transporter (OCTN1), which means your body actively pulls it in.

Right now, it's showing up in serums from brands like Amorepacific, Dr. Andrew Weil for Origins, and a handful of indie labels that got to the PubMed papers first. The sourcing story is interesting too — most cosmetic-grade ergothioneine is derived from fermented shiitake or oyster mushrooms, which gives it a clean-beauty narrative that niacinamide never quite had. It slots perfectly into the "fungi wellness" moment that's been building since 2023. Here's the uncomfortable-but-true part: the clinical evidence, while promising, is still mostly in vitro and animal-model territory. There are a few small human trials showing improved skin elasticity and reduced UV-induced redness after 12 weeks of topical application, but we're not yet at the level of evidence that retinoids or vitamin C have behind them. That said, dermatologists are cautiously optimistic, and the safety profile is excellent — no irritation, no photosensitivity, plays well with everything in your routine. It's a low-risk bet on an ingredient that might turn out to be genuinely important.

"Ergothioneine is one of the few antioxidants I'm genuinely excited about in a decade. The fact that cells have a dedicated transporter for it tells us biology considers it essential — we just haven't caught up yet." — Dr. Priya Rao, Research Dermatologist, Mount Sinai Kravitz Center

Did You See?
Dries Van Noten's
last bow was silent.
The eyeliner was not.
AW26 · Graphic Kohl at Dries Van Noten

At the Dries Van Noten AW26 show in Paris — the first collection completed entirely by the design team after the founder's retirement — makeup artist Peter Philips sent models down the runway wearing a single, unbroken line of black kohl that extended from the inner corner of each eye past the temple and into the hairline. No foundation. No lip color. Just skin, that line, and clothes so beautiful they made editors in the front row visibly emotional. It was grief rendered as graphic liner. What made it land wasn't the technique — bold liner isn't new — but the restraint around it. Every other element of the face was deliberately bare, almost raw, so the eye became the entire story. Philips reportedly mixed a matte cake liner with a drop of jojoba oil to get a texture that looked hand-drawn rather than precise. It's the kind of beauty moment that reminds you makeup can be a language, not just a routine. And that's exactly the kind of thing we live for here at Lip Service.

Beauty as an act
of re-entry.

Bottomless Closet is a New York City nonprofit founded in 1999 that provides professional clothing, styling, and beauty services — haircuts, makeup consultations, skincare — to women transitioning out of homelessness, incarceration, or long-term unemployment. The idea is deceptively simple: looking the part isn't vanity, it's armor.

18,000+
women styled and prepared for job interviews since founding

Last year, a woman named Denise walked into the Bottomless Closet space on West 25th Street for the first time in the suit she'd been given by a shelter — too big, wrong color, someone else's life. She walked out two hours later in a fitted blazer, with a fresh haircut and a tube of tinted moisturizer she'd been taught to apply herself. She got the job at a dental office four days later and told the staff she almost didn't go to the interview before her appointment. They need licensed cosmetologists willing to volunteer monthly sessions, and they always need unopened professional-grade products.

Healthy Tears
She danced at the Apollo,
the Cotton Club, the Zanzibar.
Then the world forgot.
Then a stranger remembered.

Alice Barker started dancing in Harlem in the 1930s, when the stages were hot and the music was hotter. She was a chorus girl — one of the women who moved behind and beside the names we still know. She performed at the Apollo, the Cotton Club, the Zanzibar Club. She shared stages with Frank Sinatra, Gene Kelly, Ella Fitzgerald. She was among the first Black women to dance on television with a white partner, at a time when that sentence alone could end a career or start a riot. She was, by every measure, a part of history.

But chorus girls don't get biographies. They get moments — a flash of leg in someone else's spotlight, a silhouette in the background of a photograph captioned with another person's name. By the time Alice was living in a nursing home in Brooklyn, in her late nineties, she had no footage of herself. No proof that the stages, the lights, the music had ever been real. Just her memory, and the growing silence around it.

A man named David Shuff, who worked in elder care and had a habit of listening — really listening — to his residents, heard Alice's stories and believed them. He spent three years combing through archives, film libraries, and private collections, searching for footage of the Harlem chorus lines of the 1930s and '40s. He found it. And in 2015, he brought an iPad to Alice Barker's room.

She was 102 years old. She watched herself dance for the first time. In the video — which went on to be seen by millions — her face moves through surprise, then recognition, then something that doesn't have a word in English. She laughs. She narrates. She points at the screen and says, "That's me." She had spent decades knowing what her body could do, and in that moment, she finally got to see it — young, luminous, undeniable. The world had forgotten Alice Barker, but her body never forgot itself.
