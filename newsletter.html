01
Opening Statement
I found a gray hair in my eyebrow.
It was the most honest thing that happened to me all week.

I was doing my brows in the bathroom mirror, the overhead light turned up to its most unforgiving wattage, when I saw it. A single silver hair, stiff and defiant, pointing slightly upward like a tiny antenna receiving a signal I hadn't agreed to. I plucked it, obviously. And then I stared at the tweezers for a long time, holding this very small piece of evidence that time is not asking for my consent.

The thing nobody tells you about aging is that it does not arrive in some cinematic montage. It shows up in a bathroom on a Sunday morning while you're running late. It shows up in the form of a hair that is simply a different color now. It shows up and it does not care whether you've been using retinol or drinking water or doing all the things the algorithm promised would keep you safe.

I've been thinking about this all week, the way the beauty industry sells us the idea that aging is a series of problems with purchasable solutions, when really it's just a series of tiny shifts in the way light hits your face. Some of them are beautiful. Some of them are just information. None of them are emergencies.

Anyway, welcome to Sunday's Lip Service, where we honor the antenna hairs and the questions they receive. Pull up a chair.

Brand Drama
Westman Atelier Is Pulling Two Foundation Sticks and the Excuse Doesn't Add Up
Westman Atelier has quietly removed its Baby and Atelier IX stick foundations from Sephora shelves and its own DTC site, citing raw material sourcing disruptions. But two former employees say the shades were chronic underperformers that averaged fewer than 400 units per month globally. Gucci Westman has not commented publicly, and Sephora's product pages now redirect to the brand's remaining six shades.
Industry Moves
Shiseido Just Poached L'Oréal's Head of Biotech Research
Dr. Laure Michelet, who spent nine years leading L'Oréal's advanced biotechnology and fermentation division in Chevilly-Larue, has officially joined Shiseido's Global Innovation Center in Yokohama as of this week. The move signals Shiseido's aggressive push into bio-derived actives and lab-grown collagen alternatives. L'Oréal declined to name a successor.
Trend Alert
The "Undereye Vein" Trend Just Crossed Over from TikTok to Actual Editorials
What started as a niche makeup technique, drawing faint blue veins beneath the eyes to simulate translucent skin, has now appeared in Vogue Italia's March digital cover shoot and a Zara Beauty campaign released this week. Makeup artist Isamaya Ffrench called it "the logical endpoint of the no-makeup fantasy" in an Instagram story. The look requires a superfine brush and more restraint than most of us possess.

Did You Know?
Polyglutamic acid is
making hyaluronic acid sweat.

You've probably seen it lurking on ingredient lists for a year or two, wedged between glycerin and something unpronounceable. Polyglutamic acid, or PGA, is a fermentation-derived polymer that holds up to five thousand times its weight in water. That makes hyaluronic acid, which holds about a thousand times its weight, look like it showed up to the hydration Olympics without training.

The Science, Briefly
Polyglutamic acid works by forming a moisture-retaining film on the skin's surface while simultaneously inhibiting the enzyme hyaluronidase, which breaks down your natural hyaluronic acid. The result is a dual mechanism: it hydrates topically and slows the degradation of your body's own hydration infrastructure from within.

Japanese brand Tunemakers has been formulating with PGA for years, and Korean brands like Isntree and Cosrx have rolled it into recent serum launches. But the real signal is that Paula's Choice just added a standalone PGA booster to its US lineup as of this month, a move that suggests the ingredient has crossed over from K-beauty curiosity to mainstream contender. The texture is slippery and slightly tacky, which takes some getting used to if you worship a watery toner. Here is the uncomfortable truth: most people layering PGA on top of hyaluronic acid are probably doubling up on hydration they don't need, while ignoring the lipid barrier damage that's causing the dryness in the first place. PGA is genuinely impressive science. But it is not a substitute for understanding why your skin is thirsty.

"Polyglutamic acid is one of the more elegant humectants we have access to right now, but patients often use it to compensate for a compromised barrier rather than addressing the barrier itself." — Dr. Ranella Hirsch, Dermatologist, Boston

Did You See?
Marni AW26 turned every face
into an unfinished painting
and nobody looked away.
AW26 · Raw Pigment Faces at Marni

At Marni's AW26 show in Milan, creative director Francesco Risso sent models down the runway with faces streaked in raw, unmixed pigment: cadmium yellows dragged across foreheads, oxide reds smudged along jawlines, terre verte pooling in the hollows of cheeks. Lead makeup artist Thomas de Kluyver used actual artists' pigment mixed into a clear balm base, applying it with his fingers rather than brushes. The effect was less "editorial makeup" and more "someone wept while restoring a fresco and then walked into the room still covered in the evidence." What made it land was the refusal to blend. In a season where almost every major house has chased some version of glass skin or invisible perfection, Marni offered something raw and unresolved. The pigment sat on top of the skin rather than melting into it, making each face look like a surface that had been acted upon by something outside of itself. It felt like the opposite of control. It felt, in the Lip Service sense, like the truth about beauty: it does not need to be finished to be the most interesting thing in the room.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of reentry.

Returning Citizens Beauty Collective, founded in 2022 in Baltimore by aesthetician-turned-advocate Dominique Hollins, provides free skincare consultations, dermaplaning sessions, and interview-prep beauty kits to women leaving the criminal justice system and reentering the workforce. Hollins started the program after noticing that formerly incarcerated women in her salon chair often hadn't touched their own faces in a caring way for years.

2,400+
women served with free skincare and beauty consultations since 2022

One woman who came through the program last fall had spent eight years incarcerated and told Hollins she didn't recognize her own face in the mirror. After a consultation, a gentle facial, and a tutorial on basic skincare, she said it was the first time in nearly a decade she looked at herself and saw a person rather than a case number. The Collective currently operates in Baltimore and D.C. and is raising funds to expand into Philadelphia this summer. They need product donations, licensed aestheticians willing to volunteer, and money.

Healthy Tears
Nina Simone
sang "Feeling Good"
like she had invented
the concept of being free.

Before she was an icon, before she was exiled, before the industry chewed through her publishing rights and her peace of mind, Nina Simone was a classical pianist from Tryon, North Carolina, who wanted to be the first Black woman to play Carnegie Hall as a concert pianist. The world told her no. So she became something else entirely. She became the kind of artist who could turn a Broadway show tune into a manifesto.

"Feeling Good" was written by Anthony Newley and Leslie Bricusse for a 1964 musical called The Roar of the Greasepaint. It was a pleasant number about optimism. And then Nina Simone recorded it in 1965 for her album I Put a Spell on You, and it stopped being pleasant. It became enormous. Her version opens with a voice that sounds like it is arriving from somewhere older than the song itself, somewhere beneath the lyrics, where the feeling is not cheerful but ancient and unchainable.

But the studio recording, as perfect as it is, only tells you half the story. Live, with a full band, Simone played the song like she was pulling it out of the earth. There is a moment, it happens in nearly every live version, where she pauses. She looks at the audience. The band holds. And in that silence there is something that the lyrics cannot say: that freedom is not a feeling but a practice, that it must be insisted upon every single time you open your mouth. Music critics have spent decades arguing over which live performance is the definitive one. They are all correct, because every time she sang it, she meant it again.

Years later, asked in an interview what freedom meant to her, Simone said: "I'll tell you what freedom is to me. No fear." She paused. "I mean, really, no fear." That pause held everything. It held Tryon, and the refusal, and every stage she ever set on fire. It held the fact that fearlessness is not the absence of danger but the decision to sing anyway.

The song that sounds different every time, because she meant it differently every time.
