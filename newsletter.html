Opening Statement
I reorganized my entire medicine cabinet at 1 a.m.
It did not fix what I was hoping it would fix.

There's a specific kind of delusion that hits around midnight on a Sunday — the one where you decide that the reason you feel untethered is because your retinol is next to your vitamin C and the niacinamide is behind the expired SPF you keep meaning to throw away. So you pull everything out. You line it all up on the counter like evidence. You Google expiration symbols. You wipe down shelves. And for eleven glorious minutes, you feel like a person who has her life together.

Then you catch your reflection in that terrible overhead bathroom light — the one that turns everyone into a Victorian ghost — and you remember that no amount of shelf organization can substitute for sleep, hydration, or returning your mother's call. The products go back. Not quite in the right order. You go to bed anyway.

I think about this a lot: the way beauty routines become the thing we control when everything else feels slippery. It's not shallow. It's actually deeply human — this desire to impose order on chaos, even if that chaos is just your own face at the end of a long week. The ritual matters more than the result, most of the time. We just don't like admitting that.

Anyway. Welcome to Monday's Lip Service. We have industry mess, ingredient science, runway beauty that made me hold my breath, and a story about a man who refused to be told when to stop singing. Let's get into it.

Brand Drama
Kosas quietly discontinues its Cloud Set powder — and the reviews tell the story
If you visited the Kosas site this weekend, you may have noticed the Cloud Set Baked Setting & Smoothing Powder has vanished without a press release, a farewell post, or even a sad little "sold out" tag. Sephora reviews from the last two months tell a consistent story: reformulation complaints, flashback issues, and a texture shift that longtime buyers called "chalky." The brand has not commented, which in beauty PR terms is itself a comment.
Industry Moves
L'Oréal poaches high-profile dermatologist Dr. Adeline Kikam for new clinical advisory role
Dr. Kikam, the board-certified derm behind @brownskinderm with 1.4 million followers, has signed on as L'Oréal Dermatological Beauty's North American Clinical Advisor — a newly created position that gives her oversight on claims substantiation and clinical trial design for La Roche-Posay, CeraVe, and Vichy. The appointment signals L'Oréal's growing anxiety about the FTC's tightening scrutiny of "clinically proven" language in mass-market skincare. Expect fewer asterisks, more actual data.
Runway Intel
Dries Van Noten's successor shows first solo collection in Paris — and the beauty was the story
Julian Klausner, who spent seven years as Van Noten's right hand before being named creative director last fall, sent models down the Palais de Tokyo runway with what can only be described as post-impressionist blush — diffused washes of mauve and saffron applied from temple to jawline, no lip color, brows brushed deliberately askew. Pat McGrath's team executed the looks, and backstage she reportedly called it "the most editorial brief I've been given in years." The clothes were very good. The cheekbones were better.

Did You Know?
Postbiotics are not probiotics
and your skin barrier knows the difference.

You've seen "postbiotic" on at least three new launches this quarter — lip oils, barrier creams, scalp serums, even a hand cream from Byoma that dropped last Tuesday. But unlike probiotics (live bacteria) or prebiotics (food for bacteria), postbiotics are the metabolic byproducts left behind after bacteria have already done their work. Think of them as the thank-you note, not the guest.

The Science, Briefly
Postbiotics — primarily short-chain fatty acids, bacteriocins, and cell wall fragments — interact with toll-like receptors in the skin's innate immune system to modulate inflammatory signaling without introducing live organisms. This means they can reinforce barrier function and reduce transepidermal water loss without the stability headaches that make true probiotic skincare so difficult to formulate.

The ingredient is showing up fastest in the "sensitive skin" category, where brands like La Roche-Posay (Lipikar AP+M), Kinship, and the new Byoma scalp line are using lysates derived from Lactobacillus fermentation. It fits the current consumer mood perfectly: people want microbiome-friendly without the kombucha-on-your-face energy. Postbiotics are easier to stabilize, have a longer shelf life, and don't require refrigeration — which makes them a formulator's dream and a marketing team's answered prayer. Here's the uncomfortable part: most "postbiotic" claims on packaging aren't regulated with any specificity, and the concentration needed for measurable barrier repair (typically studied at 2–5% lysate) isn't always what you're getting in that $22 drugstore serum. The science is genuinely promising — a 2025 study in the Journal of Investigative Dermatology showed a 31% improvement in barrier recovery time with topical lactobacillus-derived postbiotics versus vehicle alone. But "contains postbiotics" on a label tells you about as much as "contains water." Ask for percentages. Read beyond the front of the box.

"Postbiotics offer the anti-inflammatory benefits of a healthy microbiome without the formulation instability of live cultures — but the industry needs standardized nomenclature before consumers can make truly informed choices." — Dr. Hadley King, Board-Certified Dermatologist, Clinical Instructor at Weill Cornell

Did You See?
Julian Klausner's debut
painted blush like a bruise
and it was the most beautiful thing in Paris.
AW26 · Dries Van Noten by Julian Klausner — Diffused Blush Moment, Paris

The look was deceptively simple: bare lids, no mascara, brows brushed upward and left alone, skin so hydrated it looked almost wet. But the blush — the blush was the whole thesis. Applied in broad, watercolor-like sweeps from the orbital bone down past the hollows of the cheeks and onto the jaw, in shades that ranged from faded rose to deep saffron depending on skin tone, it read less like makeup and more like weather. Like something that had happened to the face rather than been placed on it. Pat McGrath's team used a mix of cream and liquid pigments, blending with fingers and then pressing with damp sponges to create what one backstage assistant described as "controlled chaos." What made it remarkable wasn't the technique — editorial blush placement has been cycling through runways since McQueen — but the restraint everywhere else. No lips. No liner. No contour. Just this one gesture, pushed to its emotional limit, on an otherwise untouched face. It felt like the beauty equivalent of a long pause in conversation: the confidence to let one thing speak. We'll be referencing this for the rest of the season, and frankly, the rest of the year. This is the kind of moment Lip Service exists to notice.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of dignity.

Beauty Bus Foundation was founded in 2013 by Melissa Maher after she watched her mother, who was terminally ill, light up during an in-home beauty treatment in a way that months of medical care hadn't achieved. The Los Angeles-based nonprofit provides free beauty and grooming services to chronically or terminally ill individuals and, through its shelter program, to women and families experiencing homelessness.

75,000+
free beauty and grooming services delivered since founding

Last year, a Beauty Bus volunteer visited a women's shelter in East Los Angeles and gave a resident named Maria her first haircut in over two years. Maria told the volunteer it was the first time in months she felt like someone saw her as a person and not a case number. That's not a small thing — research consistently links grooming and self-care access to improved mental health outcomes and higher rates of successful rehousing. Beauty Bus needs licensed cosmetologists, estheticians, and barbers willing to volunteer, as well as funding to expand their shelter program to three new cities in 2026.

Healthy Tears
He was told to cut it short.
He gave them twenty-one minutes instead.
Seventy-two thousand people
sang back every word.

By the summer of 1985, Freddie Mercury had been famous for over a decade in the way that eventually becomes a kind of weather — constant, inescapable, so present it stops being remarkable. Queen hadn't had a major hit in the U.S. in years. The music press had moved on. Critics used words like "bombastic" and "overblown," which were just polite ways of saying they found him too much. He was too much. That was always the point.

Live Aid was Bob Geldof's impossible ask — the biggest concert ever staged, broadcast to 1.9 billion people across 150 countries, with every major act in rock music given roughly fifteen minutes to justify their legend in real time. The slot was a trap dressed as an honor. David Bowie played well. Elton John played well. The Who played well. Everyone played well. And then, at 6:41 in the London evening, Freddie Mercury walked onto the Wembley stage in a white tank top and sat down at a piano, and something shifted in the molecular structure of the afternoon.

What happened over the next twenty-one minutes is the closest thing to a secular miracle that popular music has produced. He opened with "Bohemian Rhapsody" — truncated, just the operatic section into the headbang, a choice so audacious it bordered on comedy — and then tore into "Radio Ga Ga" as 72,000 people clapped in unison without being asked. He did the call-and-response. He did the fist pump. He prowled the stage with the energy of someone who had somewhere to be in an hour and intended to leave the building in ruins before he got there. The organizers had told Queen to keep it tight. Mercury added an improvised vocal run during "We Are the Champions" that lasted longer than some bands' entire sets. He did not appear to care about the schedule. He appeared, in fact, to be having the single best time of his life.

Music historians still call it the greatest live performance ever. Not because it was technically perfect — it wasn't — but because it was the purest demonstration of what happens when someone decides, fully and without apology, to take up every inch of space they were given and then some. "I won't be a rock star," Mercury once said. "I will be a legend." He was not wrong. He was never wrong about that.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
