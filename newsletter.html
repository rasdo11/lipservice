01
Opening Statement
I bought a lip liner at the airport.
It changed my entire week.

I was standing in a duty-free in Terminal 2 at Heathrow, slightly hungover, extremely dehydrated, doing that thing where you pretend you're browsing but really you're just killing 90 minutes before boarding. I picked up a lip liner from a brand I'd never heard of, in a shade the girl at the counter called "your lips after red wine," and I bought it because it cost nine pounds and I had no fight left in me.

I wore it on the plane. I wore it to the meeting I landed for. I wore it the next morning with nothing else on my face, and a woman in a coffee shop told me I looked like I'd had a very good weekend. I had not. I'd had a weekend defined by delayed trains and a friend canceling dinner by text. But the lip liner didn't know that.

That's the thing about beauty at its most useful. It doesn't fix anything. It just rearranges the evidence. It gives you a version of your face that tells a slightly better story than the one you lived. Not a lie, exactly. More of a generous edit.

Welcome to Monday's Lip Service. We've got corporate secrecy, a pigmentation breakthrough that doesn't involve vitamin C, runway tears that were very much on purpose, and a charity doing real work under the sun. Pull up a chair.

Brand Drama
Kosas is pulling its Revealer Concealer from international retailers, and employees have been told to redirect all press inquiries.
Multiple Sephora locations in the UK, Australia, and France have confirmed the Kosas Revealer Concealer is being quietly delisted, with no restock date given. Internal communications seen by two retail employees suggest a reformulation is underway, possibly tied to stability issues in warmer climates. Kosas has not issued a public statement, which is doing the opposite of calming anyone down.
Industry Moves
Shiseido just hired away one of L'Oréal's top sustainability scientists, and Paris is reportedly furious.
Dr. Émile Renard, who spent eight years leading L'Oréal's green chemistry division, has officially joined Shiseido's innovation lab in Yokohama as of this week. The move signals Shiseido's aggressive push into biodegradable packaging polymers, an area where L'Oréal held a comfortable lead. One industry source described the departure as "the kind of thing that gets discussed at board level."
Trend Alert
The "cold plunge face" is all over TikTok and dermatologists are already exhausted.
A wave of creators are dunking their faces in ice water for 30 to 90 seconds before applying makeup, claiming it shrinks pores, reduces puffiness, and "activates" serums. Board-certified dermatologists are noting that while brief cold exposure can temporarily reduce inflammation, the pore-shrinking claims are fantasy. The trend has already spawned three separate Amazon bestsellers in silicone ice molds shaped like faces.

Did You Know?
Tranexamic acid's little sister
is coming for your dark spots.

You've probably heard of tranexamic acid by now, the pigmentation darling that dermatologists have been quietly recommending for melasma for years. But there's a newer derivative, cetyl tranexamate, that's showing up in a handful of Korean and Japanese formulations this spring, and the clinical data is making even the skeptics sit up.

The Science, Briefly
Cetyl tranexamate is an ester form of tranexamic acid designed for better skin penetration and lipid compatibility. It works by inhibiting the plasminogen-to-plasmin pathway, which reduces the UV-triggered signaling that tells melanocytes to overproduce pigment. Early studies suggest it may be effective at lower concentrations than its parent molecule, with less of the irritation profile associated with other brightening agents.

The ingredient is already in products from Rohto (under their Melano CC line expansion) and a new launch from the Korean clinical brand Dr. Different, which dropped a cetyl tranexamate serum last week at 2% concentration. It's being positioned not as a replacement for tretinoin or vitamin C but as a layering agent, something you add to an existing routine when hyperpigmentation isn't responding to the usual suspects. Here's the uncomfortable truth: most people reaching for brightening serums don't actually need a stronger active. They need sunscreen compliance. Cetyl tranexamate won't do much if you're applying it in the morning and then spending two hours in direct sunlight with nothing on top. The researchers behind the most promising studies have been very clear that this ingredient's efficacy is inseparable from rigorous UV protection. It's not a magic eraser. It's a very good supporting actor.

"We're seeing promising results in post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation specifically, but I want to be honest with patients: this works inside a system, not as a shortcut around one." — Dr. Yoon-Ji Park, Dermatology Research Fellow, Seoul National University Hospital

AW26 · The Post-Cry Face at Miu Miu

Miuccia Prada's Miu Miu show in Paris last week sent models down the runway with reddened, slightly swollen undereyes, blotchy noses, and a deliberate absence of concealer or base. Lead makeup artist Pat McGrath described the look as "the face after the feeling," using a mix of stippled rose and berry pigments pressed into bare skin. Lashes were left undone. Lips were bitten-looking. The effect was less editorial sadness and more that specific rawness you see in someone who cried fifteen minutes ago and is now pretending everything is fine. What made it land, rather than read as gimmicky, was the clothes: precise, almost schoolgirlish tailoring in grey wool and stiff white cotton. The beauty wasn't decorative. It was narrative. You looked at each model and invented the backstory yourself. That tension between composure and visible emotion is something Lip Service thinks about a lot. The face doesn't have to be perfect to be powerful. Sometimes it just has to be honest.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of protection.

Farmworker Justice is a Washington, D.C.-based nonprofit founded in 1981 that advocates for migrant and seasonal farmworkers across the United States. In recent years, they've expanded their health programming to include dermatological care, partnering with volunteer dermatologists to provide free skin screenings and sun-protection supplies to workers exposed to extreme UV conditions daily.

2.4M+
farmworkers in the U.S. with limited or no access to dermatological care

In California's Central Valley last summer, a Farmworker Justice mobile clinic screened 140 workers in a single weekend. Eleven were referred for further evaluation of suspicious lesions. Three received early-stage skin cancer diagnoses that might have gone undetected for years. The organization provides broad-spectrum sunscreen, SPF-rated clothing, and bilingual education about UV exposure, but they operate on a shoestring. They need funding, volunteer dermatologists, and SPF product donations.

Healthy Tears
A woman from a village
of four thousand people
walked onto a stage
and dared the world
to underestimate her.

Susan Boyle grew up in Blackburn, a small town in West Lothian, Scotland, the kind of place where everyone knows your name and most of them had already decided what your life would look like. She was the youngest of nine. She'd never been married. She'd never been kissed. She lived with her cat, Pebbles, in the house her mother left her, and she sang in the church choir because it was the one place where nobody interrupted her.

In April 2009, she walked onto the Britain's Got Talent stage wearing a beige dress that didn't fit quite right, with hair she hadn't styled for the cameras, and announced to Simon Cowell and a live audience that she wanted to be a professional singer. She was 47. The audience laughed. Not politely. Openly. You can see it in the footage: the eye rolls, the smirks, the woman in the front row nudging her friend. They had already written the story before she opened her mouth.

Then she opened her mouth. She sang "I Dreamed a Dream" from Les Misérables, and within ten seconds the laughter turned to silence, and the silence turned to something closer to shame. The camera cut to Cowell, whose raised eyebrow dropped into something unreadable. Amanda Holden's eyes filled. The audience was on its feet before the second chorus.

The clip was viewed over 100 million times within a month, making it one of the most-watched videos in internet history at the time. Her debut album became the bestselling first album in UK chart history. But here's the part that stays with me: years later, in an interview, she was asked what she remembered most about that night. She didn't mention the standing ovation or the record deals. She said, "I remember thinking, before I sang, that they were right about me. And then I thought, well, let's find out." That pause between believing what the world has told you and deciding to test it anyway. That's the whole thing.

The 30 seconds that humbled an entire room.
