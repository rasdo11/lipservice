Opening Statement
I reapplied lipstick in a CVS parking lot.
It was the most honest thing I did all week.

There's a specific kind of fluorescent lighting in a CVS parking lot at 6:47 PM on a Monday that will tell you everything you need to know about your skin. It is not kind. It is not filtered. It is the lighting equivalent of a friend who says "you look tired" and means it medically. I was sitting in my car, engine off, staring at myself in the visor mirror under that merciless glow, and I reached for the one thing in my bag that felt like a decision I could actually make: a half-worn tube of something between brick and berry.

Here's the thing nobody tells you about beauty routines — the real ones, not the ones you perform for a camera or describe to a dermatologist. They happen in moments of mild desperation. They happen when you're between things. You don't apply a serum because you feel radiant. You apply it because you feel like a person who should probably try harder, and this is the version of trying harder that doesn't require you to leave your car.

I think about this a lot: the gap between how the beauty industry talks about self-care — all linen and candles and golden hour — and how it actually happens. In a parking lot. Under bad lights. With a product you bought because the packaging reminded you of someone you used to be. That gap is where the interesting stuff lives. That's where I want this newsletter to exist.

So welcome to today's Lip Service. We've got brand strategy disguised as minimalism, an ingredient that sounds boring until you understand what it's doing to your moisture barrier, a runway moment that reminded me why makeup is an art form, and an organization that proves beauty can be a bridge to something much bigger. Let's get into it.

Brand Drama
Glossier's New Visual Identity Is Giving Corporate Retreat Energy
Glossier quietly rolled out updated packaging across its core line this week — new typeface, pared-back color palette, the logo shrunk to near-illegibility. It's the visual equivalent of someone whispering to seem more important. Internal sources say the rebrand coincides with a push into Nordstrom doors across the Midwest, which tells you everything about who they're designing for now.
Industry Moves

Maren Kjeldsen, who spent six years leading formulation at Aesop (through and after the Natura acquisition), has joined L'Oréal's Luxe division as SVP of Innovation. The hire signals L'Oréal's continued bet on texture-forward, sensorial products over clinical positioning. Kjeldsen reportedly turned down a role at Estée Lauder last fall.
Runway Intel
Prada's Resort Collection Had Exactly One Print, and It Was Devastating
Miuccia showed Resort 2027 in Milan this weekend with an almost monastic restraint — columns of black, navy, concrete gray — punctuated by a single floral that appeared on exactly three looks. It was a cabbage rose rendered in what looked like faded Polaroid tones. The fashion crowd is calling it "grief florals." I'm calling it the most interesting thing she's done since 2019.

Did You Know?
Polyglutamic acid is
making hyaluronic acid nervous.

You've probably seen it creeping up ingredient lists — PGA, or polyglutamic acid, a hydrating molecule derived from fermented soybeans that holds four times more moisture than hyaluronic acid. It's been a staple in Japanese skincare for decades, but Western brands just discovered it like Columbus discovered a continent that was already occupied.

The Science, Briefly
PGA works by forming a thin moisture-retaining film on the skin's surface while simultaneously inhibiting hyaluronidase, the enzyme that breaks down your skin's natural hyaluronic acid. In other words, it doesn't just add hydration — it slows the rate at which your skin loses what it already has. Think of it less as a deposit and more as a lock on the vault.

The Ordinary launched a 10% PGA serum last month that sold out in eleven hours. Tatcha has been quietly using it in its Dewy Skin Cream since 2024. Korean brands like Isntree and Cosrx have had standalone PGA products for years, which is worth remembering the next time a legacy brand acts like they invented water. The ingredient works particularly well for anyone whose skin drinks up hyaluronic acid and still feels tight by noon — which, if you live in a climate-controlled office or a city with hard water, is most of you. Here's the uncomfortable truth: polyglutamic acid isn't a miracle, and no single humectant is going to fix a broken moisture barrier. If your skin is chronically dehydrated, you likely have a ceramide or fatty acid issue that no amount of hydration layering will solve. PGA is excellent at what it does. What it does is one part of a much bigger picture. The brands selling it as a hyaluronic acid replacement are being reductive because reductive sells.

"Polyglutamic acid is a brilliant complement to existing hydration strategies, but the narrative that it replaces hyaluronic acid entirely is marketing, not science. They occupy different molecular weight categories and work synergistically." — Dr. Shereene Idriss, Board-Certified Dermatologist, Idriss Dermatology, NYC

Did You See?
At Loewe AW26,
someone finally made a brown lip
look like it belonged in a museum.
AW26 · The Loewe Umber Lip

Jonathan Anderson's AW26 show in Paris last week was, as usual, an exercise in making you feel slightly unmoored — the kind of collection that looks simple until you realize every proportion is a centimeter off from what your brain expected. But the beauty, led by makeup artist Lucia Pica, was the thing that stopped me. A deep umber lip — matte, precise, the color of an old leather-bound book — paired with absolutely bare skin. No concealer. No blush. Visible texture. The models looked like Renaissance portraits that had learned to walk. What made it extraordinary wasn't the color itself — brown lips cycle in and out like a rumor — but the execution. Pica reportedly mixed three different lip products on a palette backstage, custom-blending for each model's skin tone so the brown read warm, not dead. In an era of one-shade-fits-all viral products, the idea that a lip color should be individually calibrated felt almost radical. It's the kind of beauty moment that reminds you why Lip Service exists: not to tell you what to buy, but to help you see what's actually happening.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of rebuilding.

Dress for Success has been helping women entering or re-entering the workforce since 1997, when Nancy Lublin founded it with a $5,000 inheritance from her great-grandfather. What started as a single location in New York providing interview-appropriate clothing has become a global network operating in over 145 cities across 25 countries.

1.3M+
women served with professional attire and career development since founding

Last year, a woman named Denise in Columbus, Ohio, walked into a Dress for Success affiliate the day before her first job interview in nine years. She'd left an abusive marriage, was living in transitional housing, and owned one pair of jeans. She left with a blazer, a pair of heels that fit, and — according to the case worker who shared the story — the first time she'd looked in a mirror and smiled in months. She got the job. The organization runs on donated professional clothing, volunteer stylists, and funding that is perpetually insufficient for the demand. They need both clothes and cash, and they need them now.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in the palm of one hand.
Twenty-one minutes
that refused to end on time.

By July of 1985, Freddie Mercury had been famous for over a decade, which is long enough for the world to think it knows you and short enough that you're still furious about being misunderstood. Queen was in an odd position — massive globally, slightly unfashionable critically, coming off a soundtrack album and a record that had divided fans. The music press had started writing about them the way you write about a building you expect to be demolished. Respectfully. Past tense.

Then came Wembley. Live Aid. A concert organized to raise money for Ethiopian famine relief, featuring virtually every major act on the planet, broadcast to an estimated 1.9 billion people. Each band got a slot. Queen got twenty minutes. They were told, explicitly, to keep it tight. There were logistics. There was a schedule. Bob Geldof had a plan.

Freddie Mercury did not care about Bob Geldof's plan. He walked onto that stage in a white tank top and grabbed seventy-two thousand human beings by the throat with nothing but his voice and the audacity to believe he could. The call-and-response — that impossible, wordless vocal volley between one man and an ocean of people — is the moment music historians point to when they try to explain what stage presence actually means. He ran through "Bohemian Rhapsody," "Radio Ga Ga," "We Are the Champions," and he did not cut a single second. He played past his time. He played like someone who understood, on some cellular level, that this was the performance against which all others would be measured. He was right.

He gave twenty-one minutes. He was asked for less. Every second over the limit was a choice — a refusal to make himself smaller for someone else's convenience. David Bowie, who performed that same day, later said it simply: "He was the one artist who could hold the whole stadium in his hand." Not the building. Not the crowd. The hand. As if all that noise and need and human chaos could be held by a single person who simply decided not to let go.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
