The Injection Report
Polycaprolactone Fillers Are Waking Up Your Immune System, and Not in a Good Way

What's going on: A study published March 29 in the Journal of Dermatological Science by researchers at Seoul National University found that polycaprolactone-based fillers (think Ellansé) trigger a sustained Th2 inflammatory response lasting up to 14 months post-injection. The team biopsied tissue from 38 patients and found elevated IL-4 and IL-13 at every follow-up. What it means: PCL fillers are marketed as "biostimulators" that build your own collagen. That part is true. But this data suggests the mechanism involves chronic immune activation, not a clean handoff. If you've ever had unexplained swelling months after a biostimulator session, this may be why. What's next: Ask your injector whether they've reviewed the Seoul National data before your next biostimulator appointment. If they haven't heard of it, that tells you something too.

The question to bring to your next consult: "What's the inflammatory profile of the biostimulator you use, and how long does immune activation typically persist?"

Put It In Your Mouth
The Selenium Study That Has GI Docs Group-Texting

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial out of the University of Porto, published March 30 in Gut, found that 200mcg of supplemental selenium daily reduced inflammatory markers in patients with mild intestinal permeability by 34% over 12 weeks. The control group saw no change. What that means: Selenium has always been the quiet mineral in the thyroid conversation, but this is the first RCT linking it directly to gut barrier function. If your skin has been reactive and your gut panel keeps coming back borderline, this is worth flagging with your doctor. This week's cheat meal: A seared salmon collar from your nearest Japanese restaurant. Lip Lab
Rare Beauty Quietly Reformulated Its Soft Pinch Tinted Lip Oil, and the New Version Is a Different Product

What's going on: Rare Beauty pushed a reformulated Soft Pinch Tinted Lip Oil to Sephora shelves this week with no announcement. Reddit's r/Sephora caught it March 28 when batch codes didn't match and the texture was visibly thinner. Why it matters: The old formula used a jojoba-and-squalane base. The new one swaps in a lighter meadowfoam seed oil blend and adds tocopheryl acetate. It's less cushiony, more glossy, and dries down faster. The verdict: If you loved the balmy slip of the original, stock up on old-batch inventory while it exists. The new version is fine. It's just a lip gloss now, not a lip treatment.

Check the batch code printed near the crimp. If it starts with "25L," that's the old formula. "26A" is the reformulation.
