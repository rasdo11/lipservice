Opening Statement
I bought the same moisturizer three times.
It was never the same twice.

Last Tuesday I opened a jar of something I've repurchased for four years — same pink lid, same font, same little speech about skin barriers on the back — and it smelled different. Not off. Not expired. Just… different, in that corporate way that means someone in a boardroom decided "streamlining" the supply chain was more important than telling me they changed the formula. I put it on anyway. My face felt like it was wearing a raincoat.

This is the thing about loyalty in beauty: it's a one-way street with excellent packaging. We build entire routines around a product, reorganize our bathroom shelves, swear by it in group chats with the fervor of a convert — and the brand reserves the right to quietly swap the second ingredient for something cheaper and never mention it. They don't owe us a press release. But they could at least owe us a whisper.

I think about this a lot — how the relationship between a woman and her products is, at its core, one of trust. Not trust that it'll make us look twenty-two. Trust that it is what it says it is. That the thing that worked yesterday will work today. That when we hand over $48 and a little sliver of hope, we're not being managed.

Anyway. Welcome to today's Lip Service, where we say the quiet parts at full volume and still manage to look great doing it.

Did You Know?
Bakuchiol had a good run
on vibes alone.

For five years, bakuchiol has been retinol's gentler, plant-based understudy — the one you reach for when your skin can't handle the real thing. But a new meta-analysis published last month in the Journal of Dermatological Science is complicating the narrative, and it's worth understanding what the data actually says before you build a whole routine around it.

The Science, Briefly
The study pooled results from 11 randomized controlled trials and found that while bakuchiol shows meaningful antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity, its effect on collagen synthesis and fine-line reduction was statistically significant in only 3 of 11 trials — and those 3 used concentrations far higher than what's in most consumer products.

This doesn't mean bakuchiol is useless. It means the "retinol alternative" framing was always a little generous. Most serums contain bakuchiol at 0.5–1%, but the trials showing wrinkle improvement used 2–4% — concentrations that few over-the-counter products disclose or deliver. The ingredient is genuinely soothing and pairs well with other actives, but calling it a retinol swap is like calling a brisk walk a marathon. Same direction, different magnitude. Brands like Herbivore, Biossance, and Ole Henriksen have built hero products around bakuchiol, and none of them are likely to update their marketing anytime soon. The uncomfortable truth is that the clean beauty movement needed a retinol replacement badly enough that the industry rounded up on the evidence. If your skin likes it, keep using it. Just maybe stop expecting it to do retinol's job.

"Bakuchiol is a perfectly respectable ingredient with anti-inflammatory properties. But the clinical evidence for anti-aging equivalence to retinol simply isn't there yet at consumer-available concentrations." — Dr. Anjali Mehta, Dermatologist, NYU Langone Health

Did You See?
Haider Ackermann sent models out
with nothing on their lips.
It was the loudest moment of the season.
AW26 · Haider Ackermann's bare-mouth beauty moment

At Haider Ackermann's AW26 show in Paris last week, the beauty direction was so restrained it bordered on confrontational. Lead makeup artist Lucia Pieroni used exactly one product on the mouth — a clear balm, applied with fingers — while the eyes were left completely bare. No concealer to even out. No mascara to open up. Just skin, in its actual state, under stadium lighting that hid nothing. Against Ackermann's razor-cut suiting in oxblood and charcoal, the effect was almost unsettling: these women looked powerful and completely undone at the same time. What makes this significant isn't that bare skin on a runway is new — it isn't — but that Ackermann paired it with clothing so sharp and architectural that the face became the only soft thing in the frame. It was a study in contrast, and it felt like a direct response to the maximalist glam-bot beauty we've been swimming in for two seasons. Fashion has a way of telling you what it's tired of before anyone else admits it. Lip Service was already suspicious. Now we're paying attention.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of armor.

Dress for Success is a global nonprofit founded in 1997 by Nancy Lublin, originally out of a $5,000 inheritance from her great-grandfather. The organization provides professional clothing, styling support, and career development to women transitioning out of poverty, homelessness, and domestic violence — suiting them up for job interviews and the lives that come after.

1.2M+
women served across 150 cities in 30 countries since founding

Last year, a Dress for Success affiliate in Detroit helped a woman named Tamika — a single mother of three who'd been living in a shelter for nine months — prepare for a nursing assistant interview. They gave her a blazer, slacks, and a pair of low heels, but what Tamika later told a volunteer mattered more than the clothes: "I looked in the mirror and I recognized myself again." She got the job. She moved her family into an apartment six weeks later. Dress for Success runs on donated professional clothing, corporate partnerships, and individual giving — and they always need more of all three.

Healthy Tears
Freddie Mercury walked onto
the biggest stage on Earth
and made 72,000 people
his backup singers.

By the summer of 1985, Freddie Mercury was already living inside the mythology of himself — four octaves, a mustache that launched a thousand Halloween costumes, a stage presence so expansive it made arenas feel like living rooms. Queen had been famous for over a decade. They'd also been quietly written off by the British press, treated as a legacy act coasting on old hits, slightly embarrassing in the way that only a band too theatrical for punk and too sincere for new wave could be.

Bob Geldof gave them a slot at Live Aid on July 13th. Twenty minutes. Maybe less. The logistics alone were nightmarish — 72,000 people at Wembley Stadium, a billion more watching on television across 150 countries, and a rotating lineup of the biggest acts alive, each getting barely enough time to prove they still mattered. Queen's set was supposed to be short, tight, functional. Someone told Freddie to keep it contained. This was a charity concert, not a coronation.

He did not keep it contained. From the first hammered piano note of "Bohemian Rhapsody" — truncated, rearranged, almost violent in its compression — Mercury owned the stadium the way weather owns a coastline. He ran through "Radio Ga Ga," "Hammer to Fall," "Crazy Little Thing Called Love," and "We Are the Champions" in twenty-one minutes, but what people remember isn't the setlist. It's the call-and-response. That moment where he stood at the microphone and simply sang vowels — "ay-oh" — and 72,000 people sang them back, perfectly, like he'd rehearsed them. He hadn't. He just understood, on some cellular level, that a crowd is an instrument if you know where to put your hands.

Music historians still call it the greatest live performance ever staged. Not because of the notes, but because of the nerve. He was a queer immigrant frontman in a country that wasn't always kind about either of those things, performing at a moment when his band was supposed to be finished, and he turned it into the most commanding twenty-one minutes in rock history. He said later, with the understatement of someone who knew exactly what he'd done: "We're a very big band, darling. We just needed reminding."

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
