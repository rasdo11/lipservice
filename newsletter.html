The Injection Report
Allergan Gutted Allē and Your Wallet Feels It

What's going on: Allergan Aesthetics rolled out a major overhaul to its Allē loyalty program this week, slashing point values, capping quarterly earnings, and eliminating several provider-tier bonuses that offices relied on to keep patient pricing competitive. The changes took effect June 23 with almost no advance warning to practices. Multiple injectors confirmed on professional forums that their per-unit rebate structure was cut by roughly 30–40%.

What it means: For years, Allē points were the invisible subsidy keeping your Botox and Juvéderm tabs manageable. Injectors passed savings through; patients stayed loyal. Without those margins, expect modest price bumps at well-run practices and significant ones at medspas that were already thin. This also opens a real window for Daxxify, Xeomin, and the RHA fillers to negotiate harder on switching incentives.

What's next: Ask your injector directly how this affects their pricing before your next appointment. If they're vague, that's your answer.

If your practice hasn't sent a communication about Allē changes yet, call before you book. Pricing may have already shifted and the front desk might not volunteer it.

Put It In Your Mouth
High-Dose Omega-3s Just Got a Collagen Co-Sign

What's going on: A randomized controlled trial published this week in the *Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology* found that 12 weeks of high-dose EPA/DHA supplementation (2.4g/day) significantly improved skin elasticity and dermal collagen density measured by ultrasound versus placebo. The cohort was women 35–55 — our people — and the effect size was comparable to some topical retinoid studies.

What that means: This isn't "fish oil is good for you" boilerplate. The dose matters. Most grocery-store capsules deliver 500–600mg combined EPA/DHA. You'd need four of those to hit the study threshold, or one proper high-potency formula like Nordic Naturals Ultimate Omega or Carlson's Elite.

This week's cheat meal: Uni toast at the bar at any restaurant that has uni right now. It's sweet, it's briny, it's peak season, and it's essentially pure omega-3 delivery on carbs.

Lip Lab
The Lip Oil Correction Is Here

What's going on: Glossier quietly reformulated Lip Gloss this week — rebranding it as a "lip oil" with a thinner viscosity and added jojoba and rosehip — joining the approximately four hundred lip oils that launched this year. Meanwhile, dermatologists on social media are finally pushing back on the category, noting that many formulas rely on occlusive shine over actual barrier repair.

Why it matters: The issue isn't oil itself — it's that most lip oils use hydrogenated polyisobutene as the primary base (basically a silicone-adjacent slip agent) with trace amounts of actual plant lipids. They feel luxurious. They're not treating anything. If your lips are chronically dry, a ceramide or lanolin-based balm underneath still does more.

The verdict: Glossier's reformulation is genuinely nicer than the old gloss — less sticky, wears well, the rosehip addition isn't just label decoration. But it's a cosmetic, not a treatment. Layer it over something that actually works.

If a lip oil lists hydrogenated polyisobutene in positions 1–3 and the 'nourishing oils' are below fragrance, it's a gloss in a dropper. Read the back of the tube.
