Opening Statement
I threw out my entire skincare routine.
It took me forty-five minutes to admit why.

Last Tuesday I stood in front of my bathroom mirror at 11 p.m. and counted seventeen products on my shelf that I'd bought because someone with luminous pores told me to. Seventeen. A retinol I was afraid of, a peptide cream that smelled like a hospital, a fermented essence I'd been applying wrong for months. I picked up each one, read the back like it was a prescription I'd never filled, and put fourteen of them in a bag.

The three I kept were boring. A cleanser that cost eleven dollars. A moisturizer my mother also uses. Sunscreen. I felt like I'd just left a religion — lighter and slightly embarrassed about how long I'd stayed.

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: the twelve-step routine isn't really about skin. It's about the feeling that if you just add one more layer, one more active, one more thirty-dollar dropper bottle, you'll arrive at some version of yourself that doesn't need fixing. But the fixing is the engine. The industry doesn't run on your confidence. It runs on the three-inch gap between your face and your reflection.

Which doesn't mean I'm done buying products — please, I'm not a monster. It means I'm paying attention to why. Welcome to today's Lip Service. Let's get into it.

Brand Drama
Rare Beauty files new trademark opposition — and the target is surprising
Selena Gomez's Rare Beauty has filed a trademark opposition against a small Swiss skincare brand called Rære Laboratoire, which launched its U.S. DTC site in January. The filing, logged with the USPTO last Thursday, argues likelihood of consumer confusion in the color cosmetics category. Rære's founder, a former La Prairie chemist, has reportedly retained the same IP counsel who defended Glossier in 2021.
Industry Moves
Estée Lauder's new global president comes from outside beauty entirely
The Estée Lauder Companies announced Friday that its new Global Brand President will be Priya Anand, most recently COO of a mid-cap athletic wear company. She has zero years in beauty. Internally, the hire is being read as a signal that ELC is prioritizing operational turnaround over creative vision — a bet that the product pipeline is fine and the logistics are the problem. Her start date is April 1.
Trend Alert
The 'cold girl' blush placement is migrating — downward
If you've noticed blush creeping below the cheekbone and onto the jawline in recent editorials, you're not imagining it. MUAs at both the Chanel and Miu Miu AW26 shows applied cream blush along the mandible, creating a flushed-from-the-neck-up effect that reads less doll, more caught-in-the-rain. Pat McGrath's team reportedly called it 'jawline flush' backstage. Give it six weeks before it's a TikTok filter.

Did You Know?
Fermented rice water is
not new. You are.

Your algorithm has probably served you a dewy-skinned woman straining milky water through a sieve this month. The ingredient is fermented rice water — technically the starchy liquid left after soaking or boiling rice, then left to ferment for 24 to 48 hours. Japanese women in the Heian period used it on their hair. Korean hanbang skincare has included it for centuries. What's changed is that three prestige brands launched rice ferment serums in the last ninety days, and now it has a marketing budget.

The Science, Briefly
During fermentation, starches in rice water break down into pitera-adjacent saccharomyces metabolites — including amino acids, vitamins B and E, and organic acids — that have been shown in small studies to support the skin's moisture barrier and improve texture by gently encouraging cell turnover.

SK-II built an empire on a related concept decades ago with its proprietary pitera, derived from yeast fermentation during sake brewing. The new wave — led by brands like Beauty of Joseon, Haruharu Wonder, and a forthcoming Tatcha limited release — is marketing rice ferment as a gentler, less sensitizing alternative to chemical exfoliants. The pitch is essentially: glow without the purge. The uncomfortable truth is that most of the clinical data on fermented rice water is thin — small sample sizes, short durations, often funded by the brands themselves. That doesn't mean it doesn't work. Fermented ingredients do contain genuinely beneficial metabolites. It means the gap between the marketing claims and the evidence is wider than anyone with a serum to sell wants to admit. If you try it, give it eight weeks and pay attention to your own skin, not someone else's ring light.

"Fermentation creates bioavailable compounds that raw ingredients don't offer — but the concentration matters enormously, and most consumer products won't disclose theirs." — Dr. Yoon-Soo Cindy Bae, Dermatologist, Laser & Skin Surgery Center of New York

Did You See?
Dries Van Noten's last bow.
The makeup said everything
the designer wouldn't.
AW26 · Dries Van Noten's Final Color Study

At the Dries Van Noten AW26 show in Paris last week — the final collection under Van Noten's own creative direction before his retirement takes full effect — lead makeup artist Peter Philips painted models in what can only be described as controlled emotion. Bare skin, almost no base, but a single saturated wash of color on each face: cobalt lids on one model, deep burgundy lips on another, a stroke of marigold across the brow bone. No two faces matched. Each one looked like a painting he'd never make again. The clothes were muted — tobacco linens, charcoal wool, quiet draping — which made the faces louder. Several editors in the front row reportedly cried, not because it was sad, but because it was so clearly a goodbye delivered in pigment instead of words. Philips told a reporter backstage that Van Noten's only brief was: "Make them look like they feel something." That's also, incidentally, everything Lip Service tries to do.

Did You Help?
Beauty as an act
of restoration.

Bottomless Closet is a New York City nonprofit founded in 1999 that provides interview-appropriate clothing, grooming essentials, and career coaching to women transitioning out of shelters, the justice system, and economic crisis. In recent years, they've expanded their beauty programming — partnering with licensed cosmetologists to offer haircuts, skincare consultations, and makeup tutorials designed specifically for job interviews.

14,000+
women dressed and coached for job interviews since founding

Last spring, a woman named Darlene walked into Bottomless Closet's midtown Manhattan space after eighteen months in a domestic violence shelter. She hadn't bought herself a lipstick in two years. A volunteer stylist helped her pick a blazer. A cosmetologist showed her a five-minute face. She got the job — an administrative role at a hospital — and later told the organization that looking put-together made her feel like she was already the person they were hiring. Bottomless Closet currently needs donations of professional clothing in sizes 14 and above, travel-size skincare, and funding for their cosmetology partnerships.

Healthy Tears
Seventy-two thousand people
in the palm of one hand.
Freddie Mercury at Live Aid,
and the set he refused to cut.

By 1985, Freddie Mercury had already done everything a rock vocalist is supposed to do. He'd written anthems that strangers sang to each other in parking lots. He'd worn catsuits on stages in countries that didn't know what to do with him. He'd built a voice that could shift from operatic precision to raw, broken pleading in the space of a single bar. And yet Queen, by that July, was considered slightly past tense — a band people respected in the way you respect a monument you no longer visit.

Then Live Aid happened. Wembley Stadium. July 13th. Seventy-two thousand people baking in the London heat for a cause that was bigger than any single performance. The lineup was stacked — Bowie, Jagger, McCartney, U2, The Who. Every artist knew the stakes: you had a short set, a global broadcast, and one chance to matter. The organizers told Queen to keep it tight. Mercury heard the note.

What he did instead was walk out in a white tank top, sit at a piano, and begin with "Bohemian Rhapsody" — not the whole thing, just enough to remind you what a cathedral sounds like when one man builds it with his throat. Then he stood up. And for the next twenty-one minutes, he conducted seventy-two thousand human beings like they were a single instrument. The call-and-response — that "ay-oh" that echoed across the stadium — wasn't a gimmick. It was a man proving, in real time, that the distance between a stage and the last row of the upper deck is imaginary if you simply refuse to acknowledge it. He did not cut the set short. He played "We Are the Champions" like it was a promise he intended to keep personally.

Music historians still call it the greatest live performance ever staged. Not because of the notes — though the notes were devastating — but because of the command. The total, almost unsettling confidence that every single person in that stadium belonged to him for as long as he wanted them. Bob Geldof, who organized the whole event, later said the phone lines for donations exploded during Queen's set. Mercury never talked about it much afterward. He once said, "I'm just a musical prostitute, my dear." He was, of course, lying. He was the opposite of someone who performs for money. He was someone who performed because silence was unbearable.

The performance every musician studies. You'll understand after 30 seconds.
